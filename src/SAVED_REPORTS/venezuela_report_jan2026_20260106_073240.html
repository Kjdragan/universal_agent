Venezuela: Political Crisis and Regional Transformation

The Ouster of Nicolás Maduro and Its Implications for Latin America and Beyond
January 6, 2026

Executive Summary

In an unprecedented military operation on January 3, 2026, United States forces captured Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro and his wife, Cilia Flores, from their residence in Caracas. The operation, launched by the Trump administration, marks a dramatic escalation in U.S.-Venezuela relations and represents the first time in modern history that a sitting Latin American head of state has been forcibly removed by U.S. military action and transported to face criminal charges in U.S. courts. Maduro now faces charges including narco-terrorism conspiracy, cocaine importation conspiracy, and weapons-related offenses in the Southern District of New York.

The capture follows months of mounting U.S. pressure, including economic sanctions, a substantial military buildup in the Caribbean, and numerous strikes against Venezuelan vessels accused of drug trafficking. President Trump has stated that the United States will "run" Venezuela until a "safe, proper, and judicious transition" can take place, with particular emphasis on rebuilding Venezuela's oil infrastructure. The operation has triggered intense debate over international law, regional stability, and the future of Venezuela's 30 million citizens, 8 million of whom are in acute need of humanitarian assistance.

Timeline of Events Leading to Maduro's Capture

2024 Presidential Election

Opposition leader Edmundo González wins the presidential election by a more than 2-to-1 margin according to opposition-collected vote tallies from over 80% of electronic voting machines. The Maduro-controlled National Electoral Council declares Maduro the winner without releasing detailed vote counts, triggering widespread protests and international condemnation.

Post-Election Repression

The Maduro regime responds to protests with full force, detaining more than 2,000 people and forcing key members of opposition leader María Corina Machado's campaign to seek asylum at a diplomatic compound in Caracas for over a year.

January 2025

Trump returns to the White House and immediately pressures Maduro to accept regular deportation flights. By summer, the U.S. builds up a substantial military force in the Caribbean, placing Venezuela's government on high alert.

December 31, 2025

The U.S. imposes additional sanctions on tankers transporting Venezuelan oil, as part of an escalating pressure campaign.

January 1, 2026

Venezuela releases more prisoners amid U.S. pressure campaign, seen as a possible conciliatory gesture.

January 2, 2026

Maduro meets with a Chinese special envoy mere hours before the U.S. operation begins. Chinese diplomats likely remain in the country during the raid.

January 3, 2026 (Early Morning)

U.S. forces launch "large-scale strikes" in at least four Venezuelan states: Caracas (federal district), La Guaira, Miranda, and Aragua. The U.S. military appears to overrun Venezuela's air defenses, with special operations forces conducting a raid on Maduro's residence inside Fuerte Tiuna, the principal military base of the Venezuelan army.

January 3, 2026 (Morning)

President Trump announces the successful capture of Maduro and Flores on social media, stating they have been moved to the USS Iwo Jima and will face criminal charges in the United States. Venezuelan Vice President Delcy Rodríguez appears on state television saying the government does not know the whereabouts of the president and first lady.

January 5, 2026

Maduro enters a plea of not guilty in his first court appearance in New York, maintaining his defiance despite facing multiple federal charges.

Current Situation on the Ground

The immediate aftermath of Maduro's capture has created a complex and fluid situation within Venezuela. Venezuelan Vice President Delcy Rodríguez has attempted to assume leadership, convening a "National Council in Defense of the Republic" made up of ruling party heavyweights. However, experts suggest she does not appear to have the backing of all factions within the party, and Chavismo no longer enjoys the widespread popular support it had two decades ago.

Venezuelan security forces are patrolling the streets as opposition forces mobilize. Opposition leader María Corina Machado has called the moment the "hour of freedom" and demanded that González, whom the opposition says won the 2024 vote, "immediately assume his constitutional mandate" as president. González has written on social media that "these are decisive hours" and that the opposition is "ready for the great operation of the reconstruction of our nation."

"We are going to run the country until such time as we can do a safe, proper, and judicious transition."
— President Donald Trump, January 3, 2026

The Venezuelan military appears fractured but not monolithic. While the regime's usually strong communication apparatus failed catastrophically in the first twelve hours following the operation, and military command-and-control chains were clearly disrupted, the armed forces remain a powerful actor that will be critical to any transition. U.S. forces are expected to provide security around critical infrastructure, particularly oil facilities, but broader public security remains a pressing challenge in a country plagued by gangs, paramilitary groups, guerrillas, and transnational cartels.

Hundreds of political prisoners remain locked up, and their fate is of top importance to the opposition and international community. The nearly 8 million Venezuelans who have fled the country—constituting one of the largest forced displacements in the world without a conventional war—are watching anxiously from exile, with many hoping to return but uncertain about what the future holds.

International Reactions and Implications

The U.S. operation has provoked sharply divided reactions across the international community, reflecting deep disagreements about international law, sovereignty, and the appropriate response to Maduro's authoritarian rule.

United Nations

Secretary-General António Guterres is "deeply alarmed" and warned the strikes could "constitute a dangerous precedent." Venezuelan Foreign Minister Yvan Gil demanded an emergency meeting of the UN Security Council.

European Union

EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas called for restraint and respect for international law, while noting the bloc has repeatedly stated Maduro lacks legitimacy and has defended a peaceful transition.

France

President Emmanuel Macron said the Venezuelan people "could only rejoice" at Maduro's removal but noted the operation "contravenes the principle of non-use of force" under international law. He backed González leading a peaceful transition.

United Kingdom

Prime Minister Keir Starmer said all countries should "uphold international law" but noted Britain would "shed no tears" about Maduro's demise. The UK was not involved in the operation.

Italy

Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, a Trump ally, defended the action as legitimate "defense" against state entities that fuel drug trafficking, while saying external military force should not be used for regime change.

Brazil

President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva condemned the attacks as a "serious affront" to sovereignty that cross an "unacceptable line" and threaten the region as a "zone of peace."

Mexico

The government strongly condemned the military action, saying it "seriously jeopardizes regional stability."

Argentina

President Javier Milei, a close Trump ally, celebrated with his slogan "Long live freedom, dammit!"

Colombia

President Gustavo Petro convened a national security meeting before dawn and sent security forces to the border in preparation for a potential "massive influx of refugees."

South Africa

Said unlawful unilateral force "undermines the stability of the international order and the principle of equality among nations."

Notably, Ukraine—which depends on U.S. support in its war against Russia—did not address the legality of a large country using military force against a much smaller one, instead focusing on Maduro's lack of legitimacy and backing democracy and human rights.

"The bombings in Venezuelan territory and the capture of its president cross an unacceptable line [and threaten] the preservation of the region as a zone of peace."
— Brazilian President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva

Legal and Constitutional Questions

Legal scholars and international experts overwhelmingly view the operation as a violation of international law. The UN Charter forbids the use of force against a state's "territorial integrity or political independence," with exceptions only for self-defense and Security Council authorizations. None of these conditions appear to have been met. The attacks appear to fall under Article 3(a) of the UN General Assembly's definition of the crime of aggression.

In the United States, constitutional questions have been raised about congressional authorization. The Trump administration maintains that the president has constitutional authority to use military force without congressional authorization for actions that support U.S. interests and are not expected to result in "prolonged and substantial military engagements." However, Democratic lawmakers argue they were misled during briefings and that the operation bypasses Congress' authority over military force.

As a sitting head of state, international law affords Maduro full personal immunity under domestic courts—including in the United States. However, since 2019, the United States and other countries have not recognized Maduro as head of state in response to widespread election fraud. The French Cour de Cassation has ruled that such immunity should apply regardless of whether a state recognizes a leader's authority—precisely to prevent politically motivated arrests.

What's Next for Venezuela

Venezuela now faces a precarious transition filled with both opportunity and grave risks. The Brookings Institution identifies three critical elements for a durable democratic transition: political legitimacy, economic stabilization, and military realignment.

Key Challenges Ahead

  • Political Vacuum: With 99.9% of the original Chavista regime still in power, questions remain about who will lead and whether genuine democratic change is possible.
  • Military Fracture: Venezuela's armed forces are fractured, criminalized, and complicit—but not monolithic. Managing their integration or dismantling is critical.
  • Economic Collapse: Venezuela's economy contracted roughly 80 percent over the last decade, the largest peacetime collapse in modern history.
  • Humanitarian Crisis: Nearly 8 million Venezuelans are in acute need of humanitarian assistance.
  • Armed Groups: Criminalized military units, narco-linked factions, and transnational cartels could wage asymmetric warfare.
  • International Pressure: China, Russia, and Iran may attempt to maintain influence or exploit the instability.

Scenarios for the Future

Analysts have outlined several possible scenarios:

Best Case: Maduro's arrest catalyzes elite defection. Regime underlings, faced with legal exposure, sanctions, and loss of patronage, negotiate guarantees for safe passage, limited amnesty, or third-country exile in exchange for transferring authority to the legitimately elected opposition led by González and Machado. Such a negotiated handover would avert mass violence, stabilize institutions, and open a narrow but viable path toward economic recovery and international reintegration.

Middle Case: The United States has been working secretly with elements of the Venezuelan government who will take over, potentially maintaining much of the Chavista structure without Maduro. This could lead to a hybrid regime with limited democratic reforms.

Worst Case: If regime remnants reject negotiation and fragment, Venezuela could descend into a protracted guerrilla conflict. Armed colectivos, criminalized military units, and narco-linked factions could wage asymmetric warfare, turning parts of the country into contested zones and prolonging civilian suffering long after the regime's formal collapse.

U.S. Involvement and the "Don-roe Doctrine"

President Trump has stated that the United States will "run" Venezuela until the situation is stabilized, with particular emphasis on rebuilding the country's oil infrastructure. This approach, dubbed the "Don-roe Doctrine" (a play on the Monroe Doctrine), represents a dramatic expansion of U.S. military and political involvement in Latin America. However, critical questions remain unanswered: What exactly does "running" the country entail? How long will it last? Who will pay for it? And what happens if remaining regime officials don't cooperate with U.S. objectives?

Secretary of State Marco Rubio has tried to walk back Trump's statements, saying the United States would have no direct governing role and would instead structure Venezuela's oil and counternarcotics policies through sanctions and blockades. This contradiction between Trump's and Rubio's statements reveals deep disagreements within the administration about the path forward.

Economic and Humanitarian Considerations

The Economic Catastrophe

Venezuela's economic collapse under Maduro is unprecedented in modern history for a country not at war. According to the Brookings Institution, Venezuela's economy contracted roughly 80 percent over the last decade. Between 2012 and 2020 alone, the economy shrank 71 percent, while inflation topped 130,000 percent.

The oil industry, which once produced 3.2 million barrels per day in 2000, has dwindled to about 1 million barrels per day. The state-owned oil company, Petróleos de Venezuela Sociedad Anónima (PDVSA), was once well-run but suffered catastrophic decline after former President Hugo Chávez fired thousands of employees in 2002-03 following a strike. Twenty years of lack of investment and mismanagement have left the industry in ruins.

Trump has suggested that U.S. oil companies will "go in, spend billions of dollars, fix the badly broken infrastructure" and that oil revenues will fund the ongoing U.S. presence. However, experts are deeply skeptical of this outlook. Venezuela's vast oil reserves consist primarily of very heavy, sour crude that is expensive and complex to produce. Restoring production to its peak would require years and billions of dollars in investment—but the current environment of political instability does not support the needed level of investment.

"We've heard this story before, when oil revenues were intended to fund the reconstruction of Iraq. It wasn't true then, and it won't be true now."
— Brookings Institution energy expert

The Humanitarian Crisis

The human cost of Venezuela's collapse is staggering. More than 7.7 million Venezuelans have migrated, representing 20 percent of the country's population. Entire families starved and began migrating on foot to neighboring countries. Those who remained lined up for hours to buy rice, beans, and other basics. Some fought on the streets over flour.

Nearly 8 million Venezuelans are now in acute need of humanitarian assistance. Basic infrastructure—electricity grids, water systems, transportation networks—is severely deteriorated. A credible stabilization effort will require an IMF-anchored package of over $50 billion over 18–24 months, combining balance-of-payments support, fiscal financing and debt restructuring, as well as parallel World Bank and Inter-American Development Bank programs.

For the nearly 350,000 Venezuelans in the United States with Temporary Protected Status, the future is uncertain. Their protected status could end permanently, and they face the prospect of being forced back to a country now undergoing military intervention and potential civil conflict. This has created profound anxiety and confusion among Venezuelan immigrant communities in the U.S.

Human Rights and Justice

Maduro's regime was marked by systematic human rights abuses. The regime engaged in violent repression against Venezuelan citizens, including arbitrary detentions, torture, sexual violence, and deaths in custody. The International Criminal Court has been investigating the Venezuelan government for these crimes since 2021, though it has yet to issue a single indictment.

Hundreds of political prisoners remain locked up, and their fate is a top priority for the opposition and human rights organizations. Any legitimate transition will require addressing these human rights violations while balancing the need for stability. The Brookings Institution recommends "conditional off-ramps—amnesty and reintegration for mid-level officers not implicated in atrocities—paired with targeted prosecutions for grave crimes and the dismantling of irregular armed groups."

Geopolitical Implications

The operation in Venezuela has significant implications for great power competition and regional geopolitics. The Trump administration's 2025 National Security Strategy declared the United States' intent to "restore American preeminence" in the Western Hemisphere through what it calls the "Trump Corollary" to the Monroe Doctrine. This doctrine states that the administration "will deny non-Hemispheric competitors the ability to position forces or other threatening capabilities, or to own or control strategically vital assets, in our Hemisphere."

That China had a special envoy meeting with Maduro hours before his capture—and likely had diplomats still in the country during the raid—sends a clear message about Beijing's role in Venezuela and the hemisphere at large. China has lent more than $60 billion to Venezuela in recent decades. The operation signals to Beijing, Moscow, and Tehran that Washington is increasingly confident operating against their interests in what it considers its sphere of influence.

From a military standpoint, the operation demonstrates the U.S. military's ability to penetrate layered Russian-derived air-defense architectures (Venezuela has S-300VM, Buk-M2, and Pantsir-S1 systems) without needing to dismantle them through prolonged campaigns. This capability will be noted in Tehran, Beijing, and Moscow, which rely on similar systems.

However, critics warn that the operation may have opened Pandora's Box. By establishing a precedent that one country can use its own legal system as a basis for attacking and capturing another country's leader, the United States may have given authoritarian regimes worldwide a justification for similar actions against leaders they don't like. Greenland, Colombia, Mexico, and Taiwan are watching nervously, while Ukrainians are likely watching in grim recognition.

Conclusion

The capture of Nicolás Maduro represents a decisive inflection point for Venezuela and for U.S. policy in Latin America. What comes next will hinge less on Washington's next move than on the calculations of the regime's remaining power brokers, military commanders, intelligence chiefs, and political enablers who are now confronted with a stark choice: negotiate an orderly exit or risk annihilation alongside a collapsing system.

For the Venezuelan people who have fought nonviolently against a highly repressive regime for over two decades, the removal of Maduro offers hope—but not certainty. The country now faces a long and difficult transition that will require international support, particularly from multilateral institutions that can provide legitimacy and resources. The United States cannot—and should not—do this alone.

A secure, democratic, and prosperous Venezuela is in the U.S. interest. But focusing narrowly on oil access or prioritizing creditor repayment over recovery would risk creating a small set of rent-seekers while keeping Venezuela's failed institutions largely intact. If the United States makes a stable, democratic Venezuela its goal, it can align its interests with those of many allies across the region and beyond.

However, the success of this audacious operation is far from guaranteed. The history of foreign-imposed regime change is sobering: democratization by force is very expensive and rarely works. Rather than making the Western Hemisphere better off, Trump's action in Venezuela may have made a relatively secure region meaningfully less stable overnight, with consequences that will unspool for years to come.

"The courage and persistence of the Venezuelan people has drawn admiration from across the globe. If the United States makes a stable, democratic Venezuela its goal, it can align its interests with those of many allies across the region and beyond."
— Brookings Institution
