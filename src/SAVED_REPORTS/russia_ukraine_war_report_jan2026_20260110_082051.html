Russia-Ukraine War: Latest Developments

Comprehensive Report on Military, Diplomatic, and Humanitarian Dimensions
January 10, 2026

Executive Summary

Nearly four years into Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, the war has entered a critical phase marked by intense diplomatic maneuvering and devastating military campaigns. This report analyzes key developments from early January 2026, including escalated missile and drone attacks on Ukrainian cities, high-stakes peace negotiations in Paris and Kyiv, unprecedented US-backed security guarantees, and mounting concerns about the conflict's global ramifications. Ukrainian forces report inflicting unsustainable casualties on Russian troops—35,000 killed in December 2025 alone—while Russia continues incremental territorial advances in eastern Ukraine. Meanwhile, a "coalition of the willing" led by the United States and European powers is working to broker a peace settlement, though fundamental disagreements over territory and security guarantees remain unresolved.

1. Recent Military Developments and Attacks

Escalating Aerial Assaults on Ukrainian Cities

Russia has dramatically intensified its long-range strike campaign against Ukraine, particularly following the US presidential election in November 2024. During 2025, Russian forces launched 54,000 long-range attack drones and 1,900 missiles against Ukrainian cities, according to the Institute for the Study of War.

Deadly Attack on Kyiv (January 2026): In the first days of January, Russia launched a massive attack on Ukraine's capital using missiles and drones. A patient was killed when a Russian drone struck a hospital in Kyiv's Obolon district. The death toll from this ongoing attack has continued to rise as recovery efforts proceed.
Kharkiv Missile Strike (January 2): Six people were killed in a Russian ballistic missile attack on Kharkiv, Ukraine's second-largest city, including a three-year-old boy. This attack prompted President Trump to express frustration with Putin, stating, "I'm not thrilled with Putin. He's killing too many people."

Civilian Infrastructure Targeted

Beyond Kyiv and Kharkiv, Russian forces have struck critical infrastructure across Ukraine:

  • Kherson Region: Russian drones struck a humanitarian assistance site in Darivska Hromada, killing one civilian and wounding two others
  • Energy Facilities: Heating and electricity plants in the northern city of Kharkiv were attacked on January 5
  • Dnipro: An American-owned oil refinery was hit, causing approximately 300 tons of oil to spill on the road

Innovative Russian Tactics and Technology

Russian forces have introduced concerning new technologies in their drone warfare:

  • MANPADS-Equipped Shahed Drones: Russia deployed Shahed drones mounted with Man-Portable Air Defence Systems designed to shoot down drone-hunting aircraft. Ukrainian experts warned pilots to avoid approaching these drones head-on.
  • Fiber-Optic Drones: Russia introduced wired fiber-optic drones impervious to electronic jamming, which contributed to the capture of the city of Siversk in Donetsk region
  • Infiltration Tactics: Russian forces shifted from large mechanized assaults to small-team infiltration tactics using groups of two soldiers to establish bridgeheads before reinforcements arrive
First Week of January 2026 Statistics:
  • 789 drones and 10 missiles launched against Ukrainian cities
  • Ukraine shot down 83% of drones and one missile
  • First two civilian deaths of the year recorded on January 5

2. Russian Casualties Reaching Unsustainable Levels

Ukrainian authorities report that Russian casualties have risen to levels that may become unsustainable under Russia's current voluntary recruitment system. According to Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy:

Recruitment vs. Attrition

Ukrainian military intelligence chief Kyrylo Budanov reported that Russia reached its quota of 403,000 recruits in 2025—averaging 33,583 per month—and planned to increase this to 34,083 per month in 2026.

According to Ukrainian commander-in-chief Oleksandr Syrskii, December 2025 marked "the first month when the unmanned systems units of the Ukrainian Defence Forces neutralised approximately as many servicemen of the occupying army as Russia conscripted in a month."

Implications: If Ukrainian casualty estimates are accurate, Russia's current recruitment model may become unsustainable, potentially forcing Moscow to deploy active reserve units or conscripts—a move that would carry significant political risk for President Putin, who has thus far kept mainstream Russian society largely unscathed by the war.

3. Diplomatic Efforts and Peace Talks

Coalition of the Willing Summit in Paris (January 6)

Representatives from 35 countries, including 27 heads of state or government, gathered in Paris for what has been termed the "coalition of the willing" summit. Key developments included:

  • French President Emmanuel Macron, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy, and British Prime Minister Keir Starmer signed a declaration of intent for deploying troops on Ukrainian territory after a ceasefire
  • Establishment of a US-Ukraine coalition coordination cell in Paris
  • Macron indicated Paris could deploy "several thousand" French troops to Ukraine post-war
  • The United States backed plans for binding security guarantees for Ukraine—the first time Washington has formally supported such a pledge

Peace Plan Status

Ukrainian negotiator Rustem Umerov stated that "most of the positions—90 percent of the peace plan—have already been agreed, work continues on the details." Zelenskyy emphasized that the remaining 10% would be difficult to finalize.

The peace framework being negotiated along three key tracks:

  1. Security guarantees for Ukraine to deter future Russian aggression
  2. Economic recovery and reconstruction
  3. Basic framework for real peace including territorial questions

Kyiv Security Talks (January 3)

Security advisers from 15 allies met in Kyiv to discuss fine points of the developing peace plan. US special envoy Steve Witkoff joined via video link. This meeting focused on:

  • Framework documents including security guarantees
  • Approaches to the peace plan
  • Sequence of further joint steps
Turkish Assessment: Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan, speaking after the Paris summit, stated: "In my view, after four years of war, we are now quite close to a lasting peace. At the very least, we see that several areas that are key to peace are being discussed in an exceptionally serious manner."

Fundamental Obstacles Remain

Key Issues Unresolved:
  • Territory: Russia, which occupies approximately 20% of Ukraine, is pushing for full control of eastern Ukraine's Donbas region. Kyiv holds a significant part of Donetsk region where attritional fighting continues.
  • Security Guarantees: Zelenskyy has repeatedly warned he will not sign a peace deal that fails to deter Russia from invading again. Ceding territory is also forbidden by Ukraine's constitution.
  • Russian Intentions: Vladimir Putin's intentions remain unclear, and there have been few signals that Russia would accept current proposals.

4. US-Backed Security Guarantees

In a significant diplomatic development, the United States for the first time backed a pledge by a broad coalition of Ukraine's allies to provide binding security guarantees to Kyiv. The "robust" guarantees would see:

  • US-led truce monitoring mechanism with European participation
  • European-led multinational force deployed to Ukrainian territory after a ceasefire
  • US commitment to "support" the European force in case of renewed Russian aggression
What's Notable: The final communique notably omitted a promise from the draft statement that Washington would commit to "support" the European-led multinational force "in case of a new attack" by Russia. This omission raises questions about the extent of US commitment.

Turkey's Role in Black Sea Security

Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan announced that Türkiye, as a NATO member with the largest fleet in the Black Sea, is prepared to assume responsibility for a naval component to monitor a potential ceasefire. Fidan stated that "significant progress has been made on this" and that Türkiye would play a major role in Ukraine's economic revitalization and development after peace.

6. International Responses and Global Security Implications

Global Warning from European Leaders

Senior European officials have warned that the war in Ukraine has made conflict in other regions, particularly the Indo-Pacific, more likely. EU defence chief Andrius Kubilius stated that new strategies and tactics—especially drone-based warfare—being tested in Ukraine could be replicated closer to home for other countries.

Australian Military Involvement: Australia deployed an E-7 Wedgetail surveillance plane to Poland to assist NATO's response to Russia's war. The aircraft undertook approximately 45 missions, including over major supply routes into Ukraine. Polish officials have welcomed greater Australian involvement, including potential participation in the massive Talisman Sabre military exercise.

China, North Korea, and Iran's Involvement

European leaders have expressed concern about China's involvement in the war, along with North Korea and Iran. Polish undersecretary of state Robert Kupiecki noted that "China and Russia cooperate strategically" and warned that international security is closely connected, with scenarios possible involving "parallel conflicts in Europe and in the Pacific Ocean."

Rise of Global South Mediation

According to an analysis in TIME magazine, small and middle powers from the Global South are increasingly stepping into mediation roles as traditional Western peacemakers have stepped back:

  • Qatar has mediated between the US and Taliban, Israel and Hamas, Ukraine and Russia (on reuniting separated children), and in conflicts in Africa and Latin America
  • Turkey hosted several rounds of Russia-Ukraine peace talks and brokered the Black Sea grain deal with the UN
  • Saudi Arabia has hosted talks to end Sudan's civil war and mediated US-Russia talks on Ukraine
Costs of Mediation: TIME noted that mediators like Qatar have paid a steep price, including sustained criticism and direct attacks on their sovereignty. An Israeli strike on Doha on September 9 demonstrated that "the cost can exceed reputational damage and escalate into a direct violation of a mediator's security and sovereignty."

8. Territorial Situation and Battlefield Dynamics

Russia's 2025 Gains

Russia made bigger advances on the battlefield in 2025 than in any other year since launching its invasion in 2022, according to an analysis by the Institute for the Study of War. Key points:

  • Russia's average daily rate of advance was 13.24 square km (5.1 sq miles) per day in 2025, compared with 9.87 sq km (3.8 sq miles) per day in 2024
  • However, territorial gains amounted to only 0.8 percent of Ukraine, consisting mainly of villages and fields
  • Russia has said it aims to capture the rest of Donetsk, Zaporizhia, and Kherson—three regions it has "annexed" on paper

Current Frontline Activity

As of early January 2026, fighting continues along multiple fronts with Russia using small infiltration tactics:

  • Kostyantynivka direction: Russian forces focusing efforts on southeastern and eastern parts of the city, using infiltration missions with small assault groups
  • Pokrovsk-Myrnohrad area: Russian forces attempting to bypass the agglomeration through Hryshyne and Rodynsne, using light vehicles and infantry in assaults
  • Hulyaipole: Russian forces conducted record number of ground assaults on January 4, with street fighting ongoing
  • Sumy Oblast: Russian forces advanced into Hrabovske but have not been able to consolidate gains to advance further
War Crimes Allegation: The Ukrainian General Staff reported that Russian forces in Pokrovsk are wearing civilian clothes in deception tactics that amount to acts of perfidy—a war crime under the Geneva Convention.
