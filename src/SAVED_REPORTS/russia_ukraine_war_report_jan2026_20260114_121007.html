Russia-Ukraine War Report

Comprehensive Analysis of Key Developments
January 7-14, 2026

Executive Summary

The period of January 7-14, 2026, marked a critical phase in the Russia-Ukraine war, characterized by intensified Russian military escalation, significant diplomatic maneuvering, and unprecedented civilian casualties. Russian forces launched the second-ever combat use of the nuclear-capable Oreshnik hypersonic ballistic missile against Ukraine, striking an aviation repair facility near Lviv. This attack drew condemnation from the United States and allies at the United Nations Security Council, with US Deputy Ambassador Tammy Bruce calling it a "dangerous and inexplicable escalation."

Simultaneously, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky announced that negotiations had reached a "new milestone," suggesting the war could potentially end in the first half of 2026. This optimism came as the "Coalition of the Willing" summit in Paris, involving representatives from 35 countries including 27 heads of state, reported progress toward a security framework that would include a U.S.-backed, European-led force deployment in post-war Ukraine.

The humanitarian situation deteriorated dramatically, with the United Nations reporting that 2025 was the deadliest year for Ukrainian civilians since the full-scale invasion began in 2022. The UN Human Rights Monitoring Mission verified 2,514 civilian deaths and 12,142 injuries in 2025—a 31% increase compared to 2024 and 70% higher than 2023. Russia's expanded use of long-range weapons exposed civilians across the entire country to heightened risk, with more than one-third of casualties occurring far from front lines.

On the ground, Russian forces continued offensive operations across multiple fronts, making incremental advances in the Borova, Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka, Hulyaipole, and western Zaporizhia directions. Ukraine's military responded with targeted strikes against Russian oil infrastructure in the Caspian Sea and military assets in occupied territories.

Military Situation Analysis

Frontline Updates

Russian forces continued offensive operations across multiple axes with incremental territorial gains. According to the Institute for the Study of War and Critical Threats assessments:

  • Kharkiv Oblast: Russian forces continued offensive operations northeast of Kharkiv City near Prylipka, Lyman, Vovchansk, Vilcha, Vovchanski Khutory, and Hrafske but did not make confirmed advances. Operations also continued southeast of Velykyi Burluk near Dvorichanske and toward Kolodyazne.
  • Kupyansk Direction: Russian forces continued offensive operations but did not make confirmed advances. Ukrainian Joint Forces Task Force Spokesperson Colonel Viktor Trehubov reported preliminary estimates of 25-90 Russian soldiers remaining in Kupyansk, with most estimates placing the number at 50-60.
  • Borova Direction: Geolocated footage indicated Russian forces recently advanced in western Ridkodub (southeast of Borova).
  • Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka Tactical Area: Geolocated footage published January 10 showed Russian forces recently advanced in eastern Kostyantynivka.
  • Pokrovsk Direction: Geolocated footage indicated Russian forces recently advanced in northern Pokrovsk in areas where previous infiltration missions occurred.
  • Hulyaipole Direction: Ukrainian military observer Kostyantyn Mashovets reported Russian forces advanced in northwestern and central Hulyaipole, with geolocated footage showing advances west of Pryluky.
  • Western Zaporizhia Oblast: Mashovets reported Russian forces advanced to the mouth of the Kinka River west of Prymorske (northwest of Orikhiv).

Oreshnik Missile Escalation

Critical Escalation: Nuclear-Capable Missile Use

Russia's deployment of the Oreshnik hypersonic ballistic missile represents a significant escalation. This was only the second time Russia has used the Oreshnik in combat since the full-scale invasion began. The missile struck an aviation repair facility in the Lviv region near the Polish border on January 11, according to the Russian Ministry of Defence.

At the emergency UN Security Council meeting, US Deputy Ambassador Tammy Bruce stated that Russia's action "risks expanding and intensifying the war" and represented an "inexplicable escalation" at a time when President Trump is trying to advance peace negotiations. Britain's acting UN ambassador, James Kariuki, called the attack "reckless," adding that "it threatens regional and international security and carries significant risk of escalation and miscalculation."

Russia claimed the missile was fired in response to Ukraine's attempt to strike one of President Putin's residences—a claim Kyiv has denied and the US has dismissed as inaccurate. Observers have rated both Oreshnik strikes as largely political and symbolic, with dummy warheads probably used, and damage caused by sonic boom and physical impact rather than live explosives.

Donbas Militarization

Al Jazeera reporting reveals that Russia is systematically transforming occupied Donbas into a "huge military base to frighten Europe." According to Pavel Lisyanskiy, head of the Kyiv-based Strategic Research and Security Institute, Moscow needs to "create a militarised springboard that is not on Russian territory."

The militarization effort includes:

  • Economic Restructuring: Moscow is pouring billions into construction projects while systematically dismantling the region's industrial base. The colossal Azovstal and Ilych steel plants in Mariupol, which once produced two-fifths of Ukraine's steel and contributed 0.6% to the nation's GDP, will not be rebuilt. Instead, resorts are planned near the Sea of Azov.
  • Resource Extraction: Donbas contains some of the world's richest coal mines (including coking coal for steelmaking) and deposits of iron ore, lithium, graphite, manganese, nickel, titanium, rare earths, and noble gas neon used in chip-making. However, extraction has been described as "barbaric," with half a dozen small water bodies disappearing in 2025 due to tectonic fissures caused by irresponsible mining.
  • Environmental Catastrophe: The remaining rivulets, lakes, and groundwater in Donbas are contaminated by chemical waste as safety standards are routinely ignored. According to Lisyanskiy, a former mine engineer, "The consequences are irreversible, it's not even a hundred years."
  • Regional Sponsorship: Russia's federal budget can no longer foot the bills, forcing some 40 Russian regions to "sponsor" construction in occupied territories. Moscow "sponsors" Donetsk and Luhansk, St. Petersburg oversees Mariupol, while the town of Shakhtarsk is "supervised" by Sakhalin island.

Casualty Statistics

According to the United Nations Human Rights Monitoring Mission in Ukraine, the total civilian casualties in 2025 reached at least 2,514 killed and 12,142 injured. The UN has verified almost 15,000 civilian deaths since Russia launched the full-scale war in February 2022, but added that "actual figures are likely higher" since many reports are "still pending corroboration" and there is no access to occupied territory and limited access to areas near front lines.

The deadliest single attack of 2025 occurred in the western Ukrainian city of Ternopil on November 19. At least 38 civilians, including eight children, were killed, and 99 people (including 17 children) were injured. According to the UN, 10 families lost two or more members each in this strike.

Danielle Bell, head of the UN monitoring mission, stated that the rise in casualties "was driven not only by intensified hostilities along the frontline, but also by the expanded use of long-range weapons, which exposed civilians across the country to heightened risk." The UN report shows clearly that nowhere in Ukraine is safe: more than one-third of civilians killed or injured were located in areas far from the front lines when Russia attacked them.

Infrastructure Damage

Russia has massively intensified its campaign of aerial attacks against Ukrainian cities, regularly terrorizing civilian populations with large-scale drone and missile assaults. In one attack during this reporting period, Moscow launched 242 drones, 14 ballistic missiles, and 22 cruise missiles against multiple cities in a single night, targeting civilian energy infrastructure and leaving hundreds of thousands of Ukrainians without power and heating.

Energy Crisis in Kyiv

Emergency crews struggled to restore heat and power to Kyiv residents more than three days after Russian strikes on energy infrastructure. Ukrainian Deputy Prime Minister Oleksii Kuleba reported that 90% of Kyiv's apartment buildings had heating restored, leaving fewer than 500 dwellings still to be connected. However, Mayor Vitali Klitschko put the number with no heating at 800, with most living on the west bank of the Dnipro River. Continued Russian shelling on January 14 caused an "even greater shortage" of electricity, with nearly 500 high-rise buildings still without heat.

In the Odesa region, Russian attacks on energy infrastructure caused blackouts affecting at least 33,500 families, with Ukraine's largest private energy firm DTEK describing the damage as "significant." The Russian Ministry of Defence claimed these were "massive strikes against energy facilities used by the Ukrainian Armed Forces."

Healthcare Targeted

On January 13, a Russian long-range drone struck a medical facility for children in Kharkiv, causing a fire. This attack came as part of a broader pattern of strikes on civilian infrastructure. In the southern city of Odesa on January 13, residential buildings, a hospital, and a kindergarten were damaged, with at least five people wounded in two waves of attacks, according to regional governor Sergiy Lysak.

International Response

Military Aid Updates

Norway announced 340 million euros ($397 million) in emergency funding to support Ukraine's energy sector and help the government maintain critical services as part of its 2026 aid package. This funding specifically targets energy infrastructure repair and maintenance.

However, challenges persist. The Pentagon halted arms shipments to Ukraine in July 2025 due to concerns about depleted U.S. stockpiles, affecting high-demand munitions including Patriot missiles, AIM-7 Sparrow air-to-air missiles, and Stinger missiles. Brad Bowman of the Foundation for Defense of Democracies warned that "if we deprive Kyiv of PAC-3 (Patriot) interceptors it means more Russian missiles getting through and more dead Ukrainians."

Germany has emerged as Ukraine's largest arms supplier in 2025, providing substantial military assistance. German Foreign Minister Johann Wadephul and US counterpart Marco Rubio agreed on the importance of a transatlantic alliance to secure lasting peace in Ukraine, emphasizing commitment to NATO's Article 5.

G7 Diplomatic Efforts

The "Coalition of the Willing" summit in Paris on January 6 marked a significant diplomatic development, bringing together representatives from 35 countries including 27 heads of state. The summit's reported draft joint statement outlines security guarantees that would include:

  • A U.S.-backed, European-led force deployed in post-war Ukraine
  • Ceasefire monitoring by Washington
  • A European-led multinational force for Ukraine
  • Commitment to support Ukraine in case of Russia breaching a ceasefire agreement
  • Long-term defence cooperation with Ukraine

UK and France Peacekeeping Proposal

As part of the multinational force, the UK and France stated their willingness to station troops in Ukraine after a ceasefire has been put in place as a military backstop in case of ceasefire violations. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz spoke for the first time about possible involvement of German troops in securing a ceasefire, though he specified they could only be stationed in NATO countries bordering Ukraine and would require parliamentary approval.

Peace Negotiations Status

Despite diplomatic efforts, significant obstacles remain. Russia has publicly rejected the draft peace plan, with Foreign Ministry officials in early January confirming they would not accept the presence of European troops in Ukraine as part of proposed postwar security guarantees for Kyiv.

Russian President Vladimir Putin declared in December that Russia's war aims in Ukraine will be met "unconditionally" and vowed to "liberate" what he termed Russia's "historical lands." At the UN, Russia's ambassador Vassily Nebenzia said that until President Zelensky "comes to his senses and agrees to realistic conditions for negotiations, we will continue solving the problem by military means."

Atlantic Council analysis notes that "Putin has no obvious route to victory in 2026 but cannot accept a compromise peace as any settlement that safeguarded Ukrainian independence would be seen in Moscow as an historic Russian defeat." After nearly four years of full-scale war, a negotiated settlement securing Ukraine's status as an independent country would represent a major political failure for Putin, who "would risk entering Russian history as the man who lost Ukraine."

Outlook and Projections

Short-Term Forecast (January-March 2026)

Military analysts expect Russia to continue its offensive operations across multiple fronts, with particular focus on capturing the remainder of Donetsk Oblast and making gains in the Kupyansk, Slovyansk, and Zaporizhia directions. The harsh winter conditions may slow mechanized operations but are unlikely to halt the conflict. Russia's use of long-range strikes against Ukrainian energy infrastructure is expected to continue as part of Moscow's strategy to break Ukrainian civilian morale.

The deployment of the new Geran-5 drone variant, with its extended 1,000-kilometer range and potential for air-to-air engagement, represents an evolution in Russian strike capabilities that may complicate Ukrainian air defense operations. Russia likely intends to use the Geran-5 to target aerial aspects of Ukraine's air defense umbrella, including interceptor drones and fighter jets.

Diplomatic Prospects

While President Zelensky's expressed optimism about ending the war in the first half of 2026 aligns with Cyprus's EU presidency timeline, significant obstacles remain. As noted by The Guardian, "there is no visible political will from Russia to engage in serious talks. President Putin thinks time is on his side, and feels emboldened by direct talks with Trump and his transactionally minded administration."

The Paris Declaration remains a "declaration of intent" rather than a concrete agreement. Critical issues remain unresolved, including what constitutes a ceasefire violation and what response it would trigger. The record of the Trump administration to date does not instill confidence in a durable commitment to any agreement linking European troops, a sizeable Ukrainian army, and U.S. involvement.

Long-Term Strategic Assessment

Russia's Strategic Calculus

Atlantic Council analysis outlines two scenarios that could disrupt Russia's current trajectory of escalating while stalling negotiations:

  1. Economic Crisis: A collapse in global oil prices combined with successful secondary sanctions enforcement could create economic crisis forcing Putin to revise priorities.
  2. Failed Spring Offensive: Mass casualties during a failed spring 2026 Russian offensive could trigger domestic instability while highlighting fading prospects of military breakthrough.

Both outcomes require significant additional action from Ukraine's partners. If the West cannot muster the requisite political will, "escalation remains Moscow's most rational path in 2026. Putin has little choice but to continue his invasion."

Humanitarian Concerns

With February marking the fifth year of the war, humanitarian organizations anticipate continued deterioration of civilian conditions, particularly during winter months. The issue of territorial concessions remains a major sticking point. President Trump's 28-point peace plan unveiled in November 2025 involved Kyiv ceding not only large amounts of Russian-occupied territory but also some territory that Ukrainian forces currently control—a proposal Zelensky has stated is unacceptable.

The environmental catastrophe unfolding in occupied Donbas, with contaminated water sources and irreversible ecological damage from irresponsible mining practices, represents a long-term humanitarian crisis that will persist long after active hostilities cease.
