Major Military Developments

Russia Deploys Oreshnik Hypersonic Missile

In a significant escalation, Russia launched its Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missile for only the second time in the war on January 9, 2026. The missile struck western Ukraine, targeting an underground natural gas storage facility in the Lviv region according to Russian military bloggers. This marked a clear warning to Ukraine's NATO allies, as the strike occurred near the border with Poland, a major hub for Western military aid flows.

According to the Russian Defense Ministry, the Oreshnik flies at Mach 10 (approximately 7,600 mph) and is "unstoppable by any missile defense system." Ukrainian intelligence indicates the missile carries six warheads, each containing six submunitions. Russian President Vladimir Putin has stated that several Oreshnik missiles used in a conventional strike could be "as devastating as a nuclear attack."

"Putin doesn't want peace, Russia's reply to diplomacy is more missiles and destruction." — EU Foreign Policy Chief Kaja Kallas

The attack killed at least four people in Kyiv, including an emergency medical worker, and damaged approximately 6,000 apartment buildings, leaving about half of Kyiv's residents without heat amid temperatures of -8°C (17.6°F). The strike also damaged the Qatari Embassy in Kyiv.

Frontline Situation and Territorial Control

Despite intensive Russian offensives, territorial changes remained minimal in the first week of January 2026. According to Ukrainian military assessments:

  • Pokrovsk Direction: Russian forces continue offensive operations but have failed to capture the city after 18 months of assaults. Ukrainian drone operators maintain fire control over Russian ground lines of communication, forcing Russian troops to deliver supplies on foot within 5-7 kilometers of the frontline.
  • Kupiansk Sector: Ukrainian forces conducted successful counterattacks, almost completely clearing the city and surrounding approaches that Russia had claimed to capture three separate times in 2025.
  • Kharkiv Region: Russian forces attacked near Vovchansk, Zelene, and other settlements. Ukrainian forces marginally advanced in western Vovchanski Khutory.
  • Donetsk Region: Intense fighting continued around Kostiantynivka, Chasiv Yar, and Druzhkivka. Ukraine still controls approximately 22% of the Donetsk region, including the major fortified agglomeration of Kramatorsk-Sloviansk-Druzhkivka-Kostiantynivka.

Casualties and Personnel Losses

Ukraine's drone forces achieved a significant milestone in December 2025, killing or seriously wounded approximately 33,000 Russian servicemen—matching or exceeding Russia's estimated monthly recruitment rate of 30,000-35,000 new soldiers. According to Ukrainian Army Commander General Oleksandr Syrsky:

"Last December was the first month when the Unmanned Systems Forces (USF) of the Armed Forces of Ukraine neutralized approximately as many servicemen of the occupation army as Russia called up for a month... The enemy lost more than 33,000 personnel. This number includes only confirmed video cases. The real losses of the occupiers are greater."

Official Ukrainian figures report total Russian personnel losses since February 2022 at 1,214,500, including 1,040 casualties on January 10, 2026 alone. Independent analysis by Mediazona, using verified death notices and obituaries, confirmed approximately 156,000 Russian soldiers killed in action in 2025—a 40% increase over 2024 figures.

The Icelandic researcher Ragnar Gmundsson estimated total Russian personnel losses for 2025 at 416,550 men, of whom approximately 110,000 died. Ukrainian sources report that for the second consecutive year, the number of killed Russian soldiers has exceeded the number wounded.

Weapons Systems and Air Operations

Ukrainian F-16 Fleet Performance

Ukraine's F-16 fighter jets, which entered combat service in August 2024, have achieved remarkable success in air defense missions. According to a Ukrainian Air Force pilot interviewed in early January:

  • 1,000+ aerial targets destroyed, including Shahed drones, jet-powered drones, and cruise missiles
  • 1,600+ ground-attack sorties conducted using Small Diameter Bombs and other precision munitions
  • One pilot reportedly shot down six cruise missiles and seven drones in a single sortie, using both missiles and the aircraft's 20mm cannon

However, Ukrainian pilots revealed that Western-provided tactics proved unsuitable for Ukraine's battlefield realities. The pilot explained: "The tactics we were taught abroad didn't quite match the war we're fighting. These tactics were based on the kinds of wars our partners had fought in before. But this war is fundamentally different."

Ukrainian crews adapted by developing new methods for:

  • Intercepting cruise missiles and attack drones near the front lines
  • Operating at low altitudes to avoid Russian surface-to-air missile systems and long-range air-to-air missiles
  • Using F-16s as decoys to draw Russian missiles away from strike aircraft

Notably, no confirmed air-to-air kills against Russian crewed aircraft have been reported. Ukrainian pilots face significant threats from Russian Su-35, Su-57, and MiG-31 fighters operating at high altitudes and launching R-37M missiles from within Russian airspace.

Ukraine has lost four F-16s in different incidents since the type entered service. To mitigate losses, Ukraine regularly disperses its F-16 fleet across multiple airfields and secondary strips, including highway landing zones.

On January 12, 2026, Russian sources claimed an S-300 anti-aircraft unit shot down a Ukrainian F-16 using two missiles. According to the Russian commander with the call sign "North," it required both missiles to destroy the aircraft, which he described as "indestructible."

Drone Warfare

Ukraine's Unmanned Systems Forces conducted 339,000 individual drone flight missions in December 2025, plus 2,100 ground drone missions. Ukrainian Maj. Robert Brovdi, commander of the USF, reported that the successful strike rate increased by 42% from November to December, with further improvements expected in January.

Ukrainian drones are credited with destroying:

  • 300+ ground targets, including command posts, drone control stations, ammunition depots, and logistical facilities
  • "Hundreds of pieces" of enemy equipment
  • Approximately one-quarter to one-half of all Russian personnel losses (with artillery and other causes accounting for the remainder)

Russian forces are also intensifying drone production and recruitment. Syrsky acknowledged that staying ahead in the "battlefield of unmanned systems" is a top priority for Ukraine's armed forces.

Diplomatic Developments and Peace Talks

Paris Summit: Major Progress on Security Guarantees

In what Ukrainian officials described as a significant breakthrough, Ukraine and its Western allies reached agreement on a framework for security guarantees at a January 6 summit in Paris. The declaration, signed by Ukraine, France, and the United Kingdom, establishes provisions for:

  • Multinational force deployment to Ukraine after any ceasefire agreement
  • "Military hubs" operated by the UK and France on Ukrainian soil to provide ongoing security support
  • Trilateral documents involving US-Ukrainian security guarantees and potential US-Ukraine-Russia peace agreements

European nations including Sweden, Spain, and Lithuania also committed to participating in post-ceaseforce troop deployments. Swedish Prime Minister Ulf Kristersson stated Sweden was ready to provide both Gripen fighters and support for Black Sea demining operations after the war.

President Zelensky indicated that progress had been made on "bilateral security guarantees" and that he expected to sign documents in the near future. However, he noted that territorial issues and control over the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant remained unresolved and might require direct discussion with US President Donald Trump.

Russian Rejection of European Peacekeepers

In a major blow to peace prospects, Russia rejected the deployment of European peacekeepers to Ukraine. After Western leaders signed the Paris declaration, Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov stated Moscow would "toughen its negotiating position," citing Ukraine's alleged drone attack on one of Putin's residences (a claim both Ukraine and the US rejected).

Analysts note that Russia appears emboldened by its belief that it is winning the war, making concessions unlikely. Putin has made no mention of peace in recent speeches, instead praising Russian soldiers for being on a "holy mission."

US-Venezuela Operation and Regional Implications

The January 4 US military operation in Caracas, which resulted in the capture of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro, has significant implications for the Russia-Ukraine conflict. The US also seized a Russian-flagged oil tanker, the Mariner, after a weeks-long pursuit in the Atlantic Ocean.

These developments are expected to:

  • Reduce global oil prices as Venezuelan oil returns to markets, squeezing Russia's war budget
  • Demonstrate US resolve after Russian air defenses failed to prevent the Maduro operation
  • Pressure Russia's shadow fleet of oil tankers attempting to bypass sanctions

Analyst Michael Burry noted that "Russian oil just became less important" in the mid- to long-term, while former US Ambassador to Ukraine John Herbst predicted the move would "reduce Russia's income and power."

Energy Infrastructure Attacks

Russian Strikes on Ukrainian Energy Systems

Russia continues its systematic campaign against Ukraine's energy infrastructure. Recent attacks have had devastating humanitarian consequences:

  • Dnipropetrovsk region: Russian drone attacks plunged the industrial southeast into darkness, leaving approximately 1 million people without electricity, water, and heating in January.
  • Kyiv: Overnight missile and drone strikes killed four people, damaged the Qatari Embassy, and left nearly 6,000 apartment buildings without heat.
  • National impact: About half of Kyiv's apartment buildings lost heating during sub-zero temperatures.

Ukrainian officials described the attacks as "deliberate torment" targeting civilians to break morale as peace negotiations continue.

Ukrainian Strikes on Russian Energy Infrastructure

Ukraine continues asymmetric attacks on Russian energy infrastructure:

  • January 10: Ukrainian drones sparked a fire at a Russian oil depot in southern Russia.
  • Ongoing campaign: Ukraine has conducted numerous strikes on Russian oil refineries, storage facilities, and the "shadow fleet" tankers transporting Russian oil.

Economic Developments

Russia's Wartime Economy Under Pressure

Russia's economic growth has slowed markedly despite unprecedented military spending. Key indicators:

  • GDP growth: Slowed to 0.6-1.5% in 2025, down from earlier expectations of 2.5%
  • November 2025: GDP expanded just 0.1% year-on-year; industrial output declined 0.7%
  • Q3 2025: GDP grew only 0.6%, down from 1.1% in Q2 and 1.4% in Q1
  • Inflation: Running at 6.6-9% (down from 9.5% in 2024 but still elevated)
  • Interest rates: Central Bank rate at nearly 20%, suppressing investment and consumption

Military spending has reached historic levels:

  • RUB 8.48 trillion (~$106 billion) in the first half of 2025
  • Over 40% of the federal budget (approximately 8% of GDP)
  • Tripled compared to 2022 levels
  • 31% increase compared to 2023

Essentially, one out of every two rubles in tax revenue now funds military purposes. This massive fiscal commitment has created deep structural distortions as the defense sector absorbs labor, capital, and resources at the expense of civilian industries.

Russian economists warn that the marginal economic contribution of additional military spending is diminishing, with risks of prolonged stagnation or recession increasing.

Tax Increases and Austerity Measures

President Putin has ordered a "significant increase" in tax collection and compliance:

  • Consumption tax: Increased from 20% to 22% on January 1, expected to generate ~$12.3 billion
  • New electronics tax: Planned for September 2026
  • Budget cuts: Transferred from Moscow to regions, affecting pensions and education

Russia's debt-to-GDP ratio remains relatively low at just below 20%, with an annual deficit of about 3.5%. However, household incomes are stagnating, and business leaders complain there is little incentive to invest in the high-interest-rate environment.

Oil Price Pressure

A 20% drop in oil prices during 2025 has squeezed Putin's war chest. The potential return of Venezuelan oil to global markets could further depress prices, with significant implications for Russian revenues. Oil exports have fallen from 50% of state income to 25% as Russia has adapted to sanctions.

Humanitarian Crisis

Civilian Casualties and Displacement

Recent Russian attacks have killed and wounded civilians across Ukraine:

  • Kyiv attack (January 9): At least 4 killed, 25 wounded
  • Dnipropetrovsk region: 7 wounded, including children
  • National: Tens of thousands of civilians killed or wounded by relentless Russian missile and drone strikes since February 2022

The UN and humanitarian agencies continue supporting efforts to restore energy services, provide emergency shelter, and deliver aid to affected populations. The World Health Organization has warned that attacks on healthcare facilities continue, with the healthcare system under immense strain.

Winter Conditions

With temperatures dropping to -8°C (17.6°F) and below, damage to heating infrastructure creates life-threatening conditions. Municipal services in Kyiv have restored power and heat to hospitals and maternity wards using portable boiler units, but residential buildings face extended outages.

Naval Operations in the Black Sea

Shadow Fleet Targeted

Ukraine continues its campaign against Russia's "shadow fleet"—vessels attempting to bypass sanctions and transport Russian oil:

  • US seizure: The Russian-flagged tanker Mariner was seized after a weeks-long pursuit
  • Ukrainian attacks: Multiple shadow fleet tankers hit off the Turkish coast
  • Black Sea attacks: Russia struck two cargo vessels off Ukraine's southern coast, killing a Syrian crew member

Turkey has launched a new phase of a demining mission in the Black Sea, joined by Bulgaria and Romania, to ensure safe commercial shipping amid the ongoing conflict.

Leadership Changes

Zelensky Reshuffle

President Zelensky continues a leadership shake-up that began in late 2025, replacing senior officials implicated in corruption scandals. Analysts suggest the reshuffle aims to:

  • Neutralize political rivals before potential peace negotiations
  • Restore trust with Western partners and the Ukrainian public
  • Account for the Trump administration's approach to Ukraine

The changes include replacements in Ukraine's security service and the hiring of a Canadian economic adviser.

Outlook and Projections

Prospects for 2026

Most analysts believe the war is unlikely to end in 2026. Ruth Deyermond of King's College London stated: "None of the conditions for a final resolution of the conflict are in place. Neither Ukraine nor Russia seems to be in a position to achieve a conclusive victory on the battlefield."

Key factors:

  • Russia's intransigence: Moscow shows no interest in concessions on territory, security guarantees, or the Zaporizhzhia nuclear plant
  • Ukrainian resilience: Despite manpower shortages, Ukraine is expected to fight until it runs out of arms or soldiers
  • Economic pressures: Russia's war machine will face greater pressure in 2026 than at any point in the past four years
  • Western support: The Paris security guarantees represent progress, but implementation depends on a ceasefire that Russia has rejected

Scenario Planning

Possible outcomes include:

  1. Continuation of all-out conflict: Limited territorial gains, high casualties (1.5+ million total combatant casualties to date), continuing strikes throughout Ukraine and Russia
  2. Frozen conflict: A temporary ceasefire without resolution, similar to other frozen conflicts, with both sides believing they can improve their position by resuming fighting later
  3. Effective Ukrainian surrender: If the Trump administration pressures Kyiv to accept Russian terms, likely leading to political volatility in Ukraine and renewed conflict later

As one analyst noted: "By late December 2026, we'll likely be asking ourselves if the war is likely to end in 2027."
