Executive Summary

Roughly one-third of new car registrations in the EU were electric in 2025, with the bloc's zero-emission fleet target effectively banning new internal combustion engine (ICE) sales from 2035. The European continent is teeming with EVs, and it has never been easier to own and charge one. However, Chinese competition looms large, with Chinese-brand plug-in share in Europe nearly doubling in 2025 from 3.4% to 6%.

Key Findings:

  • Market Share: Pure electric vehicles accounted for 16.9% of all new vehicle purchases in the EU (Jan-Nov 2025), up from 13.4% in 2024
  • Charging Infrastructure: Over 1 million public chargers deployed across the European Union
  • Policy Support: From 2025, vehicle manufacturers must reduce emissions by 15% (vs 2021 levels); 55% reduction by 2030; 100% (zero g/km) by 2035
  • China's Influence: Chinese EVs accounted for 85% of all electric vehicles sold in Brazil in 2024; similar patterns emerging in Europe
  • Consumer Adoption: Once EV sales reach 1%, they accelerate following an S-curve pattern

Policy Framework and 2035 ICE Ban

EU CO2 Emission Standards

Mandatory Reduction Targets

  • 2025 Onwards: Vehicle manufacturers must reduce emissions from new cars by 15% (vs 2021 levels)
  • 2030 Target: 55% fleet-wide CO2 reduction required
  • 2035 Target: 100% reduction (zero g/km) – effectively banning new ICE vehicle sales

2025 Amendment

In May 2025, the standards were amended to add an averaging provision for 2025-2027, meaning manufacturers can comply with targets by averaging their performance over the three years. The regulation is scheduled for review in 2026.

Compliance Status

  • Automakers are on track to meet EU CO2 performance targets
  • 3 manufacturer pools are within 5% compliance with 2025-2027 targets
  • Vehicles manufacturers rely mostly on electric cars as compliance option

Charging Infrastructure Development

Public Charging Deployment

EU Charging Infrastructure Status (End of 2025)

  • Total Public Chargers: >1 million (40 GW total capacity)
  • Leader by Country: Netherlands with almost 200,000 public chargers (most are low-power AC chargers)
  • Per Capita Leader: Norway with around 30,000 stalls (one-third are DC fast chargers)
  • Growth: Overall deployed at sufficient pace to support current EVs on road

Charging Costs

€7.43 per 100 km driven for battery electric vehicle charging – 33% cheaper than gasoline ICE vehicles. However, public charging costs vary significantly across Europe.

Smart Charging and Tariffs

  • 390 EV-related smart tariffs and services available
  • Smart meter deployment increasing
  • Time-varying tariff offers expanding
  • 13 of 26 EU member states have programs to foster private residential charging

Chinese Competition in Europe

Chinese Brand Expansion (2025)

  • Chinese-brand plug-in share in Europe nearly doubled: 3.4% → 6%
  • 10 new affordable models priced under €25,000 introduced by Chinese automakers
  • BYD, Geely, and other Chinese brands actively expanding European presence
  • Chinese EVs accounted for 85% of all electric vehicles sold in Brazil in 2024 (similar pattern emerging in Europe)

Competitive Landscape

  • Price Competition: Chinese EVs bringing down prices; pressure on European margins
  • Technology Race: European automakers focusing on next-generation platforms to compete
  • Supply Chain: China's near-complete monopoly on battery production creates dependency concerns
  • Market Share Battle: Europe must produce EVs that buyers choose over Chinese models

Challenges and Barriers

Infrastructure Gaps

  • More chargers needed in countries with lower EV uptake
  • Urban vs. rural charging access disparities
  • Grid capacity challenges for rapid charging expansion
  • Apartment dwellers lacking home charging options

Economic Barriers

Cost Challenges

  • EVs still not reaching price parity with pure combustion cars
  • Affordable EV models remain limited but increasing
  • 21+ affordable BEV models (<€30,000) now available
  • Battery prices decreasing but still ~40% of vehicle cost

Supply Chain Vulnerabilities

  • Battery Dependence: Europe imports majority of cells and upstream materials from China
  • Raw Materials: Critical minerals (lithium, cobalt, nickel) sourcing challenges
  • Manufacturing Capacity: Need to build European battery production scale

Consumer Barriers

  • Range anxiety (though lessening with improved batteries)
    • Charging time vs. refueling convenience Resale value uncertainty Technology familiarity and education gaps

Future Outlook: 2026-2035

2026 Predictions

  • Acceleration: New EU CO2 targets from 2025-2027 will drive growth
  • Chinese Competition:Further market share gains by Chinese brands expected
  • Infrastructure Expansion: Continued rapid deployment of public chargers
  • Price Parity: More affordable models reaching cost competitiveness

2030 and 2035 Targets

EU Regulatory Requirements

  • 2030: 55% fleet-wide CO2 reduction target for new passenger cars
  • 2035: 100% reduction target (zero g/km) – effectively banning new ICE sales
  • Midterm Review: 2026 review will assess progress and potentially adjust requirements

Oil Displacement Impact

EVs to displace 10 million b/d of oil by 2035 according to IEA's stated policies scenario. This represents a significant impact on global oil demand as electrification accelerates.

Long-Term Success Factors

  1. Policy Alignment: Maintaining consistent government support
  2. Infrastructure Investment: Expanding charging networks, especially fast chargers
  3. Supply Chain Localization: Reducing dependence on Chinese batteries and materials
  4. Competitive Products: European automakers producing vehicles that compete on price and features
  5. Consumer Education: Building awareness and trust in EV technology
