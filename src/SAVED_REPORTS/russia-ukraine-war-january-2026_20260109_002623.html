Russia-Ukraine War: Latest Developments

January 2026 | Comprehensive Situation Report

Executive Summary

As January 2026 begins, the Russia-Ukraine war enters its fourth year with intensifying military operations, unprecedented hypersonic missile attacks, and accelerated diplomatic efforts. Russian casualties have reached unsustainable levels according to Ukrainian estimates, while peace talks in Paris show "concrete results" but remain stalled on fundamental territorial questions. The conflict continues to have profound implications for global security, with experts warning that Europe's war has made conflict in the Indo-Pacific more likely.

Recent Military Developments

Hypersonic Missile Strikes

On January 8, 2026, Russia launched a rare hypersonic Oreshnik missile attack against Ukraine, marking one of the most significant escalations in advanced weaponry use. The Oreshnik missile, traveling up to 10 times the speed of sound (approximately 13,000 kilometers per hour), struck a critical infrastructure facility in the western city of Lviv. This missile system represents one of Russia's newest weapons, capable of carrying multiple warheads with either conventional or nuclear payloads.

The missile was "moving at a speed of about 13,000 kilometers per hour along a ballistic trajectory," according to Ukraine's Air Command "West." This was only the second time Russia has used the Oreshnik system, having first deployed it in November 2024 against Dnipro despite the weapon not being fully developed at that time.

In Kyiv, the attack began around midnight with multiple drone strikes on residential buildings, leaving four people dead and ten wounded. Critical infrastructure was damaged, plunging large parts of the city into darkness as temperatures plummeted to -5 degrees Celsius (23 degrees Fahrenheit).

Drone Warfare Evolution

Both sides have dramatically escalated their use of unmanned systems in 2025, with Ukraine successfully stepping up drone production to reach 1,000 intercept drones per day by year's end. Russia has responded by creating separate drone systems units numbering 80,000 military personnel, with plans to double this to 165,500 in 2026 and reach 210,000 by 2030.

Russia introduced wired fiber-optic drones in 2025 that are impervious to electronic jamming, which Ukrainian commander-in-chief Oleksandr Syrskii credited for Russia's ability to capture the city of Siversk in Donetsk. On January 5, 2026, Russia introduced another innovation: Shahed drones mounted with Man-Portable Air Defence Systems (MANPADS) designed to shoot down drone-hunting aircraft.

The war claimed the first two civilian deaths of 2026 on January 6, when a Russian drone struck a hospital in Kyiv, killing a patient, and a second civilian was killed southwest of the capital.

Russian Casualties Rising to "Unsustainable Levels"

Ukrainian figures suggest Russian mortality rates on the front lines are reaching levels that cannot be sustained by Russia's current method of voluntary recruitment. President Volodymyr Zelenskyy reported that in December 2025, 35,000 "occupiers were eliminated," up from 30,000 in November and 26,000 in October.

"In December, 35,000 occupiers were eliminated – and this has been confirmed with video footage. In November, there were 30,000, and in October, 26,000 eliminated occupiers."

— President Volodymyr Zelenskyy, January 6, 2026

Commander-in-chief Syrskii noted that December 2025 marked "the first month when the unmanned systems units of the Ukrainian Defence Forces neutralised approximately as many servicemen of the occupying army as Russia conscripted in a month." Russia had reached its quota of 403,000 recruits in 2025 (averaging 33,583 per month) and planned to increase this to 34,083 per month in 2026.

Ukraine estimates that almost 420,000 Russian troops were killed or wounded in 2025 alone. If these casualty reports are accurate, they suggest Russia's current recruitment model is no longer sustainable and may force Moscow to start using its active reserve or conscripts—a move that would carry significant political risk for President Vladimir Putin.

Tactical Shifts and Territorial Gains

Russia has shifted from large mechanized assaults that resulted in huge losses to infiltration tactics using small teams of two soldiers to establish bridgeheads before reinforcements arrive. These tactics enabled Russia to capture two-thirds of Pokrovsk and roughly half of neighboring Myrnohrad in Donetsk by the end of 2025.

Russia's average daily rate of advance was 13.24 square km per day in 2025, compared with 9.87 square km per day in 2024. However, its territorial gains for the entire year amounted to only 0.8 percent of Ukraine, consisting primarily of villages and fields. Russia continues to aim for full control of Donetsk, Zaporizhia, and Kherson—three regions it has, on paper, annexed in their entirety.

Political Developments and Peace Talks

Paris Summit: "Coalition of the Willing"

On January 6, 2026, the largest-ever summit of the "coalition of the willing" convened in Paris, bringing together representatives from 35 countries that have pledged support for Ukraine. The United States pledged to lead a "ceasefire monitoring and verification mechanism" with European participation if a peace deal is reached. Washington also committed to "support" a European-led multinational force deployed in Ukraine after any ceasefire "in case of" a new Russian attack.

France and the United Kingdom said they would deploy forces to Ukrainian territory if a ceasefire was agreed. The US was represented by special envoy Steve Witkoff and senior adviser Jared Kushner, while French President Emmanuel Macron welcomed President Zelenskyy for a one-to-one meeting before the summit.

"Concrete Results" Reported

Zelenskyy's new chief of staff, Kyrylo Budanov (formerly the head of military intelligence), reported that "concrete results" had been reached in ongoing talks over an end to the war and post-conflict security guarantees with Western allies. However, he provided no details about the progress made.

"Ukraine does not shy away from the most difficult issues and will never be an obstacle to peace. Peace must be dignified."

— President Volodymyr Zelenskyy, January 6, 2026

Zelenskyy has said that a peace deal is "90% ready" but warned that the remaining 10%—which includes fundamental territorial questions and security protections—would "determine the fate of peace, the fate of Ukraine and Europe, how people will live."

Territorial Disputes Remain Core Obstacle

Russia occupies approximately 20% of Ukrainian territory and has demanded that Ukraine cede four of its eastern regions. Within that topic is the status of the Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant. Kyiv has warned that ceding ground will embolden Moscow and said it will not sign a peace deal that fails to deter Russia from invading again.

German Chancellor Friedrich Merz wrote that "Russia is showing little willingness to negotiate, President Zelenskyy is struggling to maintain unity among Ukrainians, and transatlantic cooperation has changed profoundly."

Russia's Stance

Russia has repeatedly opposed any NATO boots on the ground in Ukraine to monitor a halt in hostilities. On January 8, the Russian Ministry of Defense doubled down on its longstanding position, declaring that Western forces deployed to Ukraine as part of any future peace deal would be considered "legitimate targets." Moscow has given no indication that it would accept a settlement backed by foreign troops inside Ukraine.

During the Paris talks, President Vladimir Putin attended a Russian Orthodox Christmas service where he hailed his troops' "holy mission" to defend the country, saying: "Russia's warriors have always, as if at the Lord's behest, carried out this mission of defending the fatherland and its people, saving the motherland and its people."

Humanitarian Impact

Civilian Casualties and Infrastructure Damage

According to World Vision, more than 2,400 children have been killed or injured since the escalation began in February 2022, averaging 16 child casualties each week. The situation remains dire due to ongoing hostilities and repeated attacks on civilian and energy infrastructure.

Millions of families are experiencing prolonged power outages, damaged housing, and limited access to basic services. These challenges are especially dangerous during winter months, with temperatures forecast to drop below 14°F (-10°C) in many areas. In some regions, families go up to 36 hours without heating, electricity, or water.

"I get frustrated when the lights go out because nothing can be done—no heating, no food, no studying—and our apartment gets very cold."

— Andriy, 9, a school student from Kyiv

Blackouts from attacks on energy infrastructure leave homes, schools, and hospitals without heat, electricity, or water. Children spend long hours in cold, dark homes, increasing illness risk. One in five families reports health problems linked to indoor cold.

Displacement and Humanitarian Needs

According to the UNHCR, 12.7 million people need humanitarian aid and protection. In addition to approximately 5 million people forced to live in Europe, at least 3.7 million more are displaced within Ukraine.

Psychological Impact on Children

Nearly 40% of school-aged children struggle with disrupted online or blended learning. Many shelter in basements during air raid sirens, and millions lack safe water or sanitation, raising disease risks.

"Prolonged exposure to extreme stress can have long-term effects on children's physical health, emotional stability, and ability to connect with others. For many, the damage is not temporary; it can reshape the very foundations of childhood."

— Anna, a psychologist at a World Vision-supported Child-Friendly Space in Kyiv

Global Security Implications

Warning for the Indo-Pacific

European officials warn that war in Europe has made conflict in the Indo-Pacific more likely. Military experts and diplomats agree that new strategies and tactics—drone-based or otherwise—playing out in Ukraine's theater of war may be replicated closer to home for other nations.

Andrius Kubilius, the former Lithuanian prime minister turned EU defence chief, has urged countries—including Australia—to rapidly scale up drone warfare capability: "It's very important for us to understand that what we now call provocations with drones can be a new type of warfare. You don't need always to send tanks or artillery through the border to occupy territory. But you can really terrorise neighbouring countries or countries further away with drones."

NATO's Eastern Flank

Poland's operational commander, Lt Gen Maciej Klisz, noted that while testing NATO's collective defence clause, Russia is not the only concern: "The world is interconnected and strategy is important. It includes Poland and the Ukrainian war, but it affects China as well and Australia over there."

Klisz warned that the weaponisation of maritime routes and targeting of strategic assets could be replicated by other countries, including in the Indo-Pacific. China's involvement in the war, along with North Korea and Iran, is concerning European leaders.

China-Russia Cooperation

Robert Kupiecki, Poland's undersecretary of state and security adviser, emphasized that "China and Russia cooperate strategically," and that "the international analysis and the number of scenarios of possible conflicts, parallel conflicts in Europe and in the Pacific Ocean, are great."

World War Three Concerns:

Security experts have warned that Donald Trump's capture of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro could provide the pretext and template for China to attack Taiwan or Russia to further undermine Ukraine. The rhetoric of armed conflict has reached "a never before seen scale" on both sides of the Atlantic, with NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte saying the West "must be prepared for the scale of war our grandparents and great-grandparents endured."

North Korean and Iranian Involvement

North Korea has sent thousands of troops and weapons to fight in Ukraine, raising concerns that Moscow could provide technology that strengthens Kim's nuclear-armed military. Meanwhile, Russia continues to rely on Iranian drones and imports artillery shells from North Korea, illustrating its dependency on allies for military supplies.

Analysis and Outlook

Strategic Calculations

Andrew Chakhoyan, an academic director at the University of Amsterdam and former U.S. government official, argues that the realist approach to Russia's aggression operates with a "shameless double standard," explaining Moscow's aggression as an understandable defensive reaction to NATO "expansion." He writes: "If we follow the realists' advice and let Moscow have its way, then NATO's Article 5 will be promptly tested. America and Europe will then face a choice: send troops to fight Russia directly, or watch the security architecture that delivered 70 years of peace collapse in real time."

Ukraine's Drone Success

Zelenskyy noted that the rising Russian casualty figures are "the result of the right decisions," referring primarily to Ukraine's successful drone production. This led to his appointment of Mykhailo Fedorov as defence minister, describing him as "deeply involved in the issues related to the drone line and works very effectively on digitalising public services and processes."

Ukraine reached its production target of 1,000 intercept drones per day by the end of 2025 and plans to increase this in 2026. The president praised departing Defence Minister Denys Shmyal for achieving this milestone.

Winter Infrastructure Attacks

Russia has resumed its tactic of targeting energy infrastructure to create chaos and apply psychological pressure on the population. During the first week of January 2026, Russia struck heating and electricity plants in Kharkiv. Ukraine's president has said that the aim of such attacks is "to create chaos and apply psychological pressure on the population."

The attacks come as the US Embassy in Ukraine warned of "a potentially significant air attack that may occur at any time over the next several days."
