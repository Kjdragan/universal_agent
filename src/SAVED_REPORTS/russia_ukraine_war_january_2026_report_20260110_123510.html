Russia-Ukraine War: January 2026 Comprehensive Report

Military Developments, Diplomatic Efforts, and Strategic Analysis

Executive Summary

As January 2026 begins, the Russia-Ukraine war enters its fifth year with no immediate end in sight. Despite renewed diplomatic efforts through US-brokered peace talks and a "coalition of the willing" offering security guarantees, fighting continues unabated across multiple fronts. Russia has achieved marginal territorial gains in 2025 at staggering human cost, while Ukraine continues to hold strategic positions and conduct effective long-range strikes against Russian military infrastructure. This report analyzes the military situation, diplomatic developments, casualty figures, and territorial changes as of January 10, 2026.

I. Military Developments in January 2026

A. Recent Escalation: Oreshnik Hypersonic Missile Strike

On January 9-10, 2026, Russia conducted a significant escalation by firing its hypersonic Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missile at Ukraine, striking near Lviv in western Ukraine—approximately 70km (45 miles) from the Polish border and NATO territory. According to Ukrainian officials, the attack killed four people and injured at least 22 in Kyiv, with additional strikes hitting critical infrastructure in Lviv.

Strategic Significance: The Oreshnik missile strike near Poland represents a deliberate signal to NATO and Western allies. The missile, which travels at speeds exceeding Mach 5 and is nuclear-capable, had only been used once before (November 2024). This marks the first time Russia has used the Oreshnik with what appears to be explosive warheads rather than dummy warheads used in testing.

B. Frontline Situation and Tactical Changes

As of January 7-10, 2026, Russian forces continue offensive operations across multiple sectors:

Northeastern Front (Sumy and Kharkiv Directions)

  • Russian forces attacking near Sumy City, with geolocated footage indicating advances in northern Yunakivka
  • Heavy fighting around Vovchansk and Vovchanski Khutory northeast of Kharkiv City
  • Ukrainian forces conducted marginal advances in western Vovchanski Khutory as of January 7
  • Russian 82nd Motorized Rifle Regiment reported as bogged down in forests west of Vovchansk with significant casualties

Kupyansk Direction

  • Ongoing Russian attempts to capture Kupyansk, with attacks toward the city itself and surrounding settlements
  • ISW assesses that Russian forces maintain infiltrator presence in eastern Kupyansk
  • Ukrainian forces cleared Russian small groups that had infiltrated near rear areas in Svyatopetrivka, Olenokostyantynivka, and Staroukrainka

Pokrovsk-Myrnohrad Axis (Critical Donetsk Sector)

Strategic Importance: Pokrovsk and Myrnohrad represent key logistical hubs in Donetsk region. Russia has been fighting to capture these towns for five months. By the end of 2025, Russian forces had captured roughly two-thirds of Pokrovsk and half of Myrnohrad.
  • ISW assesses Russian forces have infiltrated north of Pokrovsk, with positions intermixed with Ukrainian forces in industrial areas
  • Ukrainian drone operators maintain fire control over Russian ground lines of communication, forcing Russian resupply to be conducted on foot within 5-7km of the frontline
  • Russian forces shifted from large mechanized assaults to infiltration tactics using small teams (2-4 soldiers) to establish bridgeheads
  • Ukrainian 7th Rapid Response Corps reported 21 Russian airstrikes in the Pokrovsk-Myrnohrad direction on January 5 alone

Zaporizhia Direction

  • Geolocated footage indicates Russian advances in northern Hulyaipole as of January 7
  • Russian forces attempting infiltration through the dried bed of the former Kakhovka Reservoir near Malokaterynivka
  • Heavy fighting around Orikhiv, with Russian forces accumulating assault groups near Mali Shcherbaky, Shcherbaky, Novoandriivka, and Novodanylivka

C. Russian Tactical Innovations

Russian forces have implemented significant tactical changes in late 2025 and early 2026:

  • Fiber-Optic Drones: Introduction of wired drones impervious to electronic jamming, credited with enabling the capture of Siversk
  • Infiltration Tactics: Shift from large mechanized assaults to small team infiltrations using motorcycles and buggies for resupply
  • Anti-Thermal Camouflage: Use of special cloaks to avoid detection by thermal imaging at night
  • Shahed-MANPADS Innovation: Russia deployed Shahed drones mounted with Man-Portable Air Defence Systems designed to shoot down Ukrainian aircraft hunting drones
  • Expanded Drone Units: Russian drone forces number 80,000 personnel, with plans to double to 165,500 in 2026 and reach 210,000 by 2030

II. Diplomatic Efforts and Peace Talks

A. Paris Summit and "Coalition of the Willing"

On January 6, 2026, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy joined representatives from 35 countries including 27 heads of state in Paris for a meeting of the "coalition of the willing" focused on security guarantees for Ukraine.

Key Outcomes from Paris Summit

  • Agreement on multilayered international defense guarantees for Ukraine
  • The UK and France announced plans to establish "military hubs across Ukraine and build protected facilities for weapons and military equipment"
  • Commitments for equipment, training, and air, land, and sea support to deter future Russian aggression
  • Agreement to participate in US-led monitoring and verification of any ceasefire
  • Long-term military assistance commitments with Ukraine's armed forces to remain "first line of defense and deterrence"

B. Core Obstacles to Peace

Primary Sticking Point: According to multiple sources, the territorial issue remains the major obstacle to peace negotiations. Russia continues to push for full control of eastern Ukraine's Donbas region, while Zelenskyy has repeatedly stated that ceding Ukrainian territory is "unacceptable" and forbidden by Ukraine's constitution.

Other significant obstacles include:

  • Control of Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant: Disputed control remains unresolved
  • NATO Troop Deployment: Russian President Vladimir Putin has ruled out any deployment of troops from NATO countries on Ukrainian soil
  • Security Guarantees: Ukraine seeks binding, enforceable security guarantees, while details remain unclear
  • Trump Administration's Divided Attention: US diplomatic focus has shifted to Venezuela following the US military operation targeting Venezuelan leader Nicolas Maduro, potentially reducing attention on Ukraine peace negotiations

C. Ukrainian Leadership Changes

In a significant development, Zelenskyy appointed Kyrylo Budanov, the longstanding military intelligence head, as chief of staff following the resignation of Andrii Yermak in a corruption scandal. Budanov, known for planning audacious operations against Russia and maintaining contacts for prisoner exchanges, may signal a new approach to security and negotiations from Kyiv.

Unsustainable Casualty Trend: According to Ukrainian commander-in-chief Oleksandr Syrskii, December 2025 marked "the first month when the unmanned systems units of the Ukrainian Defence Forces neutralised approximately as many servicemen of the occupying army as Russia conscripted in a month." Russian recruitment averaged 33,583 per month in 2025, while Ukrainian drone units neutralized roughly 33,000 Russians in December 2025.

B. Independent Verification: Mediazona/BBC Count

Mediazona, working with the BBC Russian Service, has verified 156,161 confirmed Russian military deaths from February 24, 2022 to December 17, 2025. Their statistical model using Probate Registry data estimates the actual death toll at approximately 219,000 Russians.

Key Findings from Mediazona Analysis

  • 6,168 officers confirmed killed, including 12 generals (3 Lieutenant Generals, 7 Major Generals, 2 retired generals)
  • 2024 was bloodiest year: At least 100,000 Russian servicemen died in 2024 alone
  • Volunteers constitute largest category: By September 2024, volunteers became the largest category among those killed, reflecting the shift away from prisoner recruitment
  • Geographic distribution: Regions such as Dagestan (4,009), Buryatia (4,009), and the Kuzbass region (3,142) have suffered disproportionately high losses
  • Age distribution: The 21-23 age group accounted for most deaths in the first six months; volunteers and mobilized men are significantly older (30+ and 25+ respectively)

C. Russian Recruitment Challenges

Ukrainian military intelligence chief Kyrylo Budanov reported that Russia reached its quota of 403,000 recruits in 2025 and plans to increase to 34,083 per month in 2026. However, the rising casualty rates suggest current voluntary recruitment methods may be unsustainable, potentially forcing Russia to consider:

  • Deploying active reserve units
  • Using conscripts (currently kept out of the war)
  • Further mobilization, which carries significant political risk for Putin

D. Ukrainian Civilian Casualties

During the first week of January 2026 alone, Russia launched 789 drones and 10 missiles against Ukrainian cities. Ukraine shot down 83% of drones and one missile. Notable incidents include:

  • January 5: A patient killed when a Russian drone struck a hospital in Kyiv
  • January 5: Second civilian killed southwest of Kyiv
  • January 9-10: Four killed and at least 22 injured in Lviv from Oreshnik missile strike

IV. Territorial Changes and Control

A. 2025 Territorial Gains: Minimal for Maximum Cost

ISW Assessment: Russia's average daily rate of advance was 13.24 square km (5.1 sq miles) per day in 2025, compared to 9.87 sq km (3.8 sq miles) per day in 2024. However, this amounts to only 0.8 percent of Ukraine's territory—consisting of villages and fields—at the cost of nearly 420,000 Russian casualties.

According to the Ukrainian General Staff and ISW analysis:

  • Russian claims: 6,640 sq km (2,564 sq miles) captured and 334 settlements in 2025
  • ISW verified: Evidence of Russian presence in 4,952 sq km (1,912 sq miles) and 245 settlements
  • Ukrainian assessment: Territory amounting to 0.8% of Ukraine's 603,550 sq km was lost

B. Current Territorial Situation

Russia currently occupies approximately 20% of Ukraine's territory, including:

  • Control of all Luhansk region (part of Donbas)
  • Majority of Donetsk region (part of Donbas)
  • Approximately three-quarters of Zaporizhia region
  • Significant portions of Kherson region (east of the Dnipro River)
  • Crimea (annexed in 2014)

D. Phantom Gains and Information Warfare

According to Le Monde analysis, Russia has engaged in "phantom gains"—claiming territorial advances that either don't exist or are temporary. Notable examples:

  • Hulyaipole: Russia claimed to have seized the town, but only controls about 55%
  • Kupiansk: Russian military reporters admitted forces were being "squeezed out" despite claims of control
  • Systematic exaggeration: Kremlin-friendly outlets cited "systematic exaggeration of successes" leading to reserves being redeployed away from areas they were actually needed

V. Long-Range Strike Campaign

A. Ukrainian Strikes on Russian Territory

Ukraine has increasingly conducted long-range strikes against Russian military infrastructure:

  • January 6: Ukrainian Security Service (SBU) drones hit military arsenal in Kostroma region (triggering hours-long explosions) and oil depot in Lipetsk region (causing massive fire)
  • December 31, 2025: Successful Ukrainian strike against oil depot in Rybinsk
  • December 2025: Attack on Novoshakhtinsk refinery in Rostov

B. Russian Strikes on Ukraine

During 2025, Russia launched:

  • 54,000 long-range attack drones against Ukraine
  • 1,900 missiles against Ukraine
  • Last week of 2025 alone: 1,000+ drones and 33 missiles
  • Ukrainian interception rate: 86% of drones, 30 of 33 missiles in late December strikes
Energy Infrastructure Targeting: Russia continues systematic attacks on Ukraine's energy infrastructure, heating plants, and electricity generation facilities. On January 5, Russia struck heating and electricity plants in Kharkiv, and January 9-10 attacks targeted energy infrastructure and drone manufacturing sites in and around Kyiv.

VI. Strategic Analysis and Outlook

A. Russia's Strategic Calculus

Russian Objectives and Constraints

  • Primary Goal: Capture remaining unoccupied portions of Donetsk, Zaporizhia, and Kherson regions that Russia "annexed" in 2022
  • Constraint: Rising casualties at unsustainable levels for voluntary recruitment system
  • Tactical Shift: Abandoning large mechanized assaults in favor of infiltration tactics to reduce casualty rates
  • Information Operations: Using alleged Ukrainian attacks on Putin's residence to justify escalation and avoid peace talks
  • Long-term Preparation: Putin and Defense Minister Belousov preparing for prolonged war, with no immediate peace plans evident

B. Ukraine's Strategic Position

Ukrainian Strengths and Challenges

Strengths:

  • Effective drone warfare: 1,000 intercept drones per day production achieved by end of 2025
  • Successful long-range strikes against Russian logistics and military infrastructure
  • Maintaining defensive lines despite Russian pressure
  • Strong diplomatic backing from "coalition of the willing"

Challenges:

  • Exhaustion after nearly four years of full-scale war
  • Recruitment difficulties (Russian claims of 50% drop in Ukrainian recruitment)
  • Energy infrastructure under sustained attack
  • Political pressure regarding lack of elections during wartime

C. Peace Prospects

According to The Guardian analysis, Ukrainian MP Serhiy Rakhmanin identified three factors necessary for successful peace negotiations:

  1. More systematic military and financial support from Europe
  2. Stabilization of the frontline so Russian advances stop
  3. Serious economic problems beginning for Russia
Assessment: As of January 2026, there are "no objective reasons" for successful negotiations while Russia feels it is still advancing on the battlefield. However, there may be a small window for peace talks at the end of winter if the three factors above align.

D. Zelenskyy's Assessment

President Zelenskyy stated in his New Year's address that a peace agreement is "90% ready," but cautioned that "those 10% contain, in fact, everything." The remaining 10% includes core territorial disputes that neither side appears willing to compromise on.

VIII. Conclusions and Recommendations

Key Takeaways

  1. Military Stalemate: Despite territorial gains amounting to only 0.8% of Ukrainian territory, Russia continues offensive operations at unsustainable human cost
  2. Tactical Evolution: Both sides adapting to drone warfare, with Russia shifting to infiltration tactics and Ukraine maintaining fire control over Russian logistics
  3. Diplomatic Deadlock: Core territorial disputes remain unresolved; peace process "90% ready" but remaining 10% contains fundamental disagreements
  4. Escalation Risks: Oreshnik missile strike near NATO border represents dangerous escalation; potential for miscalculation increasing
  5. Sustainability Crisis: Russian casualty rates (35,000 in December 2025) may force difficult choices about conscription or mobilization in 2026
  6. International Support: "Coalition of the Willing" demonstrates continued backing, but US distraction with Venezuela may reduce diplomatic focus

Future Outlook (January-June 2026)

The war is likely to continue through at least the first half of 2026, with several critical factors determining the trajectory:

  • Weather: End of winter ground conditions will enable larger mechanized operations
  • Russian Manpower: Whether Russia can sustain recruitment without politically risky mobilization
  • Western Support: Ability of "coalition of the willing" to deliver concrete security guarantees and military assistance
  • Ukrainian Resilience: Ukrainian society's ability to withstand another year of war despite exhaustion
  • Chinese Economic Pressure: Whether China will increase pressure on Russia over economic disruption from sanctions
Warning: As the Institute for the Study of War has noted, Russia is setting conditions to deploy active reservists from Belgorod Oblast for combat missions, suggesting preparation for more intensive operations. The coming months may see a significant Russian offensive to capture remaining objectives in Donetsk and Zaporizhia regions before any potential peace negotiations gain momentum.
