Russia-Ukraine War: January 2026 Strategic Assessment

A Comprehensive Analysis of Military Developments, Territorial Control, and Peace Prospects

Executive Summary

As January 2026 marks nearly four years since Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, the conflict shows no signs of imminent resolution. Russian forces continue grinding offensive operations across multiple front lines, making incremental territorial gains at enormous human and material cost. The most significant development has been Russia's deployment of the Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missile system, a weapon not used since 2024, signaling Moscow's willingness to escalate strategically even as battlefield advances remain marginal.

According to the Institute for the Study of War (ISW) and Critical Threats assessments, Russian forces have made limited advances in several directions, particularly around Kupyansk, Kostyantynivka, Pokrovsk, and Hulyaipole. However, these gains have come at a high price, with reports of Russian units suffering severe casualties and struggling to consolidate positions. Ukrainian forces continue to conduct effective long-range strikes against Russian military and energy infrastructure, demonstrating sustained defensive capabilities despite manpower challenges.

Key Statistic: The war has killed or wounded more than 1.5 million combatants on both sides, according to Western intelligence estimates, making it the deadliest conflict in Europe since World War II.

Recent Military Developments and Territorial Changes

Northeastern Front: Kursk and Sumy Oblasts

Russian forces continue offensive operations in the Sumy direction, with geolocated footage indicating advances in northern Yunakivka (northeast of Sumy City) as of January 6, 2026. However, these operations have encountered significant difficulties:

  • Logistical Challenges: Elements of the Russian 34th Separate Motorized Rifle Brigade are reportedly struggling to advance toward Ryasne from Hrabovske
  • Personnel Crisis: A Russian milblogger reported that servicemembers in a 3.5-kilometer forested area along the international border are in "complete disarray without food or water" after months of intense fighting
  • Casualty Issues: Command elements of the Russian 6th Combined Arms Army are sending wounded servicemembers, including those from the 122nd Motorized Rifle Regiment, back into offensive operations, replacing losses with "poorly disciplined and injured servicemembers"
Assessment: The situation in the Sumy direction illustrates Russia's willingness to accept极高 casualties for minimal territorial gains, raising questions about the sustainability of such operations.

Kupyansk Direction

The Kupyansk front remains heavily contested, with ISW assessing that Russian forces operating in eastern Kupyansk maintain connections to infiltrators in the northern outskirts of the town. Ukrainian forces control areas of central Kupyansk, while Russian forces continue attacks toward the city itself and surrounding settlements including Dvorichanske, Kindrashivka, Odradne, Petropavlivka, Stepova Novoselivka, Pishchane, and Kurylivka.

Notably, geolocated footage shows Russian infiltrators operating in Kupyansk, though ISW assesses this does not represent a change in control of terrain. Russian forces have reportedly struggled to capture Kindrashivka and the eastern outskirts of Tyshchenkivka, with unconfirmed claims of advances not verified by visual evidence.

Borova and Lyman Sectors

Ukrainian forces have achieved some success in the Borova direction, with geolocated footage indicating the likely liberation of Nova Kruhlyakivka and confirmation that Ukrainian forces maintain control over northern Bohuslavka—areas Russian sources previously claimed to control.

In the Lyman direction, Russian forces continue attacks northwest toward Derylove, Shandryholove, and Serednie; north toward Karpivka; northeast toward Kolodyazi and Myrne; and east toward Zarichne. Russian sources have made unconfirmed claims of advances in eastern Svyatohirsk, central Ozerne, and north of Pazeno (southeast of Slovyansk), though these remain unverified.

Kostyantynivka Direction

This sector has seen some of the most intense fighting, with geolocated footage published on January 10 indicating that Ukrainian forces recently advanced in the southern outskirts of Kostyantynivka. The fighting has been characterized by:

  • Russian Infiltration Attempts: Multiple small-scale Russian infiltration operations that have not changed the forward edge of the battle area
  • Conflicting Claims: Russian milbloggers made competing claims about advances in Virolyubivka, Novodmytrivka, Berestok, and Illinivka, with some later admitting Russian forces could not consolidate positions
  • False Reporting: Kremlin-affiliated milbloggers acknowledged false reports about Russian seizures of several settlements, and one noted that Russian forces only recently seized Mayske despite the Ministry of Defense claiming it in late September 2025
Concern: The discrepancy between Russian Ministry of Defense claims and actual ground control suggests significant information warfare components to Russian reporting, complicating accurate assessment of territorial control.

Pokrovsk Direction

Pokrovsk remains one of the most active fronts, with Russian forces conducting intense attacks utilizing:

  • Guided Aerial Bombs: 20-30 KAB glide bombs launched daily against Ukrainian positions to destroy infrastructure and prevent effective defensive positioning
  • Small Unit Tactics: Groups of 1-3 servicemembers infiltrating settlements north of Pokrovsk, exploiting poor weather conditions that degrade Ukrainian drone operations
  • Mechanized Assaults: Using armored vehicles, light automobiles, all-terrain vehicles, and motorcycles in attempts to seize Rodynske and Bilytske

Ukrainian forces have maintained fire control over Russian ground lines of communication, forcing Russian resupply efforts to dismount and deliver supplies on foot within 5-7 kilometers of the frontline.

Hulyaipole Street Fighting

Hulyaipole has become a focal point of intense urban combat, with Ukrainian Southern Defense Forces Spokesperson Colonel Vladyslav Voloshyn reporting on January 10 that:

  • Russian forces are attacking with "all available means, including drones, aviation, and artillery"
  • Street fighting is ongoing "for each building in the city"
  • Russia is using infiltration tactics to sever Ukrainian ground lines of communication
  • Up to 100 drones operate simultaneously, including FPV drones, "waiter" drones, and fiber optic-controlled systems
  • Attack groups of 5-6 servicemembers equipped with electronic warfare equipment and anti-drone small arms are conducting assaults

Geolocated footage indicates Russian forces advanced in northern Hulyaipole as of January 7, though Ukrainian sources reported clearing infiltrated Russian small groups from multiple settlements in the area on January 5.

The Oreshnik Hypersonic Missile Strikes

Weapon System Deployment

In a significant escalation, Ukrainian and Russian officials confirmed that Russian forces conducted an Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missile strike against Ukraine on January 9, 2026. This marks the first use of this weapon system since 2024, representing a strategic messaging effort by Moscow.

Target and Impact

According to Russian state media reports, the strike targeted a facility in the Lviv region in western Ukraine. The Oreshnik system represents Russia's capability to conduct long-range precision strikes with advanced ballistic missiles, signaling Moscow's willingness to escalate strategically even as tactical gains remain limited.

Strategic Significance: The Oreshnik deployment serves as a warning to Ukraine and its Western backers, demonstrating Russia's capacity to strike deep into Ukrainian territory with advanced weaponry. However, it also reveals Russia's frustration with the slow pace of territorial advances on the battlefield.

International Reaction

The United Kingdom responded by accelerating £200 million of funding for deployment to Ukraine as air defense support was stepped up. This underscores the ongoing cycle of escalation and counter-escalation that has characterized the conflict.

Propaganda Dimensions

According to ISW, Russian state media and government officials heavily publicized the strike, framing it as a response to alleged Ukrainian provocations. Former Russian President Dmitry Medvedev made threatening statements, and the coverage emphasized the "unprecedented nature" of the attack. However, Ukrainian and Western sources noted that Russia had used similar systems previously, suggesting the publicity served primarily domestic and international messaging purposes.

Current Territorial Control Status

Russian-Controlled Territory

Nearly four years after the full-scale invasion began on February 24, 2022, Russian forces hold less Ukrainian territory than they did in the first few weeks of the onslaught. Russia continues to claim annexation of five Ukrainian regions—Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhzhya, Kherson, and Crimea—but controls only portions of these areas:

  • Donetsk: Russia has been unable to capture Donetsk in its entirety despite it being one of Russia's fundamental war goals
  • Zaporizhzhya: Ukraine controls sizable portions, including the city of Zaporizhzhia itself, though Russia occupies the Zaporizhzhya Nuclear Power Plant (ZNPP)
  • Kherson: Ukraine controls the city of Kherson and significant areas of the region, while Russia occupies the east bank of the Dnipro River
  • Luhansk: Russia controls most of Luhansk region but faces continued Ukrainian resistance
Reality Check: Despite Russia's claims to have annexed these regions, Ukrainian forces still control "sizable portions of Donetsk, Zaporizhzhya, and Kherson," according to recent assessments.

Recent Territorial Changes

According to the Russia Matters Report Card for January 7, 2026, in the period of December 9, 2025–January 6, 2026, Russian forces gained 74 square miles of Ukrainian territory. However, this incremental advance came at an enormous cost in casualties and equipment, and many claimed advances have not been verified by geolocated footage.

Territorial Disputes and Information Warfare

There have been numerous instances of Russian claims about territorial control that proved false or exaggerated:

  • Russian sources claimed seizure of Andriivka (north of Sumy City) as of December 27, but by January 7, a Russian milblogger claimed elements of the 810th Separate Naval Infantry Brigade seized it again—indicating Russian forces likely lost positions in the interim
  • Russia claimed seizure of Mayske in late September 2025, but a Kremlin-affiliated milblogger acknowledged on January 10 that Russian forces only recently seized it
  • Competing claims about Virolyubivka, with one Russian milblogger claiming Russian forces seized it, another admitting they could not consolidate positions, and Ukrainian forces reportedly retaking it
Assessment Challenge: The prevalence of false or exaggerated claims about territorial control complicates accurate assessment. Independent verification through geolocated footage remains essential for reliable reporting.

Peace Negotiation Prospects

Diplomatic Initiatives

US President Donald Trump began his second term in January 2025 with brokering an end to the war high on his agenda. In recent months, Ukraine, its European backers, and the United States have drawn closer to agreement on a blueprint for peace. However, significant obstacles remain:

Key Obstacles to Agreement

  • Territorial Issues: Fundamental disagreements over the status of the Donbas region and other Russian-occupied territories remain unresolved
  • Security Guarantees: Russia's attitude about security guarantees for Kyiv, particularly regarding the prospect of Western soldiers on the ground in Ukraine, remains a major sticking point
  • Zaporizhzhya Nuclear Power Plant: Disagreements over control and status of the ZNPP continue to complicate negotiations
  • Ukrainian Position: President Zelensky has indicated progress on "bilateral security guarantees" and "trilateral" documents but notes that territorial issues and ZNPP control remain unresolved

Russia's Negotiating Stance

Amid reports of progress in talks between Ukraine and the West, Russia has signaled a harder line. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov stated Moscow would "toughen [its] negotiating position" after Russia accused Ukraine of launching a large-scale drone attack on one of Putin's residences (an accusation Kyiv denied and which Trump later echoed CIA conclusions in questioning).

President Putin used multiple high-profile appearances—a December 19 press conference, a New Year's address, and Orthodox Christmas remarks—to signal that Russia is focused on victory, not peace. He praised Russian soldiers as being on a "holy mission" and made no mention of peace initiatives.

Russian Position: According to Dara Massicot of the Carnegie Endowment, "Putin's default position remains continuing the war," with Russian commanders "committed to offensive operations against all four Ukrainian regions" that Russia claims in addition to Crimea—Luhansk, Donetsk, Zaporizhzhya, and Kherson—in 2026.

Expert Assessments on 2026 Prospects

Analysts普遍 agree that the war is unlikely to end in 2026:

  • Ruth Deyermond (King's College London): "None of the conditions for a final resolution of the conflict are in place. Neither Ukraine nor Russia seems to be in a position to achieve a conclusive victory on the battlefield and, though both are under significant pressure, neither appears likely to collapse."
  • Mark Cancian (CSIS): "It appears that the US, Ukraine, and Europe can find common ground, but Russia is not willing to make concessions. Their stated goals are totally unacceptable to any of the other parties. Their intransigence stems from their belief that they are winning."
  • Olga Oliker (Crisis Group): "The war in Ukraine is unlikely to end in a sustainable peace in 2026." She notes that one possibility is pressure on Kyiv to accept Russian terms, "amounting to an effective surrender," which would create "a tense situation" that "may yet spark more violent conflict."

Scenarios for 2026

Several potential trajectories exist:

  • Continued High-Intensity Conflict: The war could "continue into its fifth year, with limited territorial gains, high casualties, and continuing missile and drone strikes throughout Ukraine and on key targets in Russia"
  • Temporary Ceasefire/Frozen Conflict: Deyermond suggests the war "may become a temporarily frozen conflict if a cease-fire is agreed," but notes that "both parties think their most fundamental interests are at stake," making negotiated settlement unlikely
  • Ukrainian Concessions: Pressure from the Trump administration could force Kyiv to accept Russian terms, though this would likely create instability and potential for renewed conflict
Core Conflict: For Ukraine, Russia's occupation "is an existential threat" eroding Ukrainian independence and national identity. For Russia, "Putin's presidency is now so tied to the war and to the project to subjugate Ukraine that no de-occupation or withdrawal of demands...seems possible until Russia has a new president."

International Support and Developments

European Security Commitments

European countries have stepped forward with concrete commitments to Ukraine's long-term security:

  • Sweden: Prime Minister Ulf Kristersson announced Sweden is ready to provide Ukraine with Gripen fighter jets and assist with Black Sea demining after the war ends
  • France and UK: Both countries have committed to deploying troops to Ukraine if a ceasefire agreement is reached, demonstrating Europe's evolving security commitment to Kyiv
  • Spain: Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez indicated Spain's willingness to participate in a peace mission in Ukraine
  • Lithuania: Has expressed support for deploying troops to Ukraine as part of post-war security guarantees

United Kingdom Accelerates Support

The UK responded to the Oreshnik missile strike by accelerating £200 million of funding for deployment to Ukraine and stepping up air defense support. This rapid response underscores Western commitment to bolstering Ukraine's defensive capabilities against Russian escalation.

Bilateral and Trilateral Frameworks

President Zelensky indicated progress in developing "bilateral security guarantees" and "trilateral" documents, possibly referring to:

  • US-Ukrainian bilateral security agreements
  • US-Ukraine-Russia trilateral peace frameworks
  • European security guarantee mechanisms

However, Zelensky noted that Ukraine and the United States still need to work out territorial issues and control over the Zaporizhzhya Nuclear Power Plant, suggesting these agreements remain incomplete.

International Pressure and Diplomatic Dynamics

The coming weeks could bring more clarity "as peace negotiations settle out," according to Mark Cancian. The fundamental question is whether Russia will make any concessions on key issues. So far, Moscow has shown little willingness to compromise, instead signaling a harder negotiating position.

Diplomatic Reality: While Ukraine and its Western backers have made progress on peace frameworks, Russia's refusal to make concessions on territory, security guarantees, and the ZNPP remains the primary obstacle to agreement.

Conclusion and Outlook

As January 2026 begins, the Russia-Ukraine war shows no signs of imminent resolution. Russian forces continue grinding offensive operations, making incremental territorial gains at enormous human and material cost. The deployment of the Oreshnik missile system represents strategic escalation even as tactical advances remain marginal.

Ukraine continues to demonstrate resilience and capability, conducting effective long-range strikes against Russian military and energy infrastructure while defending multiple front lines against persistent Russian assaults. However, Ukraine faces significant challenges, particularly regarding manpower and sustaining Western support.

The diplomatic track remains active but fraught with obstacles. While the United States and European countries have demonstrated continued commitment to Ukraine's security, Russia's unwillingness to make concessions on fundamental issues—territory, security guarantees, and control of occupied nuclear facilities—makes a sustainable peace agreement unlikely in 2026.

Most experts expect the conflict to continue throughout 2026, with possibilities ranging from sustained high-intensity warfare to a temporary ceasefire that freezes current positions without resolving core issues. The war's trajectory will depend on multiple factors, including:

  • Russia's ability to sustain casualties and economic pressure
  • Ukraine's capacity to maintain manpower and Western support
  • The effectiveness of Ukrainian long-range strike capabilities
  • Western unity and commitment to Ukraine's defense
  • Internal political dynamics in Moscow, Kyiv, Washington, and European capitals
Final Assessment: With both sides believing that continuing the war could improve their negotiating positions in 2026, and with fundamental issues unresolved, the conflict appears destined to grind on through its fifth year. The costs—already exceeding 1.5 million casualties and massive destruction of infrastructure—will continue to mount, making urgency for a diplomatic solution all the more acute even as the prospects for one remain dim.
