Russia-Ukraine War: Comprehensive Assessment January 2026

Report Date: January 6, 2026

War Duration: Day 1,410+ of Full-Scale Invasion

Conflict Start: February 24, 2022 (full-scale invasion)

Current Phase: attritional warfare with Russian territorial advances

Executive Summary

As January 2026 begins, Russia has achieved its largest territorial gains since 2022, capturing over 5,600 square kilometers in 2025 alone. Despite peace talks claiming to be "90% complete," Ukraine faces severe military pressure, with Russian forces advancing on multiple fronts. Western support continues but with growing political constraints, while Ukrainian society remains resilient but exhausted. The conflict shows no clear path to resolution in the near term.

1. Current Frontline Situation and Territorial Changes

5,600+ km²
Russian territorial gains in 2025 - largest since 2022

Russia's Biggest Advances Since the War's First Year

According to analysis from the Institute for the Study of War and reported by Novaya Gazeta Europe, Russian forces made their most significant territorial advances in 2025 since the early months of the full-scale invasion in 2022. The key statistics include:

  • 5,600+ square kilometers captured in 2025 (nearly 1% of Ukraine's territory)
  • This exceeds the combined territorial gains of 2023 and 2024
  • November 2025: Most successful month with over 700 km² captured
  • December 2025: Only 244 km² seized despite capturing three key Donbas cities
  • Far below the 60,000 km² seized in the first months of 2022
Current Hotspots (January 2026):
  • Pokrovsk Direction: Russian force grouping of approximately 150,000 personnel conducting small infantry group attacks (1-4 soldiers)
  • Kostyantynivka: Russian forces advancing along Pravoberezhna Street and consolidating in southeastern outskirts
  • Kupyansk: Ukrainian forces maintaining positions despite Russian claims; house-to-house clearing operations ongoing
  • Hulyaipole: Both Russian and Ukrainian forces operating within the town; no full control by either side
  • Zaporizhzhia: Russian forces bypassed Stepnohirsk, reaching Ukrainian defensive lines along the Konka River

2. Military Situation in January 2026

Day 1,410+ Key Developments

January 1-2, 2026

Russian New Year's attacks targeted Ukrainian infrastructure including drones striking hospitals, energy facilities, and residential buildings across multiple regions including Kyiv, Odesa, Volyn, and Zaporizhzhia.

January 2, 2026

Ukrainian State Border Service reported reduced Russian activity in Sumy direction, with attacks limited to small infantry groups of 2-3 soldiers due to snowy weather degrading drone capabilities.

January 3, 2026

Major escalation in Kharkiv with Russian strikes hitting city center, damaging trade center, hospital, and residential buildings. Initial reports of 7 injured, later updated to 30+ wounded.

Ongoing Tactical Patterns

Russian forces shifted from large-scale assaults to small infantry infiltration tactics (1-4 soldiers) using forest belts and basements for movement, with heavy reliance on FPV drones and electronic warfare.

Russian Tactical Adaptations

  • Small Group Infiltration: Attacks conducted by 1-4 soldier groups attempting to move between basements and forested areas
  • Reduced Heavy Equipment: Forces in Vovchansk stopped using heavy equipment, relying exclusively on small infantry assaults
  • Weather Adaptation: Snow conditions degrading Russian drone capabilities, but also making Russian troops more visible to Ukrainian reconnaissance
  • Constant Replenishment: Russian units continuously receiving personnel replacements despite heavy casualties
  • Drone Warfare: "Kill zones" extending 5-20 km on both sides of frontlines due to heavy drone presence

3. Peace Talks and Diplomatic Efforts

Zelensky's "10% Away from Peace" Statement

In his New Year's Eve address, President Volodymyr Zelensky announced that a peace agreement was "90% ready," but added crucially: "Those 10% contain, in fact, everything." This statement reflects both progress in negotiations and the intractable issues remaining.

The Peace Framework

  • Original U.S.-backed Plan: 28-point proposal including Ukrainian withdrawal from Donbas, military size caps, and suspension of NATO ambitions
  • Ukrainian Rejection: Overwhelmingly rejected as "capitulation" by both soldiers and politicians
  • Revised Framework: 20-point plan presented by Zelensky on December 23, 2025, to be signed by Ukraine, Russia, U.S., and European leaders
  • Key Condition: Any agreement must include reliable security guarantees and avoid freezing the conflict on terms favorable to Moscow

Major Obstacles

  • Territorial Questions: Russian demands for formal recognition of occupied territories remain incompatible with Ukraine's public position
  • Security Guarantees: Ukraine seeks "Article 5-like" mutual defense commitment requiring U.S. congressional approval
  • Root Causes: Russian officials insist any agreement must address "root causes" of the war
  • Trust Deficit: Ukraine and allies fear Russia would use any pretext to relaunch the war, as demonstrated by the alleged (and fabricated) drone attack on Putin's residence
Analysis from Guardian Report:

MP Serhiy Rakhmanin identified three necessary conditions for successful negotiations: (1) more systematic European military and financial support, (2) stabilization of the frontline to stop Russian advances, and (3) serious economic problems beginning for Russia. If even one factor doesn't work in Ukraine's favor, reaching a deal becomes extremely difficult.

  • 2025 Achievement: $5 billion in weapons supplies (targeting $1 billion monthly)
  • 2026 Projection: $12 billion anticipated for the coming year
  • Participation: Roughly two-thirds of NATO members contributing
  • Recent Contributors: Sweden ($275M), Norway ($146M), Denmark ($90M), Netherlands (€500M)

5. Manpower and Recruitment Challenges for Russia

Heavy Casualties:

According to President Zelensky's January 5 announcement, Ukraine eliminated more than 90,000 Russian troops over the past three months (October-December 2025). This represents an average of approximately 1,000 Russian casualties per day.

Russian Manpower Pressures

  • Continuous Replenishment Needed: Ukrainian commanders report Russian units constantly receiving personnel replacements despite high losses
  • Assault Group Depletion: Elements of Russian 121st and 122nd motorized rifle regiments unable to free surrounded assault groups in Kupyansk direction due to significant losses
  • Small-Group Tactics: Shift to 1-4 soldier infiltration tactics suggests manpower constraints or adaptation to Ukrainian defenses
  • Persistent Attacks Despite Losses: Russian forces continuing offensives across multiple fronts, indicating ability to absorb casualties

Recruitment Sources (The "Where Will They Come From?" Question)

The Moscow Times reported in early January 2026 that Russia faces ongoing challenges in finding men to fight in Ukraine, with:

  • Continued reliance on contract soldiers (kontraktniki)
  • Financial incentives insufficient to meet recruitment targets
  • Regional authorities struggling to fill quotas
  • Increasing dependence on irregular forces and volunteer battalions
  • Heavy use of Chechen units (Akhmat Spetsnaz) and other paramilitary formations
Order of Battle Insights (January 2, 2026):

Russian forces operating in Ukraine include units from multiple military districts: Southern Military District: 3rd, 8th, 51st Combined Armies; Central Military District: 2nd, 41st Combined Armies; Leningrad Military District: 6th Combined Army, 44th Army Corps; Eastern Military District: 5th, 35th Combined Armies. This diverse deployment suggests widespread mobilization across Russia's military structure.

6. Ukrainian Public Opinion on Peace Proposals

74%
of Ukrainians oppose peace plan involving withdrawal from Donbas and army cap without security guarantees

Key Poll Findings (Kyiv International Institute of Sociology, January 2, 2026)

  • 74% oppose peace plan requiring Ukrainian withdrawal from Donbas and military size caps without reliable security guarantees
  • 69% support a peace plan that would freeze the war WITH security guarantees, as long as Ukraine doesn't have to officially recognize Russian-occupied territories as Russian
  • Only 10% expect the war to end by the beginning of 2026
  • Only 16% believe it will end in the first half of 2026

Zelensky's Position

In his December 31 New Year address, Zelensky emphasized:

  • "A Budapest-style piece of paper will not satisfy Ukraine"
  • "Ukraine does not need a Minsk-style meticulously drafted trap"
  • "My signature will be under a strong agreement"
  • "We want the end of the war — not the end of Ukraine"

Soldier Sentiment

According to The Guardian, Ukrainian soldiers report exhaustion but continued determination: "When I'm at the front I feel OK, but when I come back home I have bad dreams, take antidepressants and so on" - Serhiy, Ukrainian soldier. However, he noted: "I think by this stage, probably the majority would be ready to go for a bad deal; anything to stop the fighting."

Psychological Impact:

For many Ukrainians, the new year has been the hardest psychologically since the war began. Hope of military victory that existed in 2023 has faded, replaced by grim determination and exhaustion. Yet 74% still oppose what they view as capitulation, demonstrating remarkable resilience.

7. Outlook for 2026 and Potential Scenarios

Best-Case Scenario

  • Ukrainian military and society continue holding out until Russia's situation worsens enough that the Kremlin accepts negotiations on terms that don't require Ukrainian capitulation
  • European support stabilizes and provides sufficient military and financial aid
  • Russia faces serious economic problems that pressure the Kremlin toward genuine negotiations
  • Frontline stabilizes, stopping Russian advances

Most-Likely Scenario

  • Continued Attritional Warfare: Russia maintains pressure, making gradual territorial gains while Ukraine defends with Western support
  • Diplomatic Stalemate: Peace talks continue but the "final 10%" proves insurmountable without major changes on the battlefield or in Moscow
  • Western Aid Fatigue: U.S. support remains unpredictable, Europe carries more of the burden but faces political pressures
  • Escalation Risks: Russian strikes on Ukrainian infrastructure continue, with potential for false-flag operations to justify escalation
  • Manpower Crisis Deepens: Both sides face recruitment and retention challenges, with Russia absorbing heavy casualties to maintain offensives

Worst-Case Scenario

  • Russian breakthrough in one or more sectors leads to collapse of Ukrainian defensive lines
  • Western support fractures politically, particularly if U.S. policy shifts dramatically
  • Ukraine forced into negotiations on unfavorable terms or faces military collapse
  • Conflict expands beyond Ukraine's borders, potentially drawing NATO directly into hostilities

Key Factors to Watch in 2026

  • Spring/Summer Russian Offensive: Will Russia launch a major new offensive once weather improves?
  • U.S. Political Dynamics: Will Trump administration maintain current levels of support or continue unpredictability?
  • European Unity: Can Europe maintain cohesion and funding levels, particularly with economic pressures at home?
  • Russian Economic Stability: Will sanctions, war costs, and demographic crisis begin to seriously impact Russia's capacity to wage war?
  • Ukrainian Mobilization: Can Ukraine continue to mobilize and train sufficient forces to maintain defensive lines?
  • Technology & Drone Warfare: Will either side achieve a breakthrough in electronic warfare, drone technology, or counter-drone systems that shifts the balance?
  • Black Sea Dynamics: Ukrainian strikes continue to degrade Russian naval operations, but can this advantage be maintained or expanded?

Political Considerations

  • Zelensky's Leadership: Appointment of Kyrylo Budanov as chief of staff signals potential new approach to security and negotiations
  • Election Question: Martial law prevents elections, but Trump and Russia question Zelensky's legitimacy; Valerii Zaluzhnyi waits in the wings as potential challenger
  • European Unity: Berlin talks in December 2025 showed "common sense of purpose" but territorial questions remain unresolved with "very long" road ahead

Strategic Assessment

The war in 2026 appears likely to continue as a grueling attritional conflict. Russia's willingness to accept high casualties and make gradual territorial advances, combined with Ukraine's determined resistance and Western support (despite political complications), suggests no rapid resolution. The "final 10%" of peace talks—territorial recognition, security guarantees, and enforcement mechanisms—remains intractable. The conflict's outcome may ultimately depend less on battlefield events than on political resilience in Kyiv, Moscow, Washington, and European capitals.
