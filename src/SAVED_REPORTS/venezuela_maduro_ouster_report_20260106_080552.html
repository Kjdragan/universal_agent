Venezuela After Maduro

A Comprehensive Analysis of the Post-Coup Crisis and Its Implications

Report Date: January 6, 2026 | Based on sources through January 5, 2026

Executive Summary

On January 3, 2026, the United States launched a unprecedented military operation in Venezuela, capturing President Nicolás Maduro and his wife Cilia Flores in a raid on their residence at Fuerte Tiuna military base in Caracas. The operation involved over 150 U.S. aircraft disabling Venezuelan air defenses and special operations forces extracting the Maduros to New York, where they face narco-terrorism charges. This report examines the military operation, political aftermath, humanitarian crisis, international reactions, and future scenarios for Venezuela and the region.

1. The Military Operation

Operation Overview

The U.S. military operation in the early hours of January 3, 2026, represents the most assertive American action to achieve regime change since the 2003 invasion of Iraq. According to U.S. officials, the operation involved more than 150 aircraft dispatched to disable Venezuelan air defenses, allowing entry for U.S. Special Operations forces who carried out the raid on Maduro's compound. The aircraft disabled Venezuelan S-300VM, Buk-M2, and Pantsir-S1 air defense systems—a sophisticated Russian-supplied network that U.S. forces successfully evaded through a combination of standoff effects, electronic attack, and compressed timelines.

Casualties and Damage

Preliminary assessments indicate significant casualties from the U.S. attack. Venezuelan officials report at least 80 people killed, including both civilians and members of security forces. The strikes targeted multiple locations across northern Venezuela, including Caracas, Miranda, Aragua, and La Guaira states. Images from Fuerte Tiuna, Venezuela's largest military complex, showed massive explosions and plumes of smoke rising from the installation.

Legal Justifications and Charges

The Trump administration has justified the operation as necessary to combat international drug trafficking, citing narco-terrorism charges against Maduro first unsealed in 2020. Upon arrival in New York, Maduro was indicted on multiple charges, including "Narco-Terrorism Conspiracy," "Cocaine Importation Conspiracy," and possession of machine guns. U.S. Attorney General Pam Bondi stated that Maduro and his wife would "soon face the full wrath of American justice on American soil in American courts."

"We are reasserting American power." — President Donald Trump, January 3, 2026

Constitutional and International Law Questions

The operation has raised profound legal questions. Under U.S. constitutional law, the executive branch maintains that presidents have authority to use military force without congressional authorization for actions not expected to result in "prolonged and substantial military engagements." Secretary of State Marco Rubio responded to questions about congressional authorization by stating, "This wasn't an invasion, we didn't occupy a country"—suggesting some broader campaigns might require legislative approval.

Under international law, numerous experts have condemned the operation as illegal. The UN Charter's Article 2(4) prohibits the threat or use of force against a state's territorial integrity or political independence, with exceptions only for self-defense and Security Council authorization. UN Special Rapporteur on human rights and counterterrorism Ben Saul called it "illegal aggression against Venezuela" and "illegal abduction" of a head of state. UN Secretary-General António Guterres expressed alarm over the escalation, noting that the military action "has potential worrying implications for the region" and warning that "these developments constitute a dangerous precedent."

2. Current Political Situation

Interim Government Under Delcy Rodríguez

Following Maduro's capture, Venezuela's Supreme Court ordered Vice President Delcy Rodríguez to assume the presidency as interim leader. Rodríguez, 56, is a longtime Maduro confidant who has served as vice president since 2018 and previously held positions including Minister of Foreign Affairs and Minister of Economy and Finance. The court ruled that Rodríguez should "assume and exercise, in an acting capacity, all the attributes, duties and powers inherent to the office of President" to guarantee administrative continuity.

However, the situation remains deeply ambiguous. While Trump initially stated that Rodríguez was "willing to do what we think is necessary," she appeared on state television flanked by other senior regime officials—including her brother Jorge Rodríguez (President of the National Assembly), Interior Minister Diosdado Cabello, and Defense Minister Vladimir Padrino López—and insisted that Maduro remains Venezuela's "only president." This joint appearance indicated that the core power structure supporting Maduro remains united for now.

Trump's Contradictory Visions

The Trump administration has offered strikingly different visions for Venezuela's future. In a January 3 press conference, Trump explicitly stated that the United States was now "running" Venezuela and would do so until the country's oil infrastructure was rebuilt and until the U.S. could "safely hand Venezuela over to local authorities." He emphasized that U.S. oil companies would "go in, spend billions of dollars, fix the badly broken infrastructure" and that "we'll be selling large amounts of oil to other countries."

Yet Secretary of State Rubio offered a radically different interpretation on Sunday, stating that the U.S. would have no direct governing role in Venezuela. Instead, he said the U.S. would structure Venezuela's oil and counternarcotics policies by maintaining the U.S. blockade of sanctioned tankers carrying Venezuela's oil. Rubio emphasized that the U.S. was "running" the "direction" of Venezuela's future, not the country itself, and that Washington would use "leverage" including oil embargoes to ensure compliance.

Opposition Marginalized

Perhaps most strikingly, Trump has dismissed Venezuela's democratic opposition, which won what international observers say was a landslide victory in July 2024 elections. Opposition leader María Corina Machado, recently awarded the Nobel Peace Prize, and her stand-in candidate Edmundo González have been sidelined. Trump stated that Machado does not have the "respect" needed to run the country, despite her widespread support among Venezuelans. This stance has poured cold water on opposition hopes that Maduro's removal would lead to their return to power.

Key Power Players in Post-Maduro Venezuela:

  • Delcy Rodríguez - Interim President, Maduro's former vice president
  • Jorge Rodríguez - President of the National Assembly, Delcy's brother
  • Diosdado Cabello - Interior Minister, controls intelligence agencies and pro-government militias
  • Vladimir Padrino López - Defense Minister, controls the military

3. Humanitarian and Economic Crisis

America's Largest Peacetime Economic Collapse

Venezuela's economic collapse over the past decade represents the largest peacetime contraction recorded in modern history. According to Brookings Institution analysis, Venezuela's economy contracted roughly 80% over the last ten years. The country that was once relatively rich—endowed with beautiful beaches, fertile land, an educated population, and the world's largest oil reserves—has seen its basic infrastructure severely deteriorated. Electricity grids, water systems, and transportation networks are all in desperate need of repair.

Humanitarian Emergency

Nearly 8 million Venezuelans are in acute need of humanitarian assistance, according to Brookings experts. The crisis has produced one of the largest forced displacements in world history without a conventional war or internal armed conflict—approximately 8 million Venezuelans have fled the country, seeking refuge across dozens of nations, many now living in precarious conditions. Those remaining face severe food shortages, lack of medical supplies, and widespread poverty.

The Oil Industry in Ruins

Venezuela's national oil company, PDVSA, was once a well-run enterprise producing 3.2 million barrels per day in 2000. Today production stands at roughly 1 million barrels per day—a catastrophic decline resulting from mismanagement, lack of investment, and sanctions. The situation is particularly dire because Venezuela's vast reserves consist primarily of very heavy, sour crude that is expensive and complex to produce. Restoring production to peak levels would require years and billions of dollars in investment.

"Venezuela's economy contracted roughly 80 percent over the last decade, the largest peacetime collapse recorded in modern history." — Brookings Institution Analysis

Criminalized Security Apparatus

Venezuela's armed forces are described by analysts as "fractured, criminalized, and complicit—but not monolithic." The UN has found that both SEBIN (civilian intelligence) and DGCIM (military intelligence) have committed crimes against humanity as part of a state plan to crush dissent. Former detainees have described electric shocks, simulated drownings, and sexual abuse at DGCIM black sites. The military has as many as 2,000 generals and admirals—more than double the number in the United States—with senior officers controlling food distribution, raw materials, and PDVSA, while profiting from illicit trade.

4. International Reactions

United Nations Security Council Emergency Meeting

At Venezuela's request, the UN Security Council held an emergency meeting on January 5, 2026, to discuss the U.S. operation. Venezuela accused the United States of carrying out "brutal, unjustified, and unilateral armed attacks" violating Article 2(4) of the UN Charter and called on the Council to condemn the aggression and hold the U.S. accountable. The meeting included participation from Venezuela, Argentina, Brazil, Cuba, Mexico, and other regional countries under rule 37 of the Council's provisional rules.

Regional Responses

Latin American reactions have been deeply divided:

  • Condemning the operation: Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Mexico, Uruguay, and Spain issued a joint statement firmly rejecting the "military actions undertaken unilaterally in Venezuelan territory," calling them a contravention of international law and a "dangerous precedent for peace and regional security."
  • Supporting the operation: Chile's incoming far-right president has backed the move, while Argentina's conservative government has also expressed support.
  • Mixed positions: Some countries have tried to balance their responses, condemning both Maduro's authoritarianism and the U.S. intervention.

European and Global Reactions

European responses have been muted but critical. EU High Representative Kaja Kallas stressed the bloc's commitment to peaceful resolution and called for restraint, noting that while the EU has stated Maduro lacks legitimacy, "the principles of international law and the UN Charter must be respected." French Foreign Minister Jean-Noël Barrot delivered one of the strongest statements, condemning both Maduro's authoritarianism and the U.S. intervention as violating "the principle of non-resort to force that underpins international law."

Russia, China, and Iran have all condemned the U.S. action as a violation of sovereignty and international law. Poland's Prime Minister Donald Tusk, responding to Moscow's criticism, fired back with what appeared to be a reference to Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine: "Deep self-irony is so typical for the Russian sense of humor."

The "Trump Corollary" to the Monroe Doctrine

Many analysts see the Venezuela operation as the first concrete implementation of the "Trump Corollary" to the Monroe Doctrine, outlined in Trump's November 2025 National Security Strategy. This doctrine emphasizes U.S. dominance in the Western Hemisphere and willingness to use military force to secure American interests. Trump himself referenced this vision, stating that "US dominance in the Western Hemisphere will never be questioned again" and issuing warnings to other countries in the region, including Cuba and Colombia.

5. Future Scenarios and Challenges

Scenario 1: Negotiated Transition (Best Case)

In the best-case scenario, Maduro's arrest catalyzes elite defection and a negotiated handover of power to the legitimately elected opposition. Faced with legal exposure, sanctions, and loss of patronage, regime underlings could seek guarantees for safe passage, limited amnesty, or third-country exile in exchange for transferring authority to González and Machado. Such a negotiated transition would avert mass violence, stabilize institutions, and open a path toward economic recovery and international reintegration. However, this would require the Trump administration to support the democratic opposition rather than sidelining them—a prospect that currently seems unlikely.

Scenario 2: U.S. Protectorate with Chavista Leadership (Current Trajectory)

Based on Trump's statements and Rubio's clarifications, the most likely scenario appears to be a form of "guardianship" where the U.S. dictates Venezuela's political and economic direction through an interim government led by Maduro loyalists like Rodríguez, backed by the threat of renewed military action. The New York Times describes this as the U.S. dictating policy through "conditional off-ramps" while maintaining most of the existing Chavista power structure in place. This approach would prioritize U.S. interests—drug interdiction, oil access, migration control—over democratic transformation, potentially leaving Venezuela's failed institutions largely intact.

Scenario 3: Fragmentation and Civil Conflict (Worst Case)

The darkest scenario is that regime remnants reject negotiation and fragment, with different factions competing for power. Armed colectivos, criminalized military units, and narco-linked factions could wage asymmetric warfare, turning parts of the country into contested zones. This could prolong civilian suffering long after the regime's formal collapse, potentially requiring a large-scale U.S. military intervention that the Trump administration has shown little appetite for. As one Brookings expert noted, Venezuela is "twice the size of California with a population of nearly 30 million, comparable to that of Iraq in 2003"—and Iraq descended into brutal civil war even with a multi-division ground force and hundreds of thousands of troops.

Key Transition Challenges

Regardless of scenario, any stable transition faces three critical challenges:

1. Political Legitimacy: In 2024 elections, Venezuelans voted overwhelmingly for González—a result the Maduro regime rejected. Maintaining Rodríguez, a close Maduro ally, as president indefinitely would undermine the clearly expressed will of the Venezuelan people and likely prevent the reestablishment of rule of law essential for rebuilding.

2. Economic Stabilization: A credible stabilization requires much more than investments from a few U.S. companies. Venezuela needs an IMF-anchored package of over $50 billion over 18–24 months, combining balance-of-payments support, fiscal financing and debt restructuring, as well as parallel World Bank and Inter-American Development Bank programs. This must be paired with large-scale immediate humanitarian assistance.

3. Military Realignment: Venezuela's armed forces require immediate unity of command under civilian authority; conditional off-ramps—amnesty and reintegration for mid-level officers not implicated in atrocities—paired with targeted prosecutions for grave crimes and the dismantling of irregular armed groups. Justice must be real but sequenced to avoid creating more recruits for narco-gangs.

Long-Term Strategic Implications

The U.S. operation in Venezuela carries profound implications for the international order:

  • Dangerous Precedent: By using military force to remove a head of state without Security Council authorization, the U.S. has opened what UN officials call "Pandora's Box." This could inspire similar actions from other states, potentially leading to a world where might makes right in international affairs.
  • Erosion of International Law: The operation further undermines respect for international law, state sovereignty, and civilian protections. As one expert noted, "This may be the end of the rules-based international order as we have known it since World War II."
  • Regional Realignment: The operation signals a dramatic shift in U.S. policy toward Latin America, resuscitating the Monroe Doctrine not to promote democracy but to ensure U.S. access to natural and strategic resources. This could push countries toward closer ties with China and Russia.
  • Resource Competition: The explicit focus on Venezuela's oil reserves—Trump stated that U.S. companies would take control of oil infrastructure "for their own benefit"—signals a new era of resource-based interventionism that could have implications for other resource-rich nations.

Conclusion

The capture of Nicolás Maduro marks a decisive inflection point in Venezuelan history, but it is the beginning of a new crisis rather than the end of an old one. What follows will hinge less on Washington's next move than on the calculations of Venezuela's remaining power brokers, military commanders, intelligence chiefs, and political enablers who are now confronted with a stark choice: negotiate an orderly exit or risk annihilation alongside a collapsing system.

For the Venezuelan people who have fought nonviolently against a highly repressive regime for over two decades, the removal of Maduro offers hope but also profound uncertainty. The Trump administration's dismissal of the democratic opposition and focus on oil and drug interdiction rather than democratic transformation has raised serious concerns about whether Venezuela will finally achieve the free and fair elections that its citizens have sacrificed so much to secure.

As one Brookings expert starkly warned: "The grievous damage that Trump's illegal attack on Venezuela has inflicted on international law and multilateral cooperation is perhaps the least tangible but most consequential of outcomes." The world is now watching to see whether Venezuela will become a model for successful transition or a cautionary tale about the perils of foreign-imposed regime change.
