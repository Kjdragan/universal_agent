# Vision: Landing-Page Advertisement Videos

**From:** Multi-agent ideation session, 2026-05-20 (Houston)
**Concept seed:** Two advertisement videos for the landing page of a B2B-SaaS / AI-agents service targeting small/mid business owners, surfaced via an "Is this you?" mechanic. Operator leaned cartoon. Operator flagged anxieties about cartoon reading childish, AI-ad-about-AI trope fatigue, two-videos-versus-one efficacy, and "Is this you?" feeling generic.

---

## What This Is

This document is the source of truth for what to produce, in what order, and against what governing principle, for the landing-page advertisement-video deliverable. It synthesizes three idea briefs into one unified vision and takes positions on every load-bearing design decision. It is the document a producer, engineer, or contractor should be able to read alone and act on. The full brainstorming materials — the ideation graph capturing every dialogue round, Explorer's five research reports, both snapshots, and the three idea briefs — are preserved alongside this document for anyone who wants the process behind the conclusions.

The session ran a multi-agent ideation arc — Free Thinker, Grounder, Explorer, Writer, with the operator's stand-in as Arbiter — over thirteen substantive rounds. The session deliberately interrogated the seed before accepting it, and the seed survived only in part. This vision document is honest about what survived, what changed, and why.

---

## The Core Thesis

The seed asked two distinct cognitive questions of the visitor — "is this your pain?" and "could this be your life?" — and assumed they should be answered by two videos of the same kind, surfaced by the same routing mechanic. The session's central insight is that those two questions are **two different cognitive tasks** that require **two structurally distinct productions**, sharing one capture layer but producing materially different post-production work. The recognition audience (already feels the pain) is converted by **evidence**: real product, real data, real cursor, lo-fi because the thing itself is the proof. The discovery audience (doesn't yet know what's possible) is converted by **revelation-via-specificity** — one ultra-specific scenario, named precisely, with the operator's life visible *around* the agent's work, producing aspiration through observable cause-and-effect rather than explanation.

The Free Thinker named the discovery construct "time-travel diptych" (a Tuesday in 2026 vs a Tuesday in 2027, same owner, same desk, agent work appearing as ambient texture in the 2027 frame). The Grounder, writing five minutes later without having read the Free Thinker's message, named the same construct "revelation-via-specificity" from the opposite direction (cognitive task instead of style). Those crossings are the strongest signal of the session: when two reasoning systems arrive at the same answer from opposite starting points without seeing each other's work, the answer is durable. The construct exists; we have two names for it. The vision uses "time-travel diptych" when describing the shot language and "revelation-via-specificity" when describing the cognitive principle.

## The Governing Principle

**The brief inventory is gated by the operator's PRODUCT maturity, not their PRODUCTION stack.**

The operator has Remotion, Nano Banana Pro, Veo/Kling, and media-processing skills — that's the production stack, and it's strong. The session deliberately did not assume parallel strength in the product stack: it is not yet established that the operator has a deployable agent workflow a real customer is running today, with consented footage rights, and a believable 12-month deployment runway for that customer. Every video brief in this vision is gated by a sequential operator-capability fork that lets the deliverable degrade gracefully through real / internal-tool / honest-mock / defer-to-Lottie-bridge as product maturity is honestly assessed. Better to ship a working Lottie/SVG hero now (Explorer's evidence: +5% conversion lift over baseline; -7% for autoplay video) than to ship a fake-evidence video that flips both axes of risk at once.

Every feature, interaction, and piece of content in this vision should be measured against this principle: **does it require product maturity the operator doesn't yet credibly have, and if so, what's the honest bridge that ships now?** The bridge isn't a hedge. It's the deliverable when product maturity isn't there yet, and it shows the operator the path to upgrade.

## Three Moves

The session's three confirmed-interesting ideas are presented here as aspects of one unified vision. They are not three features to pick from; they are one product, one capture, three deliverables that work together.

### Move 1 — The Prove Video (Audience A: recognition)

*A 30-second muted-autoplay screen recording of the real product on real data, gated by a four-step operator-capability fork.*

This is the recognition deliverable. A buyer scrolling onto the page who already feels the operational pain — Tuesday-night reconciliation, the inbox at 4:47 PM, the customer email that hasn't been answered — sees their own working environment on screen inside three seconds. The lo-fi quality is the proof. The style decision dissolves into an evidence-type decision: screen recording is one form; so is Loom-style dashboard narration, a customer voicemail over a transcript, an email thread with the agent's reply highlighted, a CSV with a green-highlighted "auto" column. Whatever counts as evidence for the specific operator. The brief specifies *what counts as evidence per video*, not aesthetic.

The four-step capability fork — real / internal-tool / honest-mock / defer-to-Lottie-bridge — is sequential, not parallel. The operator walks it top-to-bottom and picks the first branch they can credibly meet. Step 3 (honest-mock) carries an explicit honesty floor: the page copy must not pretend synthetic data is real. Step 4 (defer-to-Lottie-bridge) is the honesty floor at the brief level: when the product isn't yet at evidence-grade maturity, the deliverable is a Lottie/SVG-animated hero (the +5% measured-lift pattern) until real footage exists. The Prove Video becomes a v2 target in that case, not a v1 deliverable.

Page treatment is locked: muted autoplay with visible "watch with sound" button (Treepodia: up to +100% views vs autoplay-with-sound), sub-1.5s LCP requirement (below this threshold the autoplay video penalty softens to neutral; above 2s the conversion curve breaks), first-5-seconds value-prop beat (Explorer: 19.4% drop after 10s, 44% after 60s, seconds 0–5 are decisive). 30-second target length. The optional FT-2 diptych composition layer is a v1.5 A/B variant unless the diptych shot is no harder to capture than the single frame; no left-frame counterfactual (FT killed FT-5 outright in R4 — a counterfactual is hypothetical, not evidence, and violates the very axis this brief is built on).

The cognitive lever is **trust**. The tense is **past**. The question this answers is **"does this actually work?"** This is not the same brief as Move 2; they answer different questions and require different post-productions.

### Move 2 — The Reveal Video (Audience B: discovery)

*A time-travel diptych — same owner, same desk, one year apart — that triggers aspiration through observable cause-and-effect, with three cuts of one capture: pain cut, discovery cut, dual frame.*

This is the discovery deliverable. A buyer who has never used an AI agent and doesn't yet know what's possible can't decode the Prove Video — a real agent reconciling QuickBooks looks like someone using QuickBooks to a viewer with no frame. They need revelation, not evidence. The construct: a left frame showing the owner's 2026 Tuesday (drowning in 47 small jobs nobody pays them to do), a right frame showing the same owner's 2027 Tuesday (closing the laptop, on the phone with their daughter, leaving early), with the agent's work happening as ambient texture in the 2027 frame — notifications sliding in, invoices getting paid, reviews getting answered, optionally signed *"Maya replied to a Yelp review"* or *"Carlos drafted the invoice"* per FT-3's absorbed naming convention. No voiceover. No music swell. No "imagine if." No "AI" or "agents" terminology in the frame. The product is the only AI signal.

Per FT-R4, this produces three cuts of one capture:
1. **Pain cut** — 2026 frame alone, ~6-second hook, click-through to dual frame.
2. **Discovery cut** — 2027 frame alone, ambient agent work as curiosity hook, click-through to dual frame.
3. **Dual frame** — full diptych side-by-side, "this happened in 12 months," post-click or scroll-revealed.

The production pipeline branches per the operator's longitudinal-capture viability:

- **Path A-bridge — Composited approximation. THE V1 RECOMMENDED SHIP.** Same diptych structure; the 2027 frame uses composited screen content (real product, real Slack, real invoices) over a same-day live-action shot. v1-shippable today. **Honesty floor:** page copy must frame this as future-state visualisation ("what your Tuesday could look like, this time next year"), not as documentary ("twelve months later" or "this happened in 12 months"). The 2026 capture for Path A-bridge is also Path A's eventual 2026 frame — see below.
- **Path A — Longitudinal capture. THE GRADUATION TARGET.** The version Path A-bridge becomes after 12 months of real customer deployment. Operator ships Path A-bridge as v1, captures the 2026 frame today with real customer consent (which doubles as Path A's eventual 2026 frame), then 12 months later re-shoots the same customer's 2027 frame for real, swaps it into the diptych, and earns the right to use longitudinal documentary copy. The graduation is a credibility upgrade, not a re-production. Path A is not a first-class alternative anchor; it is what Path A-bridge becomes.
- **Path B — Below-fold narrated walkthrough of Move 1's footage. THE RECOGNITION-DEEPENING — NOT a discovery-video.** Grounder's earlier counter-proposal. Longer (60–90s) operator-voiceover walkthrough preserving one coherent visual language across the page. **Path B is the right answer when Audience B is a minority of traffic. It is NOT a discovery-video; it is a recognition-deepening.** If the channel-mix diagnostic points to Audience B as dominant, Path B should NOT be selected — defer the second video entirely until Path A-bridge is feasible. Shipping Path B to a discovery-dominant audience produces a recognition-deepening for an audience that needs revelation: wrong job, not a smaller version of the right one.

Path selection runs a two-tier diagnostic: tier 1 analytics (channel breakdown × self-described AI-familiarity × search-query analysis) if traffic data exists; tier 2 channel-mix fallback heuristic for solo / pre-launch operators (partner referrals + warm intros + cold lists segmented on existing pain → Audience A dominant → ship Move 1 only; content marketing on broad-AI terms + paid search "what can AI do for my business" + cold outreach with no pain signal → Audience B dominant → ship Move 2; mixed → Move 2 first, because discovery visitors bouncing on inscrutable product-cam is expensive).

The cognitive lever is **aspiration**. The tense is **future**. The question this answers is **"what does my life look like if I have this?"** This is not the same brief as Move 1; the briefs share one capture at the footage layer but produce two distinct productions in post — annotations, named-notification labels, time-jump composition, voiceover beats, ambient-texture compositing are real production work, not editing.

### Move 3 — The Landing-Page UX Strategy

*Three structural decisions that surround the videos and replace the seed's "Is this you?" routing mechanic with invisible, audience-aware page architecture.*

This move is not a video; it is the page architecture that decides how the videos are surfaced. Three components:

**3a — The seed dissolves.** "Is this you?" survives as language and dies as mechanic. A one-line headline above Move 1 (or Move 2, depending on the v1 cut choice) carries the recognition intent. There is no quiz, no parallel autoplay, no branching narrative. The video answers the recognition question inside three seconds; a quiz would duplicate that work and add a click before the value-prop is visible (CXL/Hotjar: "the first interaction must be the conversion"; Explorer: zero direct A/B evidence for quiz-routed heroes on B2B SaaS landing pages).

**3b — Demoted UX-A: below-fold case-study router.** The seed's quiz energy survives at smaller scope as a below-fold "pick your pain" router pointing to written 60-second case studies. Lives below Move 1 (or Move 2's deepening if Path B ships). Critical: the router fails if there is no content for it to point at. Authoring 3–5 case studies (one per primary ICP segment) is a v1 prerequisite, not a v2 nicety.

**3c — UX-C: v1 fallback + v1.5 destination.** Server-side variant selection / invisible personalisation operates at two levels:

- **v1: channel-mix fallback heuristic as the routing mechanism.** No analytics; no measured baseline. The operator's go-to-market channel mix becomes the implicit routing signal. Recognition-skewed mix → ship Move 1 as the single cut. Discovery-skewed mix → ship Move 2 as the single cut. Mixed → Move 2 first.
- **v1.5: both cuts shipped, invisible UX-C routing live.** Once Moves 1 and 2 are both produced and a v1 baseline conversion exists, UX-C is promoted from fallback to destination. Server-side inference (IP + UTM + referrer + cookie) decides which cut the visitor sees. The visitor never sees a quiz; the page IS the personalisation; the mechanism is invisible. Promotion criteria: v1 single-cut meets or beats the Lottie/SVG +5% control baseline over 4–6 weeks AND the segmentation traffic mix justifies the inference effort (neither audience <15% of qualified traffic).

The Grounder named three risks that gate UX-C's v1.5 promotion explicitly:

1. **Latency.** Inference must happen at edge or before-first-paint, or the visitor sees the wrong cut briefly then flickers to the right one — worse than no personalisation.
2. **ICP inference quality.** IP → industry / firmographics is noisy. A confidence threshold is required; below it, serve the higher-volume cut as a generic fallback.
3. **No pre-launch testability.** UX-C cannot be A/B tested before there's enough traffic to populate the segments. Don't ship v1.5 against a hypothetical performance lift; ship it against a measured v1 baseline.

## How They Fit Together

The unified picture: **one capture, two productions, shared raw footage, surrounded by an invisible UX architecture that lets the right cut reach the right audience without a quiz, gated by an honest fork that lets the brief degrade gracefully when product maturity isn't yet at evidence-grade.**

The natural arc:

1. Operator answers Move 1's capability fork honestly. That answer cascades.
2. If step 1 (real) or step 2 (internal-tool): operator captures the raw footage with both Move 1's needs and Move 2's needs specified in advance — annotations, named-notification placements, time-jump composition opportunities flagged before the shoot, not after. The capture session is one production day; the post-production splits into two distinct workstreams.
3. While post-production runs, Move 3's v1 ships: the channel-mix heuristic picks which cut goes live first. The page launches with one cut, one headline, the below-fold case-study router (3b), and instrumentation.
4. The first 4–6 weeks measure against a Lottie/SVG control. UX-C v1.5 promotion criteria are checked.
5. If criteria met: the second cut ships, UX-C v1.5 routing goes live, the page becomes audience-aware without any visible mechanic.
6. If criteria missed: revisit Move 1's capability fork. The fix is upstream of UX, not in UX.

If step 4 (defer-to-Lottie-bridge): the page launches with a Lottie/SVG hero and Move 3 v1's below-fold case-study router. Moves 1 and 2 are tracked as v2 targets when product maturity reaches evidence-grade. The bridge isn't a failure; it's the right deliverable at the operator's actual stage today.

The meta-principle: **honesty about product maturity is what makes every claim downstream survivable.** Evidence cannot be faked into existence. Aspiration cannot be staged into truth. Both lose if the brief tries to shortcut the operator's actual stage.

## Boundaries

These are not limitations. They are identity decisions the session ratified.

- **This is NOT cartoon-styled illustration.** The operator's initial lean toward cartoon was the right intuition asked at the wrong altitude. The category that wins B2B in 2026 isn't called "cartoon" — it's called "illustrated motion design." But more importantly, the session's central conclusion is that the *style question dissolves entirely* once the deliverable is evidence-typed (Move 1) and revelation-typed (Move 2). Cartoon-pretending-to-be-real is the failure mode the operator named without being able to articulate. The Lottie/SVG bridge in Move 1's fork step 4 is illustrated motion design done right; it is not childish cartoon.
- **This is NOT a quiz mechanic.** Visible "Is this you?" routing — quiz, parallel autoplay, branching — is killed in Move 3a. The seed's "Is this you?" language survives prominently as the page headline. The mechanic does not. Routing is invisible in v1.5 (UX-C personalisation) and absent in v1 (single cut per channel-mix heuristic).
- **This is NOT an autoplay-with-sound video.** Treepodia / Unbounce / every reputable 2026 source: autoplay-with-sound is universally panned. The Prove Video and the Reveal Video's cuts are muted autoplay with a visible "watch with sound" play-icon affordance.
- **This is NOT the AI-ad-about-AI trope.** No glossy iridescent orbs, no chrome blobs, no smiling-professional-looking-at-laptop cinematic, no voiceover saying "imagine if," no synth-pad score, no "the future is here," no abstract motion graphics, no "[startup name] is a tool that [does thing]." The Prove Video sidesteps the trope by being evidence; the Reveal Video sidesteps it by being specific and showing the product without naming AI.
- **This is NOT a single deliverable.** The session deliberately rejected the operator's hypothesis that one well-targeted video would suffice. Two cognitive tasks require two productions. The Path B fallback in Move 2 (narrated walkthrough of Move 1's footage) is the closest the vision gets to single-deliverable economics, and it is explicitly the v1 fallback when audience-mix doesn't justify two cuts.
- **This is NOT a longitudinal-only Move 2.** Path A-bridge (composited future-state visualisation) is the v1 ship; Path A is the graduation target Path A-bridge becomes after 12 months of real customer deployment. The brief must not present Path A-bridge as longitudinal — that's the same produced-ad-cosplaying-leaked-artifact failure mode the Move 1 honest-mock branch avoids.
- **This is NOT "ship Path B if Path A isn't feasible."** Path B is a recognition-deepening, not a discovery-video. If channel mix is discovery-dominant and Path A-bridge isn't feasible today, **defer the second video entirely** until it is. Substituting Path B for Path A in a discovery mix ships the wrong job to the wrong audience.
- **This is NOT a "two cuts of one asset" production model.** Earlier session framing was corrected at convergence. The footage layer is shared (one capture); post-production is two distinct workstreams (two productions). Calling it "two cuts" understates the work the discovery cut requires and risks scoping the production economics incorrectly.

## Key Design Decisions

The session treated these as settled. They are firm ground that downstream requirements can build on without re-litigating.

- **The brief inventory has three deliverables, not two.** Move 1, Move 2, Move 3. Move 3 is its own strategic bet, not a vision-document assumption.
- **Performance-vs-evidence is audience-scoped, not universal.** Evidence is the conversion lever for Audience A. Revelation-via-specificity (= time-travel diptych) is the conversion lever for Audience B. Grounder's R3 walkback of evidence-only is the corrected version of their earlier Thread D claim and is what makes Moves 1 and 2 structurally distinct.
- **Production pipeline is one capture, two productions, shared raw footage.** Not "two cuts of one asset." Locked terminology across all artifacts.
- **The four-step capability fork is sequential.** Walked top-to-bottom; the operator picks the first branch they can credibly meet. Not a parallel buffet.
- **Honesty floor on Move 1's honest-mock and Move 2's Path A-bridge.** Copy must not pretend synthetic data is real or stage a same-day shoot as longitudinal. Either branch violates this and the brief's evidence axis collapses.
- **30-second target length for Move 1; 6 / 15–30 / 30–60 for Move 2's three cuts.** Explorer-research-grounded; non-negotiable for the first version.
- **Sub-1.5s LCP is a hard constraint.** Above 2s, the autoplay video pattern is net-negative against a plain image hero. Engineering ownership must be named explicitly.
- **Muted autoplay + visible "watch with sound" affordance.** Locked page treatment for any autoplay video on the page.
- **The case-study content for Move 3b is a v1 prerequisite, not v2.** Without 3–5 case studies pointed at by the router, the affordance is broken at launch.
- **UX-C ships as v1 channel-mix fallback heuristic, not v1 invisible-segmentation routing.** Promotion to v1.5 routing requires a measured v1 baseline that beats or meets the Lottie control, AND a traffic mix that justifies routing.

## Open Questions

These are the genuine tensions to carry forward. Each has enough context that someone encountering it for the first time understands why it's hard.

### 1. Operator-capability fork: which step today?

**The question.** Which of Move 1's four-step capability fork is the first branch the operator can credibly meet *today*? Real (consented paying-customer footage), internal-tool (operator's own data), honest-mock (synthetic-but-realistic demo dataset, labelled), or defer-to-Lottie-bridge (no evidence-grade footage available yet)?

**Why it's hard.** This is the load-bearing question for the entire vision. Every brief below this answer either flows naturally or collapses. The operator knows their product better than anyone; the session deliberately did not assume product maturity. The honest answer may be "step 4 today, but step 1 in six months once a customer is live." That answer is fine — Move 1 becomes a v2 target and Move 3 v1 ships with a Lottie/SVG bridge as the hero.

**What the session surfaced about it.** Grounder's R3 pushback elevated this from a brief footnote to a vision-level concern. The vision is now structured so the operator answers honestly without losing the brief. The bridge isn't a hedge; it's a stage-appropriate deliverable.

### 2. Move 2 path selection: A / A-bridge / B?

**The question.** Which Move 2 production path matches the operator's actual go-to-market reality?

**Why it's hard.** The decision combines two independent variables: longitudinal-capture viability (do you have a real customer running today, plus consent for a re-shoot in 12 months?) and traffic mix (is Audience B ≥15% of qualified traffic, or is it a long tail you're willing to defer?). The two-tier diagnostic codifies the decision rules but the operator's judgment in the middle band is still required.

**What the session surfaced about it.** Path A-bridge is the v1 ship for discovery-dominant or mixed channel mixes (most operators); Path A is the graduation target Path A-bridge becomes after 12 months of real customer deployment, not a separate alternative; Path B is the v1 ship when channel mix is recognition-dominated and the operator wants one production, not two. **Path B is the right call when recognition dominates, but it is NOT a fallback for Path A in a discovery-dominant mix** — substituting it ships a recognition-deepening to an audience that needs revelation. If channel mix is discovery-dominant and Path A-bridge isn't feasible today, defer the second video entirely until it is.

### 3. Sub-1.5s LCP engineering ownership.

**The question.** Who owns the page-load optimisation that turns Move 1's autoplay video from -7% (the median B2B SaaS result) to neutral-or-positive?

**Why it's hard.** Poster fallback, preload-as=video, encoding pass (H.264 baseline + VP9 fallback), CDN distribution, monitoring on real-world LCP. Without a named owner, this silently degrades to the negative case and the brief's whole premise is undermined by a constraint nobody chose to solve.

**What the session surfaced about it.** Explorer's research is unambiguous that the LCP curve breaks at 2s; Digital Applied's 2026 study found the video penalty disappears on sub-1.5s LCP pages. This is the engineering work that makes the brief work.

### 4. Path A's longitudinal customer pipeline.

**The question.** If the operator picks Path A, which real customer is the subject of the 2026 shoot, and how is consent secured for the 2027 re-shoot?

**Why it's hard.** The graduation from Path A-bridge to Path A depends entirely on the longitudinal-capture being real. If the customer churns, declines the re-shoot, or wants to be anonymised, the operator never graduates — Path A-bridge remains the deliverable. That's fine: Path A-bridge stands on its own as a v1 production. The graduation is an upside, not a fallback dependency. The session did not identify a specific candidate customer; the operator would.

**What the session surfaced about it.** Path A-bridge is the structural unblock: operators ship it as v1 with no Path A candidate in hand. If they later secure a real candidate and 12 months pass, they earn the upgrade. If they don't, Path A-bridge stays as the production. Either is honest; only false-longitudinal copy is dishonest.

### 5. v1.5 UX-C promotion timing.

**The question.** When is the operator's v1 baseline mature enough to justify building 20+ Remotion variants for invisible personalisation?

**Why it's hard.** Premature personalisation burns engineering time before validating the unit case. Late personalisation leaves conversion lift on the table. The promotion criteria — meet-or-beat Lottie control over 4–6 weeks, both audiences ≥15% of qualified traffic — are the session's best answer, but the operator may have a stronger or weaker risk appetite.

**What the session surfaced about it.** Grounder's three UX-C risks (latency, ICP inference quality, no pre-launch testability) are the reason v1.5 cannot ship at v1. The risks are mitigatable, but only against a real baseline.

### 6. Demoted UX-A case-study sourcing and ownership.

**The question.** Who authors the 3–5 case studies the below-fold router points at, and what is the maintenance cadence?

**Why it's hard.** Without content, the router is a broken affordance at launch. Without an owner, the content rots. Without a cadence, even a good v1 case-study set becomes stale within 6 months. The session deliberately did not produce the case studies; they are downstream work.

**What the session surfaced about it.** This is a v1 prerequisite, not a v2 nicety. Treat it as a deliverable of Move 3, not as a content marketing task.

### 7. Agent naming for the "Maya / Carlos" convention.

**The question.** Does the agent have a real product-level name (Maya replies to a Yelp review), or is the name a marketing artifact?

**Why it's hard.** If the product calls the agent "the assistant" but the video calls it "Maya," the page-product gap creates a small but real trust break the first time a visitor signs up and meets "the assistant." The fix is either to name the agent in the product, or to use generic placeholders in the video copy ("an agent" instead of "Maya"). Either is fine; the decision is a product-naming question, not a video question.

**What the session surfaced about it.** FT-3's "agent has a name" convention survived the absorption into Move 2 as the named-notification ambient-texture pattern. It's a strong narrative device but only if the product follows through.

### 8. Interval calibration — is 12 months the right time-jump?

**The question.** FT-R4 stated 12 months as the interval between Move 2's 2026 frame and 2027 frame. Is that the right interval for this specific product?

**Why it's hard.** 12 months matches the buyer's mental model of annual review and gives the agent's compounding effect time to be visible — works as a default. But the right interval depends on the operator's actual deployment-to-stable-value timeline. Products with multi-month onboarding may need 18 months to be visibly transformed. Products with immediate productive use may show enough change at 3 or 6 months that 12 months overshoots the credible delta. The interval is baked into headline copy, the customer-shoot scheduling for Path A graduation, and the trust the visitor places in the diptych — getting it wrong reads as either too-good-to-be-true (interval too short for claimed delta) or unconvincing (interval too long for visible change).

**What the session surfaced about it.** 12 months is the default to ship with, not a hard constraint. Operator should sanity-check against their actual product timeline before locking the Path A-bridge headline copy and the Path A customer-shoot scheduling. The vision document does not re-litigate the interval; the brief flags it for operator calibration.

## What Wasn't Explored

Territory the session identified but didn't enter. Noted so future work doesn't unknowingly reinvent what was already visible.

- **Paid distribution and social cuts.** The session scoped to the landing-page deliverable. 15-second social variants for Meta / LinkedIn / YouTube pre-roll were not designed. Re-cuts from the same raw footage layer are economically straightforward but not specified. This is the natural next phase after Move 1 (and Move 2's Path A-bridge if shipped) is live.
- **A/B test design specifics for the v1 hero.** The vision calls for measuring against a Lottie/SVG control over 4–6 weeks; the test design itself (sample size, conversion lift threshold, secondary metrics like watch-with-sound rate, scroll-depth, demoted-UX-A click-through) was not specified.
- **Move 3b case-study content authoring.** Move 3 names this as a v1 prerequisite but does not produce the case studies. The 3–5 ICP-segmented written deepenings the below-fold router points at are downstream content work.
- **Product-naming for the agent.** Move 2's "agent has a name" convention implies a naming decision for the product. The session did not produce names; it preserved the convention as an option.
- **Q3 2027 longitudinal customer pipeline.** Path A's defense relies on filming a real customer today and re-filming them 12 months later. Identifying the customer, scheduling both shoots, securing consent for both is downstream work not done in this session.
- **YouTube tutorial integration.** The repo has a "YouTube demo unification" deferred initiative (per project memory). The session did not engage with whether the landing-page videos relate to that pipeline. Worth a one-paragraph check after Move 1 ships.
- **The crossing-convergence moment as a methodological signal.** The FT-R3 and Grounder-R3 dual-arrival at the same answer from opposite directions is the strongest robustness signal of this session, and it happened essentially by accident — the dual-send protocol that catches it was instituted to fix an earlier durability gap, not to manufacture convergence. Whether this pattern can be deliberately structured in future ideation sessions is an interesting methodological question the session noticed but didn't answer.
- **The earlier "FT-1 + TT one-asset-two-cuts" framing.** At convergence, this framing was corrected to "one capture, two productions, shared raw footage." The earlier framing remains in some superseded brief skeletons in `briefs/superseded/` as part of the audit trail. Future readers who encounter the earlier framing in those files should know it was deliberately corrected, not abandoned.
