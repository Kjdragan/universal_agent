The Kitten and the Giant
========================

The cardboard box behind the dumpling shop had been Pip's whole world for six
weeks. It smelled of ginger and sesame oil, which was lovely, and it kept the
rain off her ears, which was essential. But it was not, by any honest measure, a
home.

Pip was small even for a kitten — a wisp of grey smoke with eyes like two green
marbles and a tail that curled into a question mark at the tip, as if she were
perpetually asking the universe: "What now?"

The universe, on a Tuesday in October, finally answered.

                              * * *

The woman's name was June. She wore rain boots with sunflowers on them even when
it wasn't raining, and she found Pip shivering behind the dumpster while taking
the recycling out from the apartment next door.

"Oh," June said, in the voice people use when their heart breaks a little. "Oh,
sweetheart."

Pip hissed. It was not a very impressive hiss. It sounded like someone letting
air out of a very small bicycle tire.

June scooped her up anyway, wrapped her in a flannel scarf, and carried her
inside.

The apartment was warm. It smelled like coffee and old books and something else —
something enormous and alive. Pip's ears flattened. Her question-mark tail went
straight as an exclamation point.

In the living room, on a dog bed the size of a life raft, lay Bruno.

Bruno was a Great Dane. He was the color of charcoal, with a white blaze on his
chest shaped like the state of Texas. He weighed one hundred and forty-seven
pounds. His paws were the size of Pip's entire body. When he yawned — which he
did now, upon seeing the new arrival — his mouth opened wide enough to fit a
cantaloupe inside.

Pip screamed.

It was a tiny, electric scream, the kind that could shatter a thimble. She
launched herself out of June's scarf, ricocheted off the bookshelf, knocked over
a succulent, and wedged herself behind the refrigerator in a gap so narrow that
even dust bunnies had trouble fitting.

Bruno looked at June. June looked at Bruno.

"Give her time," June said.

                              * * *

For three days, Pip lived behind the refrigerator.

June left food and water at the edge of the gap. Pip ate only when the apartment
was dark and she could hear Bruno's slow, thunderous snoring from the living
room — a sound like a diesel engine idling in a cathedral.

She watched him through the gap. He was terrifying. His tail alone could knock
over furniture. When he drank water, it sounded like someone pouring a bucket
into a lake. When he walked, the floorboards creaked in complaint.

But she also noticed other things.

She noticed that he moved slowly and carefully around the apartment, as if he
understood that he was too big for the space he occupied. She noticed that when
June dropped a glass in the kitchen and it shattered, Bruno flinched and tucked
his enormous head under a pillow. She noticed that he carried a stuffed rabbit
everywhere — a rabbit so old and loved that one ear was held on with safety pins
and its stuffing leaked from a seam in its belly.

On the third night, Bruno did something that changed everything.

He walked to the refrigerator gap, very slowly. Pip pressed herself flat against
the wall, every hair standing on end, her tiny heart hammering like a sewing
machine.

Bruno lowered his massive head to the floor. He set the stuffed rabbit down at
the edge of the gap. Then he backed away and lay down six feet from the
refrigerator, resting his chin on his paws.

He didn't look at her. He just waited.

Pip stared at the rabbit. It smelled like Bruno — like warm fur and peanut
butter and the lavender detergent June used on his bed. One of its button eyes
was missing. It was, without question, the most important thing Bruno owned.

And he had given it to her.

                              * * *

On the fourth morning, June found Pip asleep on top of the stuffed rabbit,
three inches from Bruno's nose.

She did not move them. She did not take a photo, though she wanted to. She just
stood in the kitchen doorway with her coffee and her sunflower boots and watched
two animals who had figured out something that people spend whole lifetimes
struggling with: that the biggest things in the world are sometimes the gentlest,
and that the smallest things in the world are sometimes the bravest, and that
the distance between terrified and safe is often just one stuffed rabbit.

                              * * *

Pip never went back behind the refrigerator.

Within a week, she had claimed Bruno's left ear as her personal bed — she would
curl up in the fold of it and purr so loudly that June could hear her from the
next room. Bruno would hold absolutely still, barely breathing, as if a
butterfly had landed on him and he didn't want to frighten it away.

Within a month, they had developed a routine. Bruno would carry Pip around the
apartment on his back, her tiny claws gripping his fur like a jockey on a
racehorse. She would groom the spot between his eyes that he couldn't reach. He
would let her eat from his bowl first, even though her entire meal was roughly
the size of one of his kibble pieces.

At the dog park, the other dogs gave Bruno a wide berth — he was, after all, the
size of a small pony. But Pip would ride on his shoulders like a parrot on a
pirate, her green eyes blazing, her question-mark tail held high. No dog dared
approach. She was his fierceness. He was her fortress.

When strangers asked June what breed her cat was, she would say: "She's a
rescue."

And when they asked if the cat and the dog got along, June would look at the
grey wisp of smoke curled in the crater of a sleeping giant, and she would say:

"They were made for each other. They just didn't know it yet."

                              * * *

Years later, when Bruno was old and slow and his muzzle had gone white, Pip
would still curl up in the fold of his ear. She was no longer a kitten — she was
a sleek, confident cat with a permanent purr and a tail that still curled into a
question mark, though by now the question had changed.

It was no longer "What now?"

It was "Isn't this wonderful?"

And the answer, every single day, was yes.
