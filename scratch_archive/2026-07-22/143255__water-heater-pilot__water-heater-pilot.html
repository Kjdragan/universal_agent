1. What you actually have

From the label in your photo, this is a Honeywell WV8840-series combination gas control valve + thermostat — one unit that does the thermostat, the pilot safety, and the main gas valve, with an electronic control board inside. Part marking 50032539-001, date code 0912.

Under Honeywell's usual YYWW scheme that date code reads as week 12 of 2009 — so the valve is around 17 years old. Worth confirming against the serial number on the heater's own rating plate, because it drives the economics at the end of this guide.

Reading your photo left to right along the bottom edge of the valve:

  • Piezo igniter terminal (left, lightning-bolt symbol) — the black wire runs down to the white igniter module and on to the spark electrode.
  • COM terminal (centre) — this is your thermopile, the thick braided lead. It's the part that generates the electricity.
  • Red + white pair (right) — the resettable thermal switch (ECO) on the burner assembly. It's wired in series, and if it's open the valve will not run.

This is a sealed-combustion (FVIR) heater — you cannot light it with a match. Modern heaters have a sealed chamber with a flame-arrestor screen and no match port. The piezo igniter is the only ignition source you have. If the igniter is dead, that gets fixed first; there's no workaround. Don't try to defeat the seal to get a flame in there.

How this thing decides to run

Worth understanding, because it explains nearly every failure: there is no electricity coming into this heater. The pilot flame heats the thermopile, the thermopile generates a few hundred millivolts, and that tiny current is what holds the gas valve open and powers the control board. It's self-powered.

So the chain is: spark → pilot lights → pilot heats thermopile → thermopile makes voltage → voltage holds the pilot valve open and runs the board → board opens the main burner when the thermostat calls. Any break in that chain gives you the same visible symptom — "it won't light" — which is why the status light matters so much.

2. Start here — the status light is the diagnosis

Don't start turning knobs. Your valve has a built-in diagnostic and it will tell you which half of the problem you have. The codes are printed right on the label in your photo:

FlashesMeansWhat it's telling you
0 / noneControl off, or pilot out No pilot flame, or the thermopile is making no usable power at all. This is where you are right now.
1Normal operation Pilot lit, thermopile healthy, control happy. This is the goal.
2Thermopile voltage low The single most common repairable fault. Weak thermopile, dirty pilot, unstable flame, or a loose COM connection. Cheap fix.
4High temperature shutdown Water got too hot. Turn the dial down; if it repeats, the control is misreading.
5Temperature sensor failure Valve is bad. Not repairable — replacement.
7Gas control or valve failure Valve is bad. Not repairable — replacement.
8Flame sense error Control can't confirm the flame. Try a full reset; often ends up being the valve.

The pattern is a burst of flashes, then a roughly 3-second pause, then the burst repeats. Count the flashes in one burst. Do this in a dark basement — the LED is dim and it's easy to miscount or miss it entirely.

The one test that splits the problem in half. Your label says: "If Pilot is lit, Status Light will briefly turn solid red when Knob is turned to OFF."

That red flash is powered entirely by the thermopile — there's no other power source. So if you can get the pilot burning even briefly, then turn the knob to OFF and watch:

  • Red flash appears → your thermopile is generating real power. The problem is downstream (control board, or the flame isn't holding).
  • Nothing at all → thermopile, its COM connection, or the thermal switch. That's the cheap end of the problem.

3. The correct lighting procedure (and the #1 mistake)

1 — Reset. Knob to OFF. Wait a full 5 minutes. Don't skip this; it's a safety step, not a formality.
2 — Set to pilot. Turn the knob to PILOT (it aligns with the ▲ mark at the bottom).
3 — Press and HOLD the knob down. This manually holds the pilot gas valve open. It only flows gas while you're physically holding it in.
4 — Click the igniter. Press the igniter button about once a second. Watch through the sight glass. It can genuinely take 10–30 clicks, especially if the heater has been off a while and there's air in the line.
5 — Keep holding for 60 seconds after it lights. ← This is the mistake almost everyone makes. The thermopile is a lump of metal that has to physically heat up before it makes enough voltage to hold the valve open on its own. Let go too early and the pilot dies every single time — which reads exactly like a broken part.
6 — Release only when the status light blinks. Ease off slowly. If the pilot dies the instant you release, go to symptom C.
7 — Turn to your temperature. Once stable, turn the knob to HOT or your preferred setting. A/B/C are progressively hotter; ~120 °F is the usual safe target.

If it's been more than a few days since gas flowed — or the gas was recently off for any reason — there's air in the pilot line. You may need to hold the knob down for 2–3 minutes of clicking before gas even reaches the pilot. Several rounds of this is normal and is not evidence of a broken part.

4. Find your symptom

A — No spark at all when you press the igniter check first

Kill the lights and look through the sight glass while pressing. You're looking for a bright blue-white spark jumping a small gap.

  • You hear a click but see no spark → it's the wire or the electrode, not the piezo. Follow the black igniter wire from the valve terminal down to the electrode: look for chafed insulation, a loose push-on connector, or corrosion. Reseat both ends firmly.
  • Bench test: pull the igniter wire off the electrode, hold the bare end about ¼" from bare metal on the valve body, press the button. If a spark jumps to ground there, the piezo and wire are fine and your electrode is bad. If nothing jumps anywhere, the piezo module is dead.
  • No click at all → the piezo mechanism itself has failed. Common and cheap.
  • Spark gap should be roughly ⅛" from the electrode tip to the pilot hood. Too wide and it won't jump; too close and it won't ignite reliably.
  • A dirty electrode can be cleaned with fine sandpaper or emery cloth.

B — Good spark, but the pilot never lights

Spark is fine, so this is a gas delivery problem. In order of likelihood:

  • Is gas actually on? Check the shutoff on the supply line to the heater, and confirm another gas appliance (stove, furnace) still works. If nothing gas-fired works, it's the supply, not the heater.
  • Air in the line — see the box above. Keep holding and clicking.
  • Blocked pilot orifice or tube — field reports put this at 40–50% of "spark but no flame" cases. Spider webs are the classic culprit (they're drawn to the gas odorant), along with dust and carbon. See cleaning.
  • Clogged air intake / flame arrestor at the base of the tank — starves the chamber of combustion air. Very common in a dusty basement, a laundry area, or with pets.

C — Pilot lights, but dies the moment you release the knob most likely

This is the classic presentation, and it points almost straight at the thermopile circuit — the pilot burns fine while you hold the valve open mechanically, and drops the instant it has to hold itself open electrically.

  1. First, rule out the easy one: hold it a full 60 seconds. Genuinely time it. A surprising share of "broken" heaters are people holding for 15 seconds.
  2. Check the pilot flame quality. It should be a crisp blue cone that visibly wraps the thermopile tip. A small, lazy, yellow, or flickering flame won't make enough voltage no matter how long you hold. A weak flame almost always means a partially blocked pilot orifice — that's a cleaning problem, not a parts problem.
  3. Reseat the COM connection at the valve. It should be snug. These back out over 17 years of heat cycling.
  4. Check the resettable thermal switch — the red/white pair. If it's tripped or failed open, nothing will hold. Test continuity across it (below).
  5. Then suspect the thermopile itself. After ~17 years this is a completely ordinary wear-out. A 2-flash status code confirms it outright.

D — Pilot stays lit, but the main burner never fires

  • Turn the temperature dial up and listen for the valve to click open.
  • A marginal thermopile does exactly this — enough voltage to hold the pilot, not enough to also pull in the main valve. Same fix as C.
  • Check the status code. 5 or 7 here means the valve is done.

5. The three cleanings, in order of payoff

Do these with the knob at OFF and, ideally, the gas shut off at the supply valve.

1. Air intake and flame arrestor highest payoff

At the base of the tank there's an air intake screen and, behind it, the flame arrestor. Lint, dust and pet hair clog it, the burner starves, the flame goes lazy, and the thermal switch eventually trips. Vacuum the intake filter and brush dust off the intake side of the arrestor — you're working through the openings at the base, not going inside the burner chamber. Doing this twice a year prevents most repeat outages.

2. Pilot tube and orifice

The most likely single cause of "spark but no flame". Clear the orifice with a fine wire or needle, work through the tube interior, and blow it out with compressed air. Don't ream or enlarge the orifice — you're dislodging debris, not drilling.

3. Thermopile tip

Soot and oxide on the tip insulate it from the flame and cut its output. Clean it gently with fine steel wool or emery cloth. Free, takes a minute, and occasionally fixes a 2-flash outright.

6. Multimeter tests — the real answer

If you have a multimeter (set to DC millivolts), these tests end the guessing. Everything above is inference; this is measurement.

TestHowHealthyReplace if
Thermopile, open circuit Disconnect the thermopile lead from COM. Light the pilot, hold it burning 60–90 s, measure across the thermopile leads. 650–850 mV Below ~400 mV — and definitely below 350 mV
Thermopile, under load Same, but connected to the valve with the pilot lit. 400–500 mV Below ~350 mV
Thermal switch Continuity/ohms across the red + white pair, disconnected from the valve. Closed circuit, near 0 Ω Open circuit = tripped or failed

How to read the result: a good open-circuit reading that collapses under load means the valve is drawing too much current — an internal fault, not a thermopile problem. A low reading both ways means the thermopile is simply worn out. Healthy on both, and your problem is elsewhere.

If the thermal switch reads open: replacing it without asking why is a mistake. That switch trips because the chamber overheated or was starved of air — a blocked intake, a blocked flue, or a venting problem. Fix the cause. A switch that trips repeatedly is the heater telling you something real about combustion, and that one is worth a professional.

8. The uncomfortable question: how old is this heater?

If that 0912 date code is right, the valve — and almost certainly the tank — dates to 2009. Typical tank life is 8–12 years. You're past it.

That doesn't mean don't fix it. It changes which fixes are worth doing:

  • A $30–80 thermopile or igniter: absolutely do it. Cheap, quick, and even a year or two more of service is a good return.
  • A $200–400 gas valve on a 17-year-old tank: think hard. That's a large fraction of a new heater, spent on a tank that could start leaking at any point — and when a tank goes, it goes as water on your floor, not as a warning.

While you're down there, look at the base of the tank for rust, mineral crust, or damp spots. Any weeping at the bottom seam means the tank is finished and no valve repair is worth doing.

9. When to stop and call someone

  • Any persistent gas smell. Not a judgement call — leave and phone from outside.
  • Status code 5 or 7. The valve is dead; this isn't a DIY part.
  • Anything requiring the gas line to be opened. Valve replacement means breaking a gas union and leak-testing it afterward. Not a first-time DIY job.
  • The thermal switch keeps tripping. That's a combustion or venting problem and it's a genuine safety issue — carbon monoxide territory.
  • Soot, scorching, or melted plastic anywhere near the burner.
