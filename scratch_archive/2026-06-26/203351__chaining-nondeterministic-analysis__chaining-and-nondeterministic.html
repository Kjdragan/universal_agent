Chaining the Loops + Non-Deterministic Adaptation

Can the four demos be composed into one staged pipeline — and can they be bent toward a subjective goal that has no clean scalar or pass/fail check?

Engineering analysis · grounded in the four repos' engines · 2026-06-26

Verdict: Yes to both. Chaining is sound if you first decide whether your facets are separable or coupled, and you make the post-merge verify mandatory. Pointing the loops at a non-deterministic goal is also viable — and the single highest-leverage change is to turn fan-out (#4) into the evaluator: a panel of judges that converts a noisy subjective signal into a stable comparator the hill-climb can trust.

Part A — Chaining #1, #2, #3, #4

First, the factual correction: what #1 does with "many facets"

The autoresearch engine does not pick one component or optimize pieces independently. It optimizes one artifact against one scalar, and the proposer walks the knobs by coordinate descent — one knob per step, keep if the single global score improved. A five-knob config is already a many-facet search; it keeps the best joint combination. Critically, the one global score sees the interactions between knobs, so coupled knobs are handled correctly — just sequentially. So #1 already covers "many knobs in one thing, one score." Fan-out buys something different.

Where #4 actually adds value over plain #1

Separable facets → decompose

Pieces with their own artifact and own score (5 independent service configs; 5 prompts each with its own eval). Run one autoresearch per facet in parallel, then the synthesizer assembles the winners. Embarrassingly parallel — valid only if the pieces don't interact.

Coupled facets → parallel restarts

One artifact, one global score (the common case). You cannot hill-climb facets in isolation and glue them — per-facet local optima ≠ global optimum. Use fan-out as a portfolio: N loops on the same artifact, different seeds/strategies, keep the best. Buys breadth against local minima, not decomposition.

The escalation ladder (cost-ordered)

One fix to the original idea: convergence-pipeline (#3) already is self-correcting-loop (#2) with an optimize stage in front. So "use #2, and if it can't pass escalate to #3" isn't a second repair pass — what #3 adds when #2 stalls is a better starting draft (Stage 1 hands the repairer an optimized seed). With that understood, the tiers are clean:

Tier 0 · cheapest, try first

self-correcting-loop alone

Pure pass/fail. One artifact, repair against the rubric for K rounds.

Tier 1 · it stalled — draft too weak

convergence-pipeline (#3)

The optimize stage produces a stronger seed; then verify closes the edge cases the score left open.

Tier 2 · still stuck, or big/separable

subagent-orchestration wraps Tier 1, run N-wide

Separable → one pipeline per facet. Coupled → parallel restarts of the same pipeline. Keep the branch that converges (or the best partial).

Start cheap, escalate only on stall. Each tier multiplies cost (Tier 2 by N), so you never pay for breadth until a narrow attempt has actually failed.

The robust shape — and why "verify at the end" is correct

fan-out→autoresearch ×N→synthesizer merge→verify on MERGED artifact
↑ if verify fails to converge → re-fan-out with the real failure fed back in
The merge is the dangerous step. When you fan optimization across facets, the synthesizer recombines optimized pieces — and if the facets were even slightly coupled, the merged artifact can score worse than any branch, or fail tests none of the branches failed alone. A naive fan-in can manufacture a regression. That is exactly why the executable verify (#2/#3 Stage 2) belongs at the very end, on the merged result — it's the only thing that catches a bad synthesis. The verify-fails → re-fan-out loop is what makes it "keep working until the finished state passes."

Part B — Adapting to a non-deterministic artifact or goal

All four loops, as shipped, assume a deterministic, comparable signal: autoresearch needs one reproducible scalar; self-correcting needs a runnable pass/fail rubric. But many real goals have neither — "make this brief more persuasive," "raise this agent's answer quality," "make this landing page convert better." There's no val_loss and no pytest. The good news: only the evaluator has to change. The loop machinery (keep/revert, ledger, round cap, the proposer) is already fine — the LLM proposer is supposed to be non-deterministic.

The core problem: a noisy judge breaks keep-if-better

Swap the deterministic evaluator for an LLM-as-judge and you inherit three failure modes: the judge gives different scores to the same artifact on different runs (variance); the proposer learns to flatter the judge rather than improve the artifact (reward hacking); and there is no absolute ground truth to anchor to. Keep/revert depends on a trustworthy comparison — so every adaptation below is really about making the judge stable enough to compare against.

Four adaptations, in priority order

ChangeWhat it doesWhy it works
1. Pairwise, not absoluteAsk "is candidate B better than current-best A?" instead of "score B from 0–10."LLM judges are far more reliable at a head-to-head preference than an absolute number. Maps directly onto autoresearch's keep/revert: keep B only if the judge prefers it to A.
2. Judge panel = use #4 as the evaluatorFan the evaluation out to N independent judges (different lenses/rubrics), aggregate by majority vote or median.This is the elegant combination. subagent-orchestration isn't just for generating candidates — it's the variance-reduction layer. A panel cuts single-judge noise the way an ensemble cuts model variance.
3. Variance-aware keep/revertReplace the simplicity_eps tie-band with a real noise margin: keep only if the panel prefers B by more than the judge's measured noise (estimate it by re-judging the same pair).Stops the loop "accepting" changes that are within the judge's own jitter — the #1 way a subjective hill-climb drifts sideways forever.
4. Rubric decompositionBreak "quality" into sub-criteria (clarity, accuracy, completeness, tone), score each, aggregate.Several narrow judgments are more stable and more auditable than one holistic number — and the ledger now records why each step was judged better.

Don't let the loop hill-climb the judge's biases

  • Hold-out / rotating judges. Optimize against one judge framing; accept with a different one (or a different rubric order). A train/val split for subjectivity — if a gain only shows up on the proposal judge, it's overfitting.
  • Anchor on whatever is deterministic. Even a "subjective" artifact usually has hard constraints: length, must-include facts, banned claims, valid format/schema. Run the self-correcting rubric on those, and the LLM-judge hill-climb on the soft quality. That is the convergence-pipeline shape applied to a non-deterministic goal: optimize soft quality, then verify hard constraints.
  • Human-in-the-loop as the periodic ground-truth anchor. Without any objective signal you can only ever reach "judged better," never "proven better." An occasional human spot-check on the ledger's kept rows recalibrates the judge and catches drift cheaply.

The combined architecture for a subjective goal

# Non-deterministic optimize-then-verify — the four repos, re-pointed
propose   (#1 LLMProposer)  diverse candidate, optionally #4 parallel restarts for breadth
   │
evaluate  (#4 fan-out AS judge)  N judges, pairwise vs current-best, different lenses
   │                          → aggregate vote/median  → a stabilized comparator
   │
keep/revert (#1 engine)  keep only if panel margin > noise floor; append to ledger
   │   ⟲  loop until the panel stops preferring new candidates (noise-tolerant plateau)
   ▼
verify   (#2 / #3 Stage 2)  run the DETERMINISTIC hard-constraint rubric on the winner
                              (length, facts present, format valid, nothing banned)

#1 supplies the loop + ledger   #4 supplies diversity and the judge panel   #2 guards the hard constraints   #3 is the overall optimize-then-verify frame.

Honest limits

  • Cost. A judge panel multiplies eval cost by the panel size every iteration. Pairwise (compare to current-best only) keeps it O(1) per step rather than O(N²) tournaments.
  • Fuzzy convergence. "Stops improving" becomes "the panel stops preferring new candidates" — you need a noise-tolerant plateau definition and patience, or the loop chases jitter.
  • No proof, only preference. The ceiling is "the panel and your rubric agree it's better." For anything where being wrong is expensive, keep the deterministic verify stage as the final word and a human on the recalibration loop.
