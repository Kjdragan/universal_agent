Russia-Ukraine War Report

Analysis of Military Developments, Diplomatic Initiatives, and Human Impact

Coverage Period: December 18-25, 2025

Executive Summary

As Christmas 2025 approaches, the Russia-Ukraine war enters its 1,400th day with simultaneous developments on three fronts: ongoing Russian territorial advances in eastern Ukraine, intensive U.S.-led diplomatic efforts to broker a peace deal, and continued devastating attacks on civilian infrastructure. A new 20-point peace plan has emerged from U.S.-Ukrainian negotiations, featuring potential compromises on territory that President Zelenskyy describes as "either this or war" for the Ukrainian people.

Territorial and Military Situation

Russian Advances Continue

According to Russia Matters' analysis of Institute for the Study of War (ISW) data, Russian forces have maintained steady territorial gains throughout December 2025. Between November 25 and December 22, Russia gained 138 square miles of Ukrainian territory. In just the past six days (December 16-22), Russian forces captured an additional 51 square miles—approximately half the size of Nantucket Island.

Since January 1, 2025, Russia has averaged 176 square miles of territorial gains per month. As of December 22, 2025, Russian forces occupied approximately 116,057 square kilometers (44,810 square miles) of Ukrainian land, constituting 19.2% of Ukrainian territory—an area roughly equivalent to the state of Ohio.

Daily Attacks and Casualties

Russian forces launched approximately 1,300 drones, nearly 1,200 guided aerial bombs, and nine missiles towards Ukraine over a single week in mid-December, according to President Zelenskyy. The Zaporizhia region alone was shelled nearly 5,000 times in one week, wounding 60 people and damaging hundreds of buildings, according to regional Governor Ivan Fedorov.

New Peace Plan: The 20-Point Proposal

Zelenskyy's Territorial Compromise

In a significant development, President Volodymyr Zelenskyy has revealed details of a 20-point peace plan agreed upon by U.S. and Ukrainian negotiators. The proposal includes a potential compromise on one of Russia's core demands—territory in eastern Ukraine's Donbas region.

"We consider a free economic zone a potential option for a sovereign state to choose... Only a referendum can determine whether people agree to such a path if the proposal for Ukraine is precisely this, either this or war."
— President Volodymyr Zelenskyy, December 2025

Key Elements of the Proposal

  • Demilitarized Economic Zone: The portion of Donetsk that Ukraine still holds would become a "free economic zone" where Ukrainian military forces withdraw
  • Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant: The U.S. proposal calls for joint management by the U.S., Ukraine, and Russia—a arrangement Zelenskyy rejects as "very inappropriate"
  • Referendum Requirement: Any peace plan would require approval by a Ukrainian referendum with only voters in Ukrainian-controlled territory participating
  • International Forces: International peacekeeping forces would monitor the demilitarized zone
  • NATO Bid Maintained: Ukraine would not abandon its constitutional aim to join NATO
Zelenskyy's Counter-Proposal on Zaporizhzhia: Russia must withdraw from Europe's largest nuclear power plant, after which Ukraine and the U.S. would split operations 50/50—even if the U.S. chooses to transfer some of its portion to Russia.

Referendum Conditions

Zelenskyy specified four conditions for any referendum on the peace plan:

  1. A ceasefire first, followed by 60 days to prepare the referendum
  2. Ukrainian police would remain in the proposed economic zone
  3. Only Ukrainians living in Ukrainian territory could vote (excluding those under occupation)
  4. International forces must be present on the ground to guarantee enforcement

Diplomatic Activity and Negotiations

Miami Peace Talks

Intensive diplomatic activity has centered on Miami, Florida, where U.S. envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner have held separate meetings with both Ukrainian and Russian delegations. The talks, which began December 19 and continued through the weekend, represent the most serious push for a negotiated settlement since the full-scale invasion began in February 2022.

U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio characterized the progress: "I think we've made progress, but we have a ways to go, and obviously, the hardest issues are always the last issues."

Reactions from Key Players

  • Russia: Yury Ushakov, Putin's top foreign policy aide, criticized modifications made by European countries and Ukraine to the U.S. proposals, stating they "do not improve the document and do not improve the possibility of achieving long-term peace." Russia has given no indication it will agree to withdraw from seized territories and continues to demand Ukraine relinquish remaining territory in Donbas.
  • United States: Witkoff reported "productive and constructive meetings" with both Ukrainian and Russian delegations, focusing on a "shared strategic approach between Ukraine, the United States and Europe."
  • France: President Emmanuel Macron discussed the situation with NATO chief Mark Rutte and announced that starting in January 2026, Paris will continue work on providing Ukraine with "solid security guarantees, a prerequisite for a robust and lasting peace."

European Union Financial Support

EU leaders have agreed to provide an interest-free loan to Ukraine to meet military and economic needs for 2026-2027. Rather than using frozen Russian assets directly, the EU decided to borrow cash on capital markets—a compromise that avoided what Belgian Prime Minister Bart De Wever described as potential "chaos and division."

Aid Shortfall Warning: President Zelenskyy stated that Ukraine faces a foreign aid shortfall of 45-50 billion euros ($53-$59 billion) in 2026. If Kyiv does not receive a first tranche of a loan secured by Russian assets by spring, it would have to cut drone production.

Civilian Impact and Infrastructure Attacks

Energy Infrastructure Under Siege

Ukraine's energy infrastructure continues to suffer devastating attacks. As of December 2025, Ukraine has lost approximately 48% (27 gigawatts) of its pre-war installed capacity of 56.1 GW. Key statistics include:

  • About 64% of electricity generating capacity destroyed or occupied as of 2024
  • Some of Ukraine's thermal power generation was destroyed as of May 2025
  • Some of all hydropower installations were damaged and destroyed as of May 2025
  • Ukraine's energy infrastructure was operating at only a fraction of its pre-invasion generation capacity as of Fall 2025
  • Russia destroyed a significant portion of Ukraine's gas production ahead of winter 2025-2026

Odesa: A City Under Pressure

The Black Sea port city of Odesa has faced particularly heavy attacks. On December 20, Russian attacks targeting Odesa's ports killed seven people and wounded 15, according to regional Governor Oleh Kiper. The city has suffered chronic power outages since December 13, with residents facing up to 16 hours a day without electricity.

In an environmental incident, a sunflower oil spill caused by Russian aerial bombardments contaminated the shoreline around Odesa, killing wildlife and triggering warnings from conservationists. The Pivdenny port was temporarily closed to assist with cleanup operations.

Civilian Casualties

Civilian casualties continue to mount across Ukraine:

  • December 18: Russian strikes near Odesa killed a woman in her car; three children were injured
  • December 19: A Russian attack on Kharkiv region killed a 49-year-old man and 42-year-old woman in the village of Izyum
  • December 19-20: One person killed in Donetsk region, one in Zaporizhia region
  • December 20: Russian attacks on Odesa killed seven and wounded 15

Attacks on Russian Territory

Ukrainian Drone Operations

Ukraine has continued striking targets inside Russia, particularly focusing on energy infrastructure and military sites:

  • Rostov-on-Don and Bataysk: Overnight Ukrainian drone attacks killed three people, including two crew members of a cargo vessel
  • Yefremov Synthetic Rubber Plant (Tula Region): Ukrainian drones struck the facility, with debris from a downed drone igniting a fire
  • Marine Drone Storage (Crimea): Ukrainian drones struck a storage facility for marine drones in Russian-occupied territory
  • Moscow: Russian air defence units downed 16 Ukrainian drones en route to Moscow over approximately 17 hours. Two of four major Moscow airports temporarily limited operations due to the attacks

Internal Security Incidents

An explosion in Moscow killed three people, including two police officers, just days after a car bomb killed a high-ranking Russian general in the same area. A Ukrainian military intelligence (GUR) official told the Associated Press that the attack was part of a Ukrainian operation targeting police officers for participation in Russia's war in Ukraine.

Energy Infrastructure Impact: Ukrainian strikes on Russia's energy sector have caused at least 60 billion rubles in damage according to RFE/RL. As of early October 2025, Ukrainian drone strikes had forced nearly 40% of Russia's oil refining capacity offline.

International Developments

New Russian Military Capabilities

Russia has formed a military brigade equipped with its new Oreshnik hypersonic missile, according to Chief of the General Staff Valery Gerasimov. Russia first fired the Oreshnik at Ukraine in November 2024, and President Vladimir Putin has boasted that the missile is impossible to intercept and has destructive power comparable to that of a nuclear weapon.

Foreign Fighters and Legal Cases

  • Hayden Davies: A 30-year-old British man who fought for Ukraine was sentenced to 13 years in a maximum security prison camp after being convicted of being a paid mercenary by a Russian court in occupied Donetsk
  • Oscar Mauricio Blanco Lopez: A 42-year-old Colombian man was sentenced to 19 years in prison by a Russian-backed court in occupied Ukraine for fighting with Ukraine's army
  • Sergei Yeremeyev: A Belarusian man accused by Russia of blowing up two trains in Siberia for Ukraine was jailed for 22 years
  • Indian Nationals: India's Ministry of External Affairs said 202 Indian nationals were recruited into Russian armed forces, with 26 killed and seven missing

Economic Sanctions and Enforcement

Britain imposed new sanctions targeting Russian oil companies, including 24 individuals and entities, focusing on Russia's largest remaining unsanctioned oil companies: Tatneft, Russneft, NNK-Oil and Rusneftegaz.

Sweden's customs service boarded a Russian freighter anchored in Swedish waters after it developed engine problems. The vessel's owners are on the EU's sanctions list.

Debt Restructuring

Ukraine completed a deal to restructure $2.6 billion of growth-linked debt, with creditors overwhelmingly accepting a bonds-and-cash swap offer—a key step for the country to emerge from the sovereign default it suffered after Russia's full-scale invasion.

Public Opinion and Political Developments

Russian Public Expectations

A majority of Russians expect the war in Ukraine to end in 2026, according to Russia's state pollster VTsIOM. In the year-end presentation of 1,600 respondents, 70% saw 2026 as a more "successful" year for Russia than 2025, while for 55%, that hope was linked to a possible end to Russia's war in Ukraine.

Zelenskyy's Christmas Address

In his Christmas address, President Zelenskyy acknowledged marking the holiday at a "difficult" time but emphasized the nation's unity: "Ukrainians are together tonight... It hardly matters what dishes are on the table – what matters is who is at the table."

Putin's Annual Address

During his annual "results of the year" speech in Moscow, President Putin criticized Zelenskyy for refusing to discuss giving up Ukrainian territory, saying, "We know from statements from Zelenskyy that he's not prepared to discuss territory issues."

Putin also attacked Europe's handling of frozen Russian assets, labeling plans to use them to fund Ukraine as "robbery" rather than theft because it was being done openly. "Whatever they stole, they'll have to give it back someday," he said, pledging continued legal action.

Key Challenges Ahead

  • Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant: Disagreement over control of Europe's largest nuclear facility remains a major obstacle. The U.S. proposal for joint U.S.-Ukraine-Russia management is unacceptable to Ukraine
  • Donbas Territory: Russia controls most of Luhansk and about 70% of Donetsk and continues to demand Ukraine relinquish remaining territory in the region
  • International Forces: Russia has repeatedly rejected the presence of foreign troops on the front line, a key element of Ukraine's security guarantee proposals
  • Security Guarantees: Ukraine seeks specific commitments that the U.S. would respond if Russia opens fire on Ukraine again
  • Energy Infrastructure: Ukraine's power grid faces collapse, with eastern regions "at the brink" of blackout and Kyiv also at risk
  • Aid Shortfall: Ukraine needs 45-50 billion euros in 2026 to sustain defense and economic stability
