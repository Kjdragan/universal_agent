Russia launched its largest drone and missile barrage in months, targeting Kyiv and multiple regions just days before Christmas. Meanwhile, peace negotiations between Ukraine, Russia, and the United States continued in Miami, with President Zelenskyy scheduled to meet President Trump in Florida on December 28. Russian forces made steady territorial advances, particularly in eastern Ukraine, while Ukrainian forces achieved tactical successes in Kupyansk. 1. Major Military Developments

1.1 Massive Russian Assault on Kyiv (December 27)

Scale of Attack: Russia launched approximately 500 drones and 40 missiles at Kyiv in the early hours of December 27, killing at least one person (a 47-year-old woman) and wounding at least 22 others, including two children. The air raid alert lasted nearly 10 hours.

  • Infrastructure Damage: One-third of Kyiv was left without heat as temperatures hovered around freezing (0°C). Approximately 320,000 households lost power in the broader Kyiv region.
  • Targeting: The attack focused on energy facilities and civilian infrastructure, continuing Russia's campaign to degrade Ukraine's energy grid ahead of winter.
  • Polish Response: Poland scrambled fighter jets and placed ground-based air defense systems on heightened readiness to secure its airspace as Russian strikes targeted western Ukraine.

1.2 Christmas Eve Attacks (December 24-25)

  • Multiple Regions Targeted: Russia fired more than 650 drones and three dozen missiles across 13 Ukrainian regions, killing at least three civilians, including a 4-year-old child in Zhytomyr region.
  • Energy Infrastructure Hit: Western Ukraine's energy facilities were hit the hardest, according to Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko, who condemned the "deliberate and cynical" attack on the eve of Christmas.
  • Emergency Power Cuts: Large-scale power cuts were implemented across the country as emergency workers struggled to restore the energy system.

1.3 Territorial Changes and Battlefield Updates

Russian Advances (December 16-22): According to the Institute for the Study of War (ISW) and Russia Matters data, Russian forces gained 51 square miles of Ukrainian territory in just six days. Since January 1, 2025, Russia has averaged 176 square miles of territorial gains per month.

Key Territorial Changes:

  • Zaporizhzhia Region: Russian forces captured Kosivtseve village, securing more than 23 square kilometers (9 square miles) of territory.
  • Kharkiv Region: Russian forces claimed capture of Vilcha and Prylipka villages, though Ukrainian sources denied complete Russian control.
  • Donetsk Region: Heavy fighting continues around Siversk, Kostyantynivka, and Pokrovsk, with Russian forces making gradual advances in some areas.
  • Ukrainian Counterattacks: Ukrainian forces made tactical gains in Kupyansk, now controlling nearly 90% of the strategic northeastern town. They also liberated five settlements southeast of Oleksandrivka after a 100-day operation.

1.4 Assassinations and Internal Security Issues

  • Lieutenant General Fanil Sarvarov: A car bomb killed the Russian General Staff's Operational Training Department chief in southern Moscow on December 22. This marks the third assassination of a senior Russian military officer in just over a year, with Russian sources pointing to Ukrainian involvement.
  • Additional Moscow Bombing: Two police officers and another person were killed in a separate blast in Moscow on December 24.
  • Crimea Assassination: Former Russian Hispaniola Brigade leader Stanislav Orlov was assassinated by Russian security services in occupied Crimea on December 4, reportedly on suspicions of arms trafficking.

2. Diplomatic Developments and Peace Talks

2.1 US-Brokered Negotiations in Miami

  • Separate Meetings: US envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner held separate meetings with Ukrainian and Russian delegations in Miami. Both sides described the talks as "productive and constructive."
  • 20-Point Peace Plan: A draft 20-point peace plan, developed by Ukraine, the US, and European partners, is reportedly 90% complete. The plan addresses security guarantees, territorial issues, and Ukraine's post-war reconstruction.
  • Zelenskyy-Trump Meeting: Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy is scheduled to meet US President Donald Trump in Florida on December 28 to continue finalizing the agreement.
  • Security Guarantees: The US has offered Ukraine a 15-year security guarantee deal, though Kyiv is seeking a longer-term agreement with protections similar to NATO's Article 5.

2.3 Official Statements and Assessments

US Vice President JD Vance: "I wouldn't say with confidence that we're going to get to a peaceful resolution... I think there's a good chance we will, I think there's a good chance we won't."

  • Kremlin Position: Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov stated that Ukraine's version of the 20-point plan is "radically different" from what Russia has discussed with the US. He called Ukrainian and European proposals "rather unconstructive."
  • Zelenskyy's View: The Ukrainian president described negotiations as "very close to a real result" and said the process "all looks quite worthy," though he conceded "not everything is ideal."
  • Referendum Option: Zelenskyy indicated he would consider putting the peace plan to a referendum if unable to secure stronger terms from the US, provided Russia agrees to a 60-day ceasefire first.

3. Humanitarian and Economic Impact

3.1 Civilian Casualties and Displacement

  • Weekly Death Toll: At least 7 civilians confirmed killed in major attacks on December 24 and 27, including children.
  • Forced Displacement: Russian troops forcibly moved about 50 residents from Hrabovske village in Sumy region to Russia, including 13 Ukrainian servicemen, according to the Ukrainian human rights ombudsman.
  • Total Displaced: 10.6 million Ukrainians remain displaced (24% of pre-war population of 44 million).

3.2 Energy Infrastructure Crisis

Winter Energy Emergency: Russia's intensified attacks on Ukraine's energy grid have pushed the system to the brink of collapse. The strikes targeted residential buildings, power grids, and "essentially the entire infrastructure of daily life."

  • Power Outages: Emergency power cuts implemented across multiple regions, with western Ukraine hit hardest. Approximately 180,000 consumers lost electricity across five regions (Mykolaiv, Zaporizhia, Cherkasy, Dnipropetrovsk, and Sumy).
  • System Damage: Ukraine's energy infrastructure was operating at only about 50% of its pre-invasion generation capacity as of Fall 2025. Recent strikes have further degraded this capacity.
  • Restoration Efforts: Ukrainian emergency crews working to restore power "as quickly as possible," though Prime Minister Svyrydenko warned that emergency cuts would continue until the system stabilizes.

3.3 Economic Consequences

  • Ukraine's Budget Gap: Ukraine faces a foreign aid shortfall of 45-50 billion euros ($53-59 billion) in 2026, according to President Zelenskyy.
  • Debt Restructuring: Ukraine successfully restructured $2.6 billion of growth-linked debt, with creditors accepting a bonds-and-cash swap offer.
  • EU Financial Support: EU leaders agreed to provide an interest-free loan to Ukraine for military and economic needs in 2026-2027, avoiding the use of frozen Russian assets after Belgium raised legal concerns.
  • Russian War Spending: Russia will spend 5.1% of its GDP on the war in 2025, amounting to approximately 11 trillion rubles ($136 billion), according to Defense Minister Andrei Belousov.

4. Military Analysis and Strategic Assessment

4.1 Russian Military Strategy

  • Winter Offensive: Russia has shifted focus to targeting Ukraine's energy and logistics systems, aiming not merely to punish Ukraine but to "destabilise Europe" via potential refugee flows if Ukraine's energy system collapses.
  • Infiltration Tactics: Russian forces increasingly use small group infiltration tactics supported by drones and guided glide bombs, exploiting fog and poor winter weather to mask movements.
  • False Reports Problem: ISW reports that Russian military officials routinely provide inflated reports about Ukrainian casualties and Russian advances to Putin, creating a "rosy picture" that may be shaping the Russian president's understanding of the battlefield.
  • Resource Concentration: Russian forces can advance when they concentrate forces on specific sectors, but this often leaves other areas vulnerable to Ukrainian counterattacks.

4.2 Ukrainian Capabilities and Challenges

  • Domestic Production: Ukraine's military-industrial complex now provides up to 40% of what the armed forces need, up from 15-20% in 2022.
  • Artillery Shortages: Ukrainian forces continue to face critical shortages of artillery shells, with front-line officers reporting inability to strike confirmed targets due to lack of munitions.
  • Drone Warfare: Ukrainian drone strikes successfully targeted Russian oil infrastructure, including the Novoshakhtinsk Petroleum Products Plant in Rostov Oblast and Temryuk seaport in Krasnodar Krai.
  • Personnel Issues: Ukraine struggles with mobilization challenges, described as "weak and cowardly" by one analyst, though other sources note Ukrainian morale remains high among front-line troops.

4.3 International Support

  • US Military Aid: The US Senate passed the fiscal 2026 National Defense Authorization Act, providing $800 million for Ukraine through the Ukraine Security Assistance Initiative.
  • Norwegian Support: Norway announced purchase of ammunition for Ukraine's F-16 fighter jets and air defense systems worth 3.2 billion Norwegian crowns ($290 million).
  • Polish Production: Poland decided to begin producing antipersonnel mines for the first time since the Cold War, planning to deploy them along its eastern border and potentially export them to Ukraine.
  • EU Gas Ban: The European Parliament approved a plan to phase out Russian gas imports by late 2027.

5. Regional and Global Implications

5.1 Belarus's Increasing Involvement

  • Territory Used for Attacks: Zelenskyy accused Russia of using Belarusian territory to intensify drone strikes against railway infrastructure in western Ukraine, circumventing Ukrainian air defenses.
  • Hypersonic Missiles: US researchers found evidence that Russia is likely stationing nuclear-capable hypersonic ballistic missiles at a former airbase in eastern Belarus.
  • Oreshnik Deployment: Belarusian President Lukashenko stated Russia will deploy a maximum of 10 Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missiles to Belarus.
  • Production Facilities: A large ammunition factory is under construction near Minsk to produce 122mm and 152mm artillery shells for Russia's war effort.

5.2 North Korean Support

  • "Blood, Life and Death": North Korean leader Kim Jong Un declared his nation and Russia had shared "blood, life and death" as he sent New Year's greetings to Putin.
  • Troop Deployment: Pyongyang has deployed thousands of troops to Russia to support the war effort against Ukraine.

5.3 Strategic Assessments

ISW Assessment: The Institute for the Study of War continues to assess that it will take Russian forces at least 2-3 years to seize the remainder of Ukrainian-controlled Donetsk Oblast at current tempo, contrary to Kremlin claims of imminent victory.

  • American Enterprise Institute: A Russian victory would force the US to spend an additional $808 billion over five years to reinforce NATO—nearly seven times current US support for Ukraine.
  • War Termination Scenarios: Analysts outline five potential models: burnout (exhaustion), armistice (Korean-style), Finlandization (territorial concessions for sovereignty), recurring crisis dynamics (India-Pakistan model), and hybrid occupation (Georgian scenario).

6. Looking Ahead: Key Questions and Projections

6.1 Immediate Outlook (Week of December 28-January 3)

  • Zelenskyy-Trump Meeting: The December 28 meeting in Florida will be crucial for finalizing the 20-point peace plan and addressing territorial issues.
  • Winter Campaign: Russia is expected to continue targeting energy infrastructure while attempting small territorial advances before weather conditions further deteriorate.
  • Ceiling on Russian Advances: Ukrainian forces in Kupyansk demonstrate that Russian claims of "imminent collapse" of Ukrainian defenses may be exaggerated.

6.2 Medium-Term Scenarios (January-March 2026)

  • War Ending Prospects: Analysts suggest a "window of opportunity" for peace may emerge in late 2026 if Russia realizes it cannot achieve rapid breakthroughs.
  • US Political Transition: The January inauguration could shift US policy, though current negotiations suggest continuity in the Trump administration's approach.
  • European Leadership: Europe is increasingly taking the central role in Ukraine's security architecture as questions persist about long-term US commitment.

6.3 Critical Uncertainties

  • Whether Putin will accept any agreement that doesn't give Russia complete control of Donetsk and Luhansk regions
  • If Ukraine can sustain its defense without a significant increase in Western military aid
  • The impact of continued energy infrastructure attacks on Ukrainian civilian morale and resilience
  • Whether Russia's economy can sustain current war spending levels through 2026
  • The degree to which false reporting has distorted Russian leadership's understanding of battlefield realities

7. Key Takeaways

  1. Military Stalemate Continues: Russia makes slow, steady territorial gains but at enormous cost, while Ukraine demonstrates continued capacity for tactical counterattacks.
  2. Peace Talks Proceed Despite Violence: Diplomatic efforts continue even as Russia launches its largest attacks in months, suggesting both sides see negotiations as strategically valuable regardless of battlefield developments.
  3. Humanitarian Crisis Worsening: Energy infrastructure attacks are creating a humanitarian emergency that could drive mass refugee flows into Europe, particularly if the grid collapses during winter.
  4. Asymmetric Information Challenge: Russian leadership may be making decisions based on inflated battlefield reports, creating risks of miscalculation and overconfidence.
  5. Western Unity Tested: Peace negotiations are straining the Western alliance, with different visions for ending the conflict emerging between the US, EU, and Ukraine.
  6. Long-Term Security Dilemma: Even if fighting stops, experts warn that without robust security guarantees, any agreement could collapse within years, leading to renewed conflict.
