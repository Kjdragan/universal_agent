Russia-Ukraine War: Comprehensive Report

As of December 25, 2025 - Day 1,400 of the Full-Scale Invasion

1. Executive Summary

As of late December 2025, the Russia-Ukraine war has reached a critical juncture. On the battlefield, Russian forces continue their methodical, albeit slow, advance in eastern Ukraine, having gained approximately 4,700-5,100 square kilometers (1,800-1,970 square miles) of territory in 2025 according to independent estimates. Meanwhile, intense diplomatic activity has produced a 20-point US-Ukrainian peace framework that is now under review by Moscow, though Russia has given no indication it will accept terms requiring territorial concessions.

The conflict has entered its fourth year since the full-scale invasion of February 24, 2022, with both sides demonstrating resilience. Ukraine maintains strong defensive fortifications in Donetsk's "fortress belt" while continuing to strike deep into Russian territory using drones and missiles. Russia, despite claims of significant territorial gains, faces mounting casualties—estimated at over 240,000 killed—and is preparing its society for a prolonged conflict.

The diplomatic landscape has shifted notably following the US presidential transition, with the Trump administration pursuing a negotiated settlement. However, fundamental disagreements remain over territorial control, particularly in Donetsk and Luhansk regions, and management of the Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant.

Discrepancies in Claims: A significant gap exists between Russian official claims and independent assessments. According to Al Jazeera's reporting citing the Institute for the Study of War (ISW), Russian President Vladimir Putin claimed his forces had seized over 5,100 square kilometers of Ukrainian territory in 2025, while Russian Defence Minister Andrei Belousov cited 6,300 square kilometers. However, ISW analysis confirms no more than 4,984 square kilometers containing 196 settlements—far fewer than the 300 settlements claimed by Russian officials.

Territory Verification Issues: Putin's specific claims about capturing key towns face strong contradictions from open-source evidence. ISW has found no evidence to confirm claimed seizures of extensive portions of Hulyaipole (Russian presence in only 7.3%), Lyman (2.9%), or Kostiantynivka (no more than 5%). Even Russian military bloggers report more modest advances than Putin's assertions, claiming a maximum of 7% for Lyman and 11% for Kostiantynivka.

Combat Operations

Air and Drone Campaign: During the week of December 18-24, Russia launched 1,227 drones and 41 missiles at Ukrainian targets. Ukraine's air defenses intercepted 80% of drones and 83% of missiles, but strikes on Saturday and Tuesday killed at least four civilians. On December 22-23, Russia conducted another large-scale missile and drone strike against Ukraine overnight.

Ukrainian Deep Strikes: Ukraine continues targeting Russian infrastructure with increasing reach:

  • December 20: Ukrainian SBU struck two Russian Su-27 fighter jets at Belbek airbase near occupied Sevastopol in Crimea, following an earlier attack that weakened air defense systems and hit a MiG-31 interceptor
  • December 18: Ukraine attacked a Russian oil rig in the Caspian Sea belonging to Lukoil, damaging a drilling platform

Assassination Operations: On December 22, Ukrainian military intelligence used an assassination operation to kill Lieutenant General Fanil Sarvarov, chief of the Russian General Staff's operational training department. Ukrainian Defensive Positions

The "Fortress Belt": The BBC reports on a critical defensive line—a "fortress belt" running 50km (31 miles) through western Donetsk. According to ISW analysis, "Ukraine has spent the last 11 years pouring time, money, and effort into reinforcing the fortress belt and establishing significant defence industrial and defensive infrastructure."

This fortified region presents significant challenges to Russian advances. ISW estimates that it would take Russian forces another two years to seize the remainder of the Donetsk region "at great cost," given that cities in the fortress belt are "significantly larger" than Pokrovsk (a town of about 23 sq km that Russia has been trying to capture for nearly two years).

4. Diplomatic Efforts

The US-Ukraine 20-Point Peace Plan

Following marathon talks in Florida, US and Ukrainian negotiators have reached consensus on a comprehensive 20-point peace framework. This represents significant progress from earlier US proposals that Ukraine viewed as heavily favoring Russia. Key elements include:

  • Security Guarantees: NATO-level Article 5 guarantees for Ukraine, with a separate bilateral US document outlining conditions for response to renewed Russian aggression
  • Military Strength: Ukraine maintains an 800,000-person peacetime army with no requirement to recognize occupied territories as de facto Russian
  • EU Accession: Specific timeline for Ukraine's accession to the European Union
  • Economic Support: $800 billion mobilization for reconstruction, including technology, data centers, AI, and gas sectors
  • Trade: Accelerated US-Ukraine free trade agreement; similar US-Russia deal proposed

Outstanding Issues

Point 14 (Donetsk/Luhansk): The most contentious issue concerns the fate of Donetsk and Luhansk regions. Ukraine refuses to cede territory, while Russia demands full control. The US proposal to create "free economic zones" or demilitarized areas under international monitoring remains unresolved.

Point 12 (Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant): Management of Europe's largest nuclear facility remains disputed:

  • US Proposal: Consortium with Ukraine, US, and Russia each holding 33% stakes, with Americans as primary manager
  • Ukrainian Counter-Proposal: Joint US-Ukraine venture where Americans control 50% and decide how to distribute their share to Russia
  • Reconstruction Cost: Billions needed to restore the plant and adjacent dam

According to President Zelensky, these two points required 15 hours of discussion alone, highlighting their complexity.

Referendum Requirement

Ukraine insists that any territorial arrangement must be ratified by referendum, requiring a 60-day period during which hostilities would cease. Zelensky emphasized: "people can choose: this ending suits us or not."

Russian Response

Russia has rejected a US-proposed Christmas ceasefire according to Zelensky, and Kremlin spokesperson Dmitry Peskov indicated Russia would "formulate" its position and "continue contacts in the very near future through existing channels." Russia continues to demand Ukraine relinquish all of Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhia, Kherson, and Crimea—ultimatums Ukraine has rejected.

European Financial Support

On December 20, the European Council approved a 90 billion euro ($106 billion) loan to Ukraine over two years. Ukraine will receive at least 45 billion euros ($53 billion) annually, repayable only from Russian funds. However, EU members pointedly refused to connect this to the 210 billion euros in Russian state assets immobilized in European banks, after Italy, Bulgaria, Malta, and Belgium vetoed using frozen assets as collateral.

US Policy Shift

President Trump ended military assistance to Ukraine after taking office but agreed to continue weapons sales financed by the EU, Canada, Australia, and Japan. This arrangement has successfully continued funding the war effort. Relations between Trump and Zelensky have improved significantly following the February 28 Oval Office incident.

6. Analysis and Implications

Information Warfare and Credibility

The significant discrepancy between Russian official claims and independent verification reflects ongoing information warfare. Putin's assertions about territorial gains exceed what even Russian military bloggers report, suggesting either poor intelligence from subordinates or deliberate exaggeration for domestic and diplomatic audiences. According to ISW, "false reports are likely shaping Russian President Vladimir Putin's understanding of the battlefield situation."

This creates a dangerous dynamic where Russian leadership may make decisions based on inaccurate information, potentially leading to strategic miscalculations. The ISW's systematic refutation of Putin's claims using satellite and open-source evidence represents an important counter-narrative effort.

Putin's Incentives to Continue War

Analysis from the George W. Bush Presidential Center reveals structural factors discouraging peace from Putin's perspective. If the war ended, Putin would face a massive demobilization of soldiers who could reveal the true cost of the conflict, threaten social stability, and potentially organize political challenges.

Key disincentives for peace include:

  • Veteran Reintegration: Hundreds of thousands of soldiers would return home, potentially revealing the truth about war crimes, casualties, and corruption
  • Criminal Activity: Over 12,000 crimes committed by returning soldiers (likely undercounted), including domestic violence and drug/alcohol abuse
  • Political Challenges: Veterans could leverage military service to build independent political power bases
  • Economic Disruption: Russia's wartime economy has become heavily dependent on military production

The Bush Center analysis suggests Putin may seek to redirect soldiers to other conflict zones, such as sub-Saharan Africa, where Russia maintains an estimated 20,000-strong mercenary footprint following Prigozhin's death.

Energy Infrastructure Targeting

The economic dimension of the war features escalating attacks on energy infrastructure:

Ukrainian Strikes on Russia:

  • Caused at least 60 billion rubles in damage according to RFE/RL (March 2025)
  • Forced nearly 40% of Russia's oil refining capacity offline as of October 2025
  • General Syrskyi claims Ukraine's strikes have cost Russia more than $21.5 billion in 2025

Russian Strikes on Ukraine:

  • 64% of Ukraine's electricity generating capacity destroyed or occupied as of 2024
  • Nearly all thermal power generation destroyed by May 2025
  • Significant portions of hydropower installations damaged
  • Overall electricity consumption down by approximately one-third
  • Eastern regions "at the brink" of blackout; Kyiv facing up to 16 hours daily without power

Domestic Russian Public Opinion

According to Russia's state pollster VTsIOM, 70% of 1,600 respondents believe 2026 will be more "successful" than 2025. For 55% of respondents, this hope is linked to a possible end to the war in Ukraine. This suggests the Kremlin may be preparing public opinion for a potential settlement while managing expectations.

NATO and European Security Architecture

The peace framework's security guarantees represent a significant evolution. NATO-level Article 5 guarantees for Ukraine, combined with EU accession, would effectively integrate Ukraine into Western security structures without formal NATO membership—a creative solution to Russia's red line on NATO expansion. France's President Macron announced that starting in January, Paris will continue work by the "coalition of the willing" to provide Ukraine with "solid security guarantees, a prerequisite for a robust and lasting peace."

Strategic Outlook

The conflict shows characteristics of protracted stalemate. Russia can sustain slow territorial advances through attrition but cannot achieve decisive victory. Ukraine cannot reclaim all occupied territory without substantial additional support but can prevent Russian victory and impose unacceptable costs through deep strikes.

The diplomatic track remains the most likely path to resolution, but requires Russia to accept terms falling short of its maximalist demands—or Ukraine accepting territorial concessions it has repeatedly rejected. The 20-point framework represents substantial progress but faces significant hurdles on Points 12 and 14.

Warning: The coming months will prove critical. If Russia rejects the framework and Ukraine cannot sustain its defense, 2026 could see renewed Russian offensives seeking to break the fortress belt and capture remaining Ukrainian-held territories in Donetsk. Conversely, if Ukraine secures additional military support and the framework gains traction, a negotiated ceasefire could emerge.

Humanitarian Consequences

The human cost continues mounting. According to Russia Matters, 10.6 million Ukrainians remain displaced (24% of pre-war population). Casualty figures, while disputed, clearly run into the hundreds of thousands on both sides. The destruction of energy infrastructure threatens civilian suffering during winter, with Kyiv residents facing extended blackouts.

The environmental damage is also significant. Al Jazeera reports a sunflower oil spill contaminating the shoreline around Odesa after Russian attacks on port infrastructure, killing wildlife and triggering conservation warnings.
