3. Peace Negotiations and Diplomatic Efforts

3.1 The 20-Point Peace Plan

In December 2025, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky revealed details of a new 20-point peace plan developed in coordination with US and European negotiators. This represents a revision from an earlier 28-point proposal that had been criticized as overly favorable to Russia.

Key Provisions of the 20-Point Plan:
  • Territory: Freezing the frontline in Luhansk, Donetsk, Zaporizhia, and Kherson oblasts as of the signing date
  • Military: Ukrainian peacetime military capped at 800,000 personnel (up from 600,000 in previous proposal)
  • NATO: No explicit prohibition on Ukrainian NATO membership (removed from earlier proposal)
  • Security Guarantees: US and European security commitments with secret clauses detailing response mechanisms
  • Donetsk Compromise: Proposed demilitarized or "free economic zone" in Ukrainian-held Donetsk territory

3.2 Russian Position and Response

Russia Rejection of Key Proposals: Russian officials have signaled they will reject many provisions of the 20-point plan, including:
  • Lack of guarantee prohibiting Ukrainian NATO membership
  • Insufficient limits on Ukraine's military forces
  • Missing provisions about Ukraine's neutral status
  • No guarantees about Russian language status
  • No provisions on frozen Russian assets or sanctions relief

Russia continues to insist on recognition of its annexations of all four oblasts and Crimea, Ukraine's neutrality, and the lifting of all Western sanctions.

3.3 US Role and Trump Administration

President Donald Trump has positioned himself as the primary mediator, stating "Zelenskyy doesn't have anything until I approve it." The Trump administration has pushed Ukraine toward concessions while simultaneously threatening Russia with additional sanctions if it refuses to negotiate seriously.

However, European leaders remain concerned about being excluded from talks and have emphasized that Ukraine and Europe must be central to any agreement. British Prime Minister Keir Starmer, French President Emmanuel Macron, and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz have formed a "coalition of the willing" to support Ukraine's position.

7. Outlook and Projections

7.1 Short-Term Outlook (2026)

Military analysts and experts offer several scenarios for 2026:

Optimistic Scenario:

A "window of opportunity" for peace may emerge in the second half of 2026 if Russia fails to breach Ukrainian lines and realizes Kyiv can sustain the war of attrition. This would require Ukraine to maintain Western support and bolster its military capabilities.

Most Likely Scenario:

A "pause" or frozen conflict rather than full peace, similar to the Korean model. Ukrainian military officials believe Russia cannot be fully trusted and any ceasefire would likely be temporary.

Pessimistic Scenario:

Continuation of war into 2027 or beyond. The "Finnish scenario" would force Ukraine to recognize occupied territories as Russian, while the "Georgian scenario" would mean no resolution but continued refusal to recognize occupation.
