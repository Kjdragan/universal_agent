Russia-Ukraine War: December 2025 Report

Comprehensive Analysis of Key Developments, Territorial Changes, and Peace Negotiations

Executive Summary

As of December 2025, the Russia-Ukraine war has entered a critical phase marked by intense diplomatic activity alongside continued heavy fighting. This report synthesizes the latest available information from multiple authoritative sources, providing an overview of territorial changes, recent attacks, peace negotiations, and the evolving military and humanitarian situation on the ground.

Current Situation (December 2025): Russian forces have captured approximately 51 square miles of Ukrainian territory in just the past week (Dec 16-22), bringing the total area under Russian control to roughly 19.2% of Ukrainian territory. Since January 1, 2025, Russia has gained an average of 176 square miles per month according to ISW data analyzed by Russia Matters.

Recent Major Attacks on Kyiv

On December 27-28, 2025, Russia launched one of its largest aerial assaults on Ukraine's capital since the full-scale invasion began in February 2022. According to reports from Al Jazeera, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy stated that Russia launched nearly 500 drones and 40 missiles, targeting energy and civilian infrastructure across Ukraine, with Kyiv as a primary focus.

The assault had devastating consequences:

  • At least two people were killed in Kyiv and the surrounding region
  • At least 46 people were wounded, including two children
  • More than 40% of residential buildings in Kyiv were left without heat
  • More than a million households in and around Kyiv were left without power
  • Energy facilities across Ukraine were struck, with emergency power cuts implemented

Poland's Air Navigation Services Agency reported that the Rzeszow and Lublin airports in southeastern Poland were temporarily shut during the Russian attacks, although no violations of Polish airspace were reported.

Peace Negotiations and Diplomatic Developments

Significant diplomatic activity has unfolded throughout December 2025, centered around a developing 20-point peace plan that emerged from talks between the United States, Ukraine, and European partners.

Key Elements of the Emerging Peace Framework:

  • Ukrainian President Zelenskyy has revealed details of potential compromises, including a proposal for a "free economic zone" in Ukrainian-controlled parts of Donetsk oblast
  • The plan includes provisions for a demilitarized zone monitored by international forces
  • Zelenskyy stated that any peace plan would need to be put to a referendum in Ukraine
  • U.S. President Donald Trump has positioned himself as a key arbiter, stating "He [Zelenskyy] doesn't have anything until I approve it"
  • The framework calls for Ukraine's military to be capped at 800,000 personnel during peacetime (increased from earlier proposals of 600,000)

Russia's Response:

  • Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov stated that Russia will not sign any peace agreements to end the war "right now"
  • Russia rejected a U.S.-proposed Christmas ceasefire
  • Kremlin officials continue to insist on recognition of Russia's annexation of four Ukrainian oblasts (Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhia, and Kherson)
  • Russia demands that Ukraine relinquish the remaining territory it holds in the Donbas region

Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant:

A significant sticking point in negotiations involves the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant (ZNPP), Europe's largest nuclear facility, which has been under Russian occupation since early in the invasion. Reports indicate that proposals have included U.S. management of the facility, though Zelenskyy has characterized joint commercial activity with Russia as "very inappropriate and not entirely realistic."

Territorial Changes and Frontline Dynamics

The military situation on the ground continues to evolve, with Russian forces making gradual but persistent advances in certain sectors while Ukrainian forces have achieved tactical gains in others.

Russian Advances:

  • Recent Russian gains in the period November 25-December 22 totaled 138 square miles according to ISW data
  • Russia captured the town of Myrnohrad in Donetsk region, as well as Rodynske and Artemivka
  • Russian forces also claimed to have taken Huliaipole and Stepnohirsk in the Zaporizhia region
  • Geolocated footage indicates Russian advances near Siversk and in the Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka tactical area

Ukrainian Counterattacks:

  • Ukrainian forces have made tactical gains in the Kupyansk direction, with Russian milbloggers increasingly acknowledging Ukrainian successes
  • Ukrainian forces liberated five settlements southeast of Oleksandrivka in an operation lasting over 100 days
  • Ukrainian forces continue their long-range strike campaign against Russian oil and energy infrastructure

Analysis of Russian Reporting:

The Institute for the Study of War (ISW) has documented a pervasive culture of false reporting within the Russian military command. Multiple reports indicate that Russian military and security authorities regularly provide President Vladimir Putin with updates that inflate Ukrainian casualties, highlight Russian resource advantages, and downplay tactical failures. This false reporting has created a distorted picture of battlefield realities that may be affecting Kremlin decision-making.

Humanitarian and Civilian Impact

The war continues to exact a heavy toll on civilian populations:

  • Energy infrastructure remains a primary target, with Russia continuing a campaign to split Ukraine's energy grid in half
  • Chernihiv City and Oblast officials reported strikes on critical and energy infrastructure, causing tens of thousands of residents to lose power
  • In Kharkiv, a thermal power plant strike disrupted power generation, heat supplies, and transport
  • A Russian drone attack on Odesa Oblast caused a sunflower oil spill that contaminated the shoreline around the southern Ukrainian city, killing wildlife

International Support and Aid

Western support for Ukraine continues, though with evolving dynamics:

  • The European Union agreed to a 90 billion euro ($105.5 billion) interest-free loan for Ukraine to support financial requirements during 2026-27
  • Canada announced $1.83 billion in additional economic aid to Ukraine
  • Denmark opened a rocket fuel plant for artillery ammunition production for Ukraine
  • Ireland announced 125 million euros in new funding, including 100 million euros in non-lethal military assistance and 25 million euros for energy system support

Outlook and Analysis

As 2025 draws to a close, several factors will likely shape the trajectory of the conflict in 2026:

  • Military analysts assess that it would likely take Russian forces at least another 2-3 years to seize the remainder of Ukrainian-controlled Donetsk Oblast at the current tempo
  • Both sides are experiencing significant personnel and materiel constraints
  • The success of any peace agreement will depend heavily on whether Russia can be persuaded to compromise on its maximalist territorial demands
  • Ukraine's ability to continue resisting depends on sustained Western support and its own capacity to mobilize sufficient manpower

The coming months will be critical in determining whether the current diplomatic initiatives can lead to a lasting ceasefire and negotiated settlement, or whether the war will continue into its fifth year with continued attrition and territorial changes along the frontline.
