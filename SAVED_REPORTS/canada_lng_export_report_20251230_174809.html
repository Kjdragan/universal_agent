Canada's LNG Export Projects

A Comprehensive Analysis of North American Energy Markets
December 2025 | Research Report

Executive Summary

Canada's liquefied natural gas (LNG) export sector reached a historic milestone in 2025 with the commencement of commercial operations at the LNG Canada facility in Kitimat, British Columbia. This event marks Canada's entry into the global LNG market after more than a decade of development and represents the largest private sector investment in Canadian history at approximately CAD $40 billion.

This report examines the current state of Canada's LNG export industry, its economic impacts, energy security implications for North America, environmental considerations, and future outlook including Phase 2 developments. The analysis draws on recent data from government sources, industry reports, and market analysis to provide a comprehensive overview of this transformative development in Canadian energy infrastructure.

1. Overview of Canada's LNG Export Projects

Canada's LNG export industry is centered in British Columbia, where seven major export projects and one infrastructure project are in various stages of development. According to Natural Resources Canada, these projects represent a potential capital investment of nearly $109 billion and combined production capacity of 50.3 million tonnes per annum (MTPA) of LNG, equivalent to approximately 6.62 billion cubic feet per day (Bcf/d).

1.2 LNG Canada Joint Venture Structure

The LNG Canada project is operated by LNG Canada Development Inc., a joint venture comprising five major international energy companies:

  • Shell PLC (40% ownership) - Project operator through Shell Canada Energy
  • PETRONAS (25% ownership) - Malaysia's state-owned oil and gas company through North Montney LNG LP
  • PetroChina Company Limited (15% ownership) - Through PetroChina Kitimat LNG Partnership
  • Mitsubishi Corporation (15% ownership) - Through Diamond LNG Canada Partnership
  • Korea Gas Corporation (KOGAS) (5% ownership) - Through Kogas Canada LNG Partnership

2. Current Status and 2025 Milestones

June 2025

LNG Canada achieved commercial operation status and loaded its first cargo for export on the tanker Gaslog Glasgow. This marked Canada's entry into the global LNG market and the first large-scale commercial LNG operation in the country.

July 2025

First cargo shipped from Kitimat to Asian markets, with Prime Minister Mark Carney declaring that "by turning aspiration into action, Canada can become the world's leading energy superpower."

October 2025

Natural gas intake at LNG Canada reached record levels, averaging 882 MMcf/d according to British Columbia Energy Regulator data, indicating strong ramp-up of operations.

November 2025

LNG Canada celebrated its 25th cargo delivered from Kitimat, demonstrating consistent operational performance in its first months of commercial operation.

December 2025

Fluor Corporation and JGC Corporation announced the completion and handover of Train 2, marking the full completion of Phase 1 and bringing total capacity to 14 MTPA.

2.1 Construction and Economic Impact

The construction of Phase 1 created significant economic activity:

  • More than 50,000 Canadians contributed directly to building Phase 1
  • Over $3.3 billion CAD spent on goods and services contracted with Indigenous businesses and joint ventures
  • More than $550 million CAD spent with local area businesses
  • Over 300 full-time permanent jobs created for operations

Coastal GasLink Pipeline

The 670-kilometer Coastal GasLink pipeline supplies natural gas from northeast British Columbia to the LNG Canada facility. The project received dozens of citations for failures related to erosion and sediment control during construction, resulting in fines of hundreds of thousands of dollars. The pipeline construction sparked nationwide protests due to being routed through Wet'suwet'en and Gitxsan territory without the consent of hereditary chiefs.

Length: 670 km
Capacity: 2.1 Bcf/d
Cost: ~$6.6 billion

3. Economic Impact on Canada and North American Markets

3.1 National Economic Benefits

The economic impact of Canada's LNG projects extends far beyond direct employment and construction spending. British Columbia Premier David Eby stated that LNG Canada alone will boost national GDP by approximately 0.4% and generate billions of dollars in economic benefits for British Columbians.

Key Economic Projections

  • Government Revenue: Billions in tax revenue over project lifespan
  • Trade Balance: Diversification away from U.S.-centric energy exports
  • GDP Growth: 0.4% boost to national GDP from LNG Canada Phase 1
  • Investment: $109 billion in total capital investment across seven projects

3.2 North American Energy Market Dynamics

Canada's LNG exports are positioned to significantly impact North American energy markets in several ways:

Market Diversification

According to the Canadian Association of Petroleum Producers (CAPP), Canada's LNG exports will "bolster North-American energy availability and could reduce U.S. reliance on its own LNG, coal and oil alternatives by displacing those fuels." This dynamic creates an interesting scenario where Canadian LNG actually strengthens continental energy security rather than competing directly with U.S. interests.

Price Competitiveness

Canadian natural gas offers significant cost advantages:

  • Canadian natural gas prices trade at less than half the price of the U.S. Henry Hub benchmark
  • West coast LNG in Canada "competes exceptionally well against anything being developed in the United States," according to Petronas Canada CEO Mark Fitzgerald
  • Shorter shipping routes to Asian markets compared to U.S. Gulf Coast facilities

North American LNG Capacity Expansion

According to the U.S. Energy Information Administration, North America is positioned to add more than 17 Bcf/d of new liquefaction capacity by 2029, which would more than double the region's export capacity. This expansion represents more than half of global LNG growth projected through 2029.

4. Energy Security Implications for the Continent

4.1 Reducing Dependence on Single Markets

For decades, Canada has been virtually landlocked in terms of natural gas exports, with approximately 97% of exports going to the United States. This created vulnerability to U.S. policy changes and market conditions, as evidenced by the trade tensions that emerged in 2025 following the U.S. presidential election.

"This is something Canada really needs right now," LNG Canada CEO Chris Cooper said, pointing to trade tensions with the United States. The startup of LNG Canada comes at a time when trade tensions have heightened Canada's desire to diversify its export markets.

4.2 Strategic Positioning

Canada's geographic and infrastructure advantages include:

  • Ice-free harbor at Kitimat enabling year-round operations
  • Direct access to Pacific markets without needing to transit the Panama Canal
  • Abundant natural gas reserves in northeast British Columbia (93 trillion cubic metres estimated)
  • Proximity to Asian markets reducing shipping time and costs compared to U.S. Gulf Coast exports

4.3 Continental Energy Integration

Rather than purely competing with U.S. LNG exports, Canadian LNG can complement North American energy security by:

  • Providing additional supply sources during periods of high Asian demand
  • Reducing pressure on U.S. Gulf Coast infrastructure
  • Offering alternative supply routes that enhance market flexibility
  • Strengthening North America's collective position as a global energy supplier

5. Environmental Considerations and Emissions Impacts

5.1 The Emissions Displacement Argument

A report from the Public Policy Forum, produced in partnership with the Canadian Chamber of Commerce Future of Business Centre, argues that expanding Canada's LNG and oil exports "could significantly reduce global greenhouse gas emissions" by displacing higher-emission energy sources in Asian markets.

Environmental Advantages Claimed

  • Canadian LNG is produced with some of the lowest emissions intensity in the world
  • Can displace coal-fired power generation in Asia
  • Electrification of LNG facilities reduces carbon footprint compared to global competitors
  • B.C. has implemented methane regulations to reduce fugitive emissions

5.2 Environmental Concerns

An independent report commissioned by the British Columbia government warns that the province's pursuit of new LNG projects "threatens to set back progress" in reducing greenhouse emissions and B.C. is expected to fail its 2030 climate targets.

Key Environmental Challenges

  • Insufficient electricity supply: B.C. lacks sufficient clean electricity to fully electrify LNG supply chains
  • Opportunity costs: LNG electrification competes with other sectors like critical mineral mining and transportation electrification
  • Methane emissions: Natural gas production and transport result in methane leakage
  • Carbon lock-in: New LNG infrastructure creates decades-long fossil fuel dependence
  • Market uncertainty: Expected LNG glut through 2030 could push prices to lowest levels since pre-Ukraine invasion

5.3 Indigenous Rights and Environmental Stewardship

The Gitanyow Hereditary Chiefs have submitted letters to 43 banks and pension funds urging against financing for Ksi Lisims LNG, citing:

  • Threats to salmon populations in the Nass Watershed
  • Climate impacts that could affect traditional ways of life
  • Inadequate consultation and absence of Indigenous consent
  • Withholding of consent by four of six participating Nations

However, the Haisla Nation, hosting the LNG Canada site, has partnered on the project and is advancing Cedar LNG, which they describe as "the largest majority-Indigenous owned project of its kind in the world."

6. Future Outlook Including Phase 2 Developments

6.1 LNG Canada Phase 2

As of December 2025, LNG Canada's Phase 2 expansion remains in the front-end engineering and design (FEED) stage, with the Fluor-JGC joint venture awarded the FEED contract but no final investment decision yet reached. A final investment decision is expected sometime in 2026 or 2027.

Phase 2 Specifications

Additional Capacity: 14 MTPA (1.84 Bcf/d)
Total Combined Capacity: 28 MTPA (3.68 Bcf/d)
Status: FEED in progress
FID Timeline: 2026-2027
Environmental Permit: Already approved for both phases

6.2 Ksi Lisims LNG

The Nisga'a Nation-led Ksi Lisims LNG project near Prince Rupert has been designated a project of national significance and referred to the Major Projects Office for fast-tracking. A final investment decision is expected early in 2026. The project would become Canada's second-largest LNG facility with 12 MTPA capacity.

6.3 Cedar LNG

This Haisla Nation-led floating LNG project in Kitimat has secured all funding and is expected to start shipments in 2028. A major milestone was reached with Ovintiv Inc. signing a 12-year agreement securing half a million tonnes per year of liquefaction capacity.

6.4 Woodfibre LNG

Located near Squamish, this project has seen costs rise to US$8.8 billion from the original US$5.1 billion estimate, illustrating the challenges of remote construction in British Columbia. The project is under construction with workforce housed on offshore "floatels" to avoid straining local infrastructure.

6.5 Market Outlook Challenges

Despite Canada's advantages, analysts note several challenges:

Headwinds for Expansion

  • Asian demand decline: Asia's LNG demand is set to fall by 5% in 2025, the steepest annual decline since 2022, according to the Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA)
  • Global LNG glut: Expected to persist through 2030, potentially depressing prices
  • High Canadian costs: Remote construction, complex permitting, and labor logistics make Canadian projects more expensive than U.S. alternatives
  • Competition: U.S. Gulf Coast has over 200 MTPA either operating or under construction versus Canada's 20 MTPA in first wave
  • Policy uncertainty: Environmental regulations create additional costs and timeline risks

7. Market Implications for US-Canada Energy Trade

7.1 Evolving Trade Relationship

The year 2025 brought significant changes to Canada-U.S. energy relations, driven primarily by trade tensions following the U.S. presidential election. These developments accelerated Canadian efforts to diversify export markets and reduce dependence on the United States as the sole market for Canadian energy exports.

7.2 Current Trade Patterns

According to the Canada Energy Regulator:

  • In 2023, Canada exported 7.8 Bcf/d of natural gas, all to the United States
  • Natural gas exports to the U.S. were valued at $13 billion
  • Canada imported 2.0 Bcf/d of natural gas, nearly 100% from the U.S.
  • This existing integrated infrastructure provides flexibility for continental market balancing

7.3 Policy Developments

The signing of a Memorandum of Understanding between Canada and Alberta governments in November 2025 represents what Calgary Chamber of Commerce CEO Deborah Yedlin called a "tectonic" shift in federal-provincial energy relations. The agreement includes:

  • Construction of one or more private-sector financed pipelines to transport at least one million barrels per day of low-emissions Alberta bitumen to Asian markets
  • Withholding implementation of the oil and gas emissions cap originally scheduled for 2030
  • Suspension of Clean Electricity Regulations in Alberta pending new carbon pricing agreements
  • Funding commitments for carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) infrastructure

8. Conclusion and Strategic Implications

Canada's emergence as an LNG exporter in 2025 represents a transformative development in North American energy markets with far-reaching implications for trade, energy security, and climate policy. The $40 billion LNG Canada Phase 1 project, as the largest private investment in Canadian history, demonstrates both the scale of opportunity and the magnitude of risk involved in capital-intensive energy infrastructure.

8.1 Key Takeaways

Strategic Advantages

  • Geographic Position: Pacific coast location provides direct access to growing Asian markets
  • Cost Competitiveness: Canadian gas trades at less than half U.S. Henry Hub prices
  • Low Emissions Intensity: Among the lowest-emission LNG projects globally when fully electrified
  • Indigenous Partnerships: Novel ownership models like Cedar LNG create new economic frameworks

Critical Challenges

  • Market Timing: Asian LNG demand declining while supply glut emerges
  • Construction Costs: Remote locations and complex terrain increase costs substantially
  • Environmental Trade-offs: LNG expansion threatens achievement of climate targets
  • Indigenous Opposition: Some projects face significant legal and social resistance
  • Policy Uncertainty: Regulatory complexity creates investment risk

8.2 Looking Ahead

The success of Canada's LNG industry will depend on multiple factors: global demand dynamics, particularly in Asia; the ability to control construction and operating costs; the resolution of environmental and Indigenous concerns; and the maintenance of policy support from federal and provincial governments. The Phase 2 final investment decision in 2026-2027 will be a critical bellwether for the future of Canadian LNG expansion.

For North American energy markets, Canadian LNG represents both increased continental supply capacity and a mechanism for market diversification that enhances energy security. Rather than purely competitive, Canadian and U.S. LNG exports can be complementary, providing flexibility and resilience to serve global demand while maintaining energy security at home.

The path forward will require balancing economic development objectives with climate commitments, Indigenous rights, and environmental protection. How Canada navigates these complex trade-offs will determine whether LNG becomes a cornerstone of the country's energy future or a transitional bridge to a different energy system.
