Russia-Ukraine War Weekly Report

December 20-26, 2025 | Day 1,394-1,401 of the Invasion

Executive Summary: The past week has witnessed a grim paradox in the Russia-Ukraine war: intensive US-led shuttle diplomacy advancing a 20-point peace plan concurrent with the most severe Russian aerial bombardment of 2025. While negotiators in Florida report "productive" talks, Moscow launched over 650 drones and three dozen missiles across 13 Ukrainian regions in a single night, killing at least three civilians including a 4-year-old child. Russian forces continue grinding advances in eastern Ukraine, capturing the town of Siversk, while Ukraine expands its strategic drone campaign against Russian oil infrastructure. Fundamental disagreements remain on territorial control and security guarantees, with US Vice President JD Vance acknowledging he lacks "confidence" a peaceful resolution will be reached.

Territorial Control (Dec 22, 2025): Russia occupies approximately 116,057 square kilometers (44,810 sq mi) of Ukrainian territory — roughly 19.2% of Ukraine's pre-war land area, equivalent in size to the state of Ohio.

Russian Advances: Moscow gained 51 square miles of Ukrainian territory in the past six days alone (Dec 16-22). Since January 1, 2025, Russia has averaged 176 square miles of territorial gains per month.

Energy Assault: On December 23-24, Russia fired more than 650 drones and three dozen missiles at Ukraine, forcing emergency power cuts across multiple regions.

Peace Negotiations: The 20-Point Plan and Fundamental Impasses

The diplomatic track has accelerated significantly with US-led shuttle diplomacy producing what Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy describes as a "concrete but unfinalized" 20-point peace framework. Separate talks occurred in Miami between US envoys (Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner) and Ukrainian delegations, then with Russian representatives led by Kirill Dmitriev. The plan reportedly consists of three additional documents: security guarantees, a detailed US military response mechanism, and a "Roadmap for Ukraine's Prosperity" economic recovery plan.

Areas of Reported Consensus:

  • Security Guarantees: The US has offered "strong" security commitments mirroring NATO's Article 5, including a bilateral US-Ukraine agreement detailing response mechanisms to renewed Russian aggression, utilizing satellite technology and early warning systems
  • Free Economic Zones: Transforming contested Donbas areas into special economic zones with international monitoring
  • Economic Framework: Reconstruction funds, accelerated US-Ukraine free trade agreement, EU market access, and a development fund targeting technology, AI, and energy sectors
  • Military Force Structure: Ukraine would maintain an 800,000-person peacetime army (increased from 600,000 in earlier drafts)
  • Post-War Elections: Ukraine would hold elections after signing the agreement

Unresolved Core Issues

Despite reported progress on approximately 90% of "first developments," two fundamental disputes remain unresolved:

  • Territorial Control in Donetsk: The most contentious issue. Russia demands Ukraine withdraw from the remaining 30% of Donetsk region it controls (approximately 15% of the broader Donbas). Ukraine has rejected this territorial concession. The US proposal would create a demilitarized "free economic zone" subject to a Ukrainian referendum. Russia currently controls about 75% of Donetsk and 99% of neighboring Luhansk.
  • Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant: Europe's largest nuclear facility remains under Russian occupation. The US proposed a joint Ukraine-Russia-US consortium (33% ownership each). Ukraine countered with a US-Ukraine joint venture where Washington decides how to distribute its 50% share. Zelenskyy noted this single point required 15 hours of discussion.

Kremlin Position: Russian officials have signaled skepticism about the latest proposals. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov described talks as a "working progress" rather than a breakthrough. Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov criticized Ukrainian and European "attempts to torpedo" peace talks and insisted any settlement must address "root causes" — code for maximalist demands including Ukraine's NATO abandonment, demilitarization, "denazification" (government replacement), recognition of Russia's annexations, and sanctions relief. These demands date to Putin's June 2024 speech and remain incompatible with the 20-point plan.

US Assessment: Vice President JD Vance offered a sobering outlook: "I think there's a good chance we will [reach peace], I think there's a good chance we won't. Sitting here today, I wouldn't say with confidence that we're going to get to a peaceful resolution." Secretary of State Marco Rubio acknowledged progress but stated: "It's not our war. We can't force Ukraine to make a deal. We can't force Russia to make a deal. They have to want to make a deal."

Military Developments: Grinding Advances and Strategic Bombardment

Russian Gains in Eastern Ukraine

Russian forces continue slow, attritional advances in Donetsk region. The Ukrainian General Staff confirmed the withdrawal from the eastern town of Siversk (pre-war population: 10,000) on December 24. Russian forces had earlier claimed control of Svyato-Pokrovske. The capture of Siversk brings Moscow closer to the remaining "Fortress Belt" cities of Sloviansk and Kramatorsk — Ukraine's last fortified defensive anchors in Donetsk.

Pokrovsk Direction: Geolocated footage indicates Ukrainian forces recently advanced in northwestern Pokrovsk, while Russian forces attacked within the town itself. Ukrainian officials report they control northern Pokrovsk and can still conduct rotations, with Russian forces controlling roughly 30% of nearby Myrnohrad.

Kupyansk Setbacks: In a significant reversal, Kremlin-affiliated Russian milbloggers acknowledged that Ukrainian forces have liberated or maintained positions across 182.64 square kilometers in the Kupyansk direction since December 11. One prominent milblogger accused the Russian military command of "blatantly lying" to "the very, very top" (referring to President Putin) about exaggerated gains. This marks a rare admission of Ukraine's successful counterattacks.

Northern Front: Ukrainian forces are battling an attempted Russian breakthrough in Sumy region. Fighting continues in the village of Grabovske after Moscow forcibly moved approximately 50 civilians from the border village into Russia. Earlier, Russia captured Vysoke, a short distance from Grabovske.

Russia's Aerial Campaign: Systematic Energy Infrastructure Destruction

December 23-24 witnessed the most massive Russian combined drone and missile assault of 2025. The attack targeted 13 Ukrainian regions:

  • Scale: 650+ drones and three dozen missiles
  • Civilian Casualties: At least three killed, including a 4-year-old child in Zhytomyr region
  • Infrastructure Damage: Emergency power cuts across Sumy, Dnipropetrovsk, Kherson, Kharkiv, and Chernihiv oblasts. A thermal power plant strike in Kharkiv killed one person, injured 13, and forced the city onto "energy islands."
  • Strategic Pattern: ISW assesses Russia is systematically splitting Ukraine's energy grid in half, with eastern regions "at the brink" of blackout and Kyiv also at risk.

Ukrainian Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko condemned the "deliberate and cynical attack on the eve of Christmas." Zelenskyy stated: "An attack carried out essentially in the midst of negotiations aimed at ending this war... Every Russian strike against Ukraine and the intense Russian assaults on the front line prove that Ukraine's commitment to ending the war far exceeds Russia's."

Ukraine's Strategic Strike Campaign: Targeting Russia's War Economy

Ukraine continues expanding its long-range drone and missile campaign against Russian infrastructure supporting the war:

Oil Infrastructure: Ukraine struck four vessels in Russia's shadow fleet, the Novoshakhtinsk oil refinery in Rostov region (a major supplier for Russian military operations), and the Tamanneftegaz oil terminal in Krasnodar Krai. Ukrainian drones also attacked oil storage facilities at Temryuk port and a gas processing plant in Orenburg region.

Defense Industry: Ukrainian forces struck the Efremov Synthetic Rubber Plant in Tula Oblast, which produces components for explosives and solid rocket fuel. Ukraine's SBU also assassinated Lieutenant General Fanil Sarvarov, chief of the Russian General Staff's Operational Training Department, via car bomb in Moscow City on December 22.

Navy: Ukraine struck a storage and maintenance site for naval drones near occupied Myrnyi, Crimea, following a December 15 attack on a Kilo-class submarine at Novorossiysk Naval Base.

Intelligence and Strategic Analysis

Putin's Imperial Ambitions Extend Beyond Ukraine

Atlantic Council analysis warns that Vladimir Putin's December 17 address to Defense Ministry officials signaled maximalist goals far beyond Ukraine. Putin declared Russia would achieve "the liberation of its historical lands by military means" if diplomatic efforts fail. His definition of "historically Russian lands" encompasses:

  • The entirety of Ukraine (Putin: "I consider the Russian and Ukrainian peoples to be one people. In this sense, all of Ukraine is ours")
  • Former Soviet territories including Finland, Poland, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Belarus, Moldova, and Central Asian nations
  • Former Eastern Bloc satellite states

US intelligence assessments confirm Putin has not abandoned aims of capturing all of Ukraine and reclaiming parts of Europe. "The Europeans are convinced of it. The Poles are absolutely convinced of it. The Baltics think they're first," one report noted.

Russian Military's Culture of Deception

The Financial Times reported that Russian military authorities regularly provide Putin with inflated reports highlighting Ukrainian casualties and resource advantages while downplaying tactical failures. Chief of General Staff Valery Gerasimov is responsible for these briefings. This "rosy picture" has led Putin to believe Russia can win through attrition. Contradictorily, economic officials like Central Bank Chair Elvira Nabiullina and Finance Minister Anton Siluanov present more sober assessments.

Russian milbloggers' recent admissions of false reporting in Kupyansk — where they claimed control of 11 settlements while actually holding only 3 — further exposes how Putin's cognitive warfare effort feeds on and encourages these false reports.

European Security Concerns

NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte warned that Europe would face growing threats if support for Ukraine is reduced. Germany's Foreign Ministry stated Russia would continue threatening European security regardless of any ceasefire, warning Moscow might use a pause to prepare attacks on NATO members. A Russian victory would force the US to spend an additional $808 billion over five years reinforcing NATO — nearly seven times current US support for Ukraine.

Humanitarian and Economic Impact

Energy Crisis Deepens

Russia's systematic targeting has destroyed approximately 48% (27 gigawatts) of Ukraine's pre-war installed power generation capacity of 56.1 GW. Specific losses include:

  • 64% of electricity generating capacity destroyed or occupied
  • Significant portions of thermal power generation destroyed
  • Substantial damage to hydropower installations
  • Destruction of 90% of Ukraine's gas production ahead of winter 2025-2026
  • Overall electricity consumption has fallen by approximately 60%

Residents in Kyiv face up to 16 hours daily without power as Russian strikes since October have pushed the grid to the brink.

Economic and Human Costs

Displacement: 10.6 million Ukrainians remain displaced (24% of Ukraine's 44 million pre-war population). Approximately 6 million have fled the country, while 4.6 million are internally displaced.

Aid Shortfall: Zelenskyy announced Ukraine faces a $45-50 billion foreign aid shortfall in 2026. Without a first tranche of loans secured by frozen Russian assets by spring, Ukraine will be forced to cut drone production.

Debt Restructuring: Ukraine successfully restructured $2.6 billion of growth-linked debt through a bonds-and-cash swap — a key step toward emerging from sovereign default.

International Support and Sanctions

European Assistance

EU leaders agreed to provide an interest-free loan to Ukraine for military and economic needs in 2026-2027. Britain imposed new sanctions on Russian oil companies, including 24 individuals and entities targeting Tatneft, Russneft, NNK-Oil, and Rusneftegaz.

US Politics and Peace Prospects

Trump administration officials report approximately 90% consensus on terms in previous talks, with multi-national force deployment to Ukraine under consideration. However, US negotiators have pressured Ukraine toward concessions, with Politico reporting the administration believes Russia might accept EU membership for Ukraine coupled with mutual defense guarantees. Zelenskyy has suggested Ukraine could compromise on NATO membership if provided bilateral security guarantees similar to NATO's Article 5.

War Crime Sentences

A Russian court sentenced British man Hayden Davies, who fought for Ukraine, to 13 years in a maximum security prison camp for being a "paid mercenary." Belarusian man Sergei Yeremeyev received a 22-year sentence for blowing up two trains in Siberia for Ukraine.

Looking Ahead: Scenarios for War Termination

Analysis from Small Wars Journal outlines five historical models for how the Russia-Ukraine war could end:

  1. Burnout (Iran-Iraq Model): Prolonged attrition ending only when both sides are exhausted. Risks frozen lines, incomplete sovereignty restoration, and gradual erosion of reform momentum.
  2. Armistice (Korean Model): Military agreement suspending hostilities without political peace treaty. Would reduce daily attrition but institutionalize territorial loss and incomplete sovereignty. Long-term viability depends on sustained US-backed deterrence.
  3. Finlandization (Winter War Model): Ukraine preserves statehood but concedes territory for sovereignty guarantees, adopting long-term neutrality. Politically unlikely given strong Ukrainian public support for EU/NATO integration.
  4. Recurring Crisis (India-Pakistan Model): Unresolved sovereignty disputes produce cycles of limited wars and crisis bargaining. Would trap Ukraine in instability, force sustained mobilization, and embed a long-term security fault line in Europe.
  5. Hybrid Occupation (Georgian Model): Russia maintains partial occupation while cultivating pro-Russian government in Kyiv. Would formalize independence with deep strategic vulnerability, fragmenting Ukraine and undermining democratic institutions.

Comparative analysis suggests most endgames pose significant risks unless anchored in strong, enduring Western security guarantees. The decisive question is not when the war ends, but what kind of order follows.

Conclusion

As the war approaches its fourth year in February 2026, the fundamental paradox remains: diplomacy advances while destruction escalates. The 20-point peace plan represents the most serious negotiation framework to date, yet Russia's maximalist demands — unchanged from 2022 — remain incompatible with Ukraine's minimum requirements for sovereignty and security. The massive Christmas aerial assault, killing children and destroying energy infrastructure, sends what Zelenskyy termed an "extremely clear signal about Russia's priorities."

Ukrainian analyst Ihor Tyshkevich warned of the "Finnish scenario" — Kyiv recognizing occupied regions as Russian — or the "Georgian scenario" — no control over occupied areas but refusal to recognize them as Russia's. The worst-case development, he noted, would be an interim scenario where the war freezes but talks continue indefinitely.

Meanwhile, average Ukrainians grow wearier. "Donetsk was the source of our problems. Let Russia have it and pay tens of billions to restore it," 63-year-old former economist Taras Tymoshchuk told Al Jazeera. "I want to wake up because the birds are singing, not because I hear Russian drones and missiles."

Yet Ukraine's military-industrial complex now provides 40% of armed forces' needs — up from 15-20% in 2022. And Ukraine continues striking deep into Russian territory, targeting the oil infrastructure that fuels Moscow's war machine. The question for 2026 is whether Ukraine's resilience and Western support can outlast Russia's capacity for attrition before any negotiated settlement takes hold.
