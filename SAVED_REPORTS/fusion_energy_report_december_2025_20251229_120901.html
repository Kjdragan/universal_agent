Fusion Energy Developments: December 2025 Report

Scientific Breakthroughs, Commercial Milestones, and Key Achievements

Report Prepared: December 29, 2025

Executive Summary: December 2025 marks a watershed moment for fusion energy with multiple record-breaking achievements, substantial commercial investments, and unprecedented scientific progress. The month witnessed Tokamak Energy setting three new performance records, the National Ignition Facility achieving repeated ignition milestones, major corporate mergers positioning fusion for commercialization, and significant advances in plasma physics and magnet technology. This comprehensive report synthesizes developments from 41 leading scientific, commercial, and policy sources.

1. Historic Achievements in Plasma Performance

1.1 Tokamak Energy's Record-Breaking ST40 Results

Triple Performance Records Set

On December 19, 2025, Tokamak Energy announced that its ST40 spherical tokamak achieved three simultaneous performance records, marking what the company called "knockout results" to end 2025:

  • Plasma Current: Reached 1 mega-ampere (MA, 1,000,000 amperes), surpassing the previous record of 0.85 MA
  • Stored Energy: Nearly doubled compared to previous campaigns
  • Fusion Triple Product: Improved by 33% to approximately 8 ± 2 × 10¹⁸ m⁻³·keV·s, the highest ever achieved in a privately-owned tokamak

Technical Significance: The plasma current achievement is particularly critical as it demonstrates the ability to generate the poloidal magnetic field necessary for plasma confinement. The fusion triple product—combining plasma temperature, density, and energy confinement—represents a key metric on the path to commercial fusion energy. Operating in H-mode (high-confinement mode) enabled these breakthroughs.

The ST40, operating in Oxfordshire, UK, has now paused operations for a $52 million upgrade program called LEAPS (Lithium Evaporations to Advance PFCs in ST40). This upgrade, conducted in partnership with the U.S. Department of Energy and UK Department for Energy Security and Net Zero, will apply lithium coatings to all plasma-facing components—a technique pioneered by Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory that has demonstrated significant plasma performance improvements.

1.2 High-Temperature Superconducting Magnet Breakthrough

In a separate breakthrough, Tokamak Energy's Demo4 platform achieved a world-first by successfully generating magnetic field strengths of 11.8 Tesla at -243°C (-405.4°F) using a complete High Temperature Superconducting (HTS) magnet system. This represents the first time fusion power plant fields have been created in a full HTS magnet configuration.

System Specifications: The Demo4 system features 14 toroidal field magnets and two poloidal field magnets, managing seven million ampere-turns of electrical current through its central column. CEO Warrick Matthews described this as a "major victory" for the sector, validating one of the technical solutions needed for commercial fusion energy.

2. National Ignition Facility: Repeated Ignition Success

2.1 April 2025: Record Energy Yield

According to Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, an experiment on April 7, 2025, set new records for both energy yield and target gain. The National Ignition Facility (NIF) achieved:

  • Energy Yield: 8.6 megajoules (±0.45 MJ uncertainty)
  • Target Gain: 4.13 (from 2.08 MJ laser input in a 456-terawatt peak power pulse)

This achievement significantly surpassed the initial December 2022 ignition milestone that produced 3.15 MJ from 2.05 MJ of laser energy (target gain of approximately 1.5).

2.2 June 2025: LANL-Led Ignition Achievement

On June 22, 2025, a Los Alamos National Laboratory-led team working with LLNL achieved ignition using NIF, generating 2.4 MJ of energy (±0.09 MJ uncertainty) and creating a self-sustaining burning plasma with a novel approach called THOR (Torus with High Optical Reflectivity). This marked the first time a team outside LLNL's primary ignition group achieved the milestone.

2.3 October 2025: Nuclear Survivability Experiment

In a demonstration of fusion's national security applications, LLNL conducted an experiment delivering 2.065 MJ of laser energy to produce 3.6 MJ of fusion yield (target gain of 1.74). This experiment exposed weapons-grade plutonium samples to intense thermonuclear neutron radiation, providing unprecedented data on nuclear weapons survivability in hostile threat environments.

3. Theoretical Breakthrough: Fusion Reactors and Dark Matter

3.1 Axion Production in Fusion Reactors

On December 28, 2025, University of Cincinnati researchers published a theoretical study in the Journal of High Energy Physics demonstrating how future fusion reactors could produce axions—hypothetical particles long suspected to be a component of dark matter. The research, conducted with collaborators from Fermi National Laboratory, MIT, and the Technion-Israel Institute of Technology, outlines how neutrons in deuterium-tritium fusion reactors could spark rare reactions producing these particles.

Production Mechanisms: Two primary pathways were identified: (1) neutrons interacting with lithium-lined reactor vessel walls, triggering nuclear reactions that create dark sector particles, and (2) neutrons colliding with other particles and slowing down, releasing energy through bremsstrahlung ("braking radiation") that could generate axions or axion-like particles.

This research notably revisits a concept once featured in the television show "The Big Bang Theory," where fictional physicists struggled to solve the axion production problem. Lead researcher Jure Zupan noted that while the sun produces more axions through similar processes, fusion reactors could use different mechanisms to generate these particles.

4. Commercial Developments and Major Investments

4.1 Trump Media-TAE Technologies Merger

$6 Billion All-Stock Transaction

On December 18, 2025, Trump Media & Technology Group (TMTG), parent company of Truth Social, announced an all-stock merger valued at more than $6 billion with TAE Technologies, a California-based fusion developer. This merger would create one of the first publicly traded nuclear fusion companies in the United States.

Transaction Details:

  • Each TAE common stock valued at $53.89 per share
  • Trump Media to provide up to $200 million in cash at signing
  • Additional $100 million available upon SEC filing
  • Shareholders from both companies to own approximately 50% of combined entity
  • Transaction expected to close mid-2026, pending approvals

Corporate Structure: Devin Nunes, TMTG CEO, will serve as co-CEO alongside TAE CEO Michl Binderbauer. Donald Trump Jr. will join the nine-member board. The combined company plans to identify a site and begin construction in 2026 on what it calls the world's first utility-scale fusion power plant—a 50-megawatt facility.

TAE Technology: TAE specializes in field-reversed configuration using hydrogen-boron fuel (rather than deuterium-tritium). The company has built and operated five fusion reactors, holds over 1,600 patents, and raised more than $1.3 billion from investors including Google, Chevron Technology Ventures, Goldman Sachs, and Renaissance Technologies.

4.2 Other Commercial Power Purchase Agreements

According to Energy Council analysis, major power purchase agreements continue signaling commercial confidence:

  • Italian energy giant ENI: Announced purchase of $1 billion worth of electricity from Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS)
  • Google: Entered commercial agreement with CFS for fusion energy

CFS, a MIT spin-off backed by Bill Gates' Breakthrough Energy Ventures, plans to begin construction of its Arc facility in 2027-28, aiming to deliver electricity to the grid in the early 2030s with 400 megawatts of capacity.

5. Global Fusion Facility Updates

5.1 ITER Project Progress

The 37th ITER Council meeting reported significant progress, with ITER Director-General Pietro Barabaschi noting that the project is "somewhat ahead of schedule while spending is less than anticipated." Key developments include:

  • Completion of the central solenoid assembly by General Atomics (a 15-year, $1 billion project)
  • Start of production of all divertor components
  • Installation of the first gyrotron for electron cyclotron heating
  • Completion of bioshield penetrations in the Tokamak Building

Central Solenoid Specifications: When completed, this will be the largest and most powerful pulsed superconducting magnet ever built—nearly 60 feet high, weighing 1,000 tons, with stored magnetic energy of 6.4 gigajoules and maximum field of 13 tesla. It will enable a powerful pulsed current of 15 megaamperes.

Updated Timeline: ITER's first plasma is now targeted for 2034, with the full fusion-powered phase beginning in 2039. While this represents a delay from original plans, ITER officials note the actual delay is approximately four years and allows for a more robust startup with a largely complete machine.

5.2 Other International Progress

  • China's EAST reactor: Achieved record 1,066 seconds (nearly 18 minutes) of steady operation at extreme heat in January 2025
  • South Korea's K-STAR: Recorded plasma operation at 100 million degrees Celsius for 48 seconds in April 2024
  • Germany's Wendelstein 7-X: Achieved 43 seconds of plasma containment in May 2025
  • Joint European Torus (JET): Reportedly achieved up to 60 seconds containment time in final experiments before retirement

6. Investment Landscape and Economic Projections

6.1 Global Private Investment Exceeds $10 Billion

According to the IAEA World Fusion Outlook 2025, global private investment in fusion has surpassed $10 billion, reflecting growing confidence in the sector. The Fusion Industry Association reports that fusion companies raised more than $2.6 billion in just the first seven months of 2025.

6.2 Economic Impact Projections

MIT modeling cited by the IAEA suggests fusion could play a significant role in future electricity generation:

  • Low-cost scenario ($2.8K/kW in 2050): Fusion could reach up to 50% of global electricity generation by 2100
  • High-cost scenario ($11.3K/kW): Fusion could still reach 10% of global electricity generation by 2100
  • Economic value: With rising demand for clean electricity, fusion could add trillions of dollars to global GDP

7. Technology Trends and Innovation

7.1 High-Temperature Superconducting Magnets

HTS technology emerges as a critical enabler for compact fusion devices. Unlike ITER's magnets requiring 4 Kelvin temperatures, new HTS materials can operate at 93 Kelvin—dramatically reducing cooling requirements. Projects integrating HTS include:

  • Commonwealth Fusion Systems' SPARC and ARC reactors
  • WHAM (University of Wisconsin project)
  • Various tokamak, stellarator, and mirror machine designs

7.2 Alternative Approaches Gaining Ground

The fusion landscape is diversifying beyond traditional tokamaks:

  • Stellarators: Germany's Wendelstein 7-X demonstrating advantages in plasma stability
  • Field-Reversed Configuration: TAE Technologies' hydrogen-boron approach
  • Inertial Confinement: Multiple private companies pursuing laser-driven fusion following NIF's success
  • Magnetized Target Fusion: General Fusion's hybrid approach targeting early 2030s grid connection

8. Challenges and Remaining Hurdles

8.1 Technical Challenges

Despite remarkable progress, significant hurdles remain:

  • Materials science: Developing first-wall materials capable of withstanding continuous neutron bombardment
  • Tritium breeding: Establishing sustainable fuel cycle infrastructure
  • Heat extraction: Engineering efficient thermal management systems
  • Plasma confinement: Achieving sustained high-performance operation at commercial scale

8.2 Economic and Regulatory Challenges

  • Capital intensity: Early projects require tens of billions of dollars
  • Regulatory framework: No globally harmonized definition of fusion power plant exists
  • Grid integration: Fusion plants will likely require new electricity infrastructure
  • Competition: Must compete with rapidly expanding renewables (solar, wind) with falling costs

9. Conclusion

December 2025 represents a pivotal month in fusion energy development. The convergence of record-breaking scientific achievements, substantial commercial investments, and technological maturation suggests fusion is transitioning from purely scientific pursuit to impending commercial reality. While significant challenges remain, the momentum gained in 2025—particularly in HTS magnets, repeated ignition achievements, and private-sector commitment—positions the fusion community for accelerated progress toward grid-connected fusion power in the 2030s.

The field's evolution from approximately five fusion companies a decade ago to 45-48 today, combined with over $10 billion in private investment and multiple power purchase agreements, demonstrates growing confidence that fusion can become a viable component of the global clean energy portfolio. As the IAEA notes, fusion energy is no longer confined to experimental research but is emerging as a strategic national priority for research and development worldwide.
