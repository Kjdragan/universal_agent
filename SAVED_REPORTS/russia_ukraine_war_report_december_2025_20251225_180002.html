Russia-Ukraine War Report

Comprehensive Analysis of Recent Developments
December 25, 2025

Executive Summary

As the Russia-Ukraine war approaches its fourth anniversary, the conflict remains deadlocked with intensifying diplomatic efforts alongside continued grinding warfare on the ground. December 2025 has seen significant diplomatic activity, with U.S.-led peace negotiations in Florida producing a 20-point draft proposal, while Russian forces maintain slow but steady territorial gains in eastern Ukraine.

Key Headlines:
  • U.S. and Ukraine have reached consensus on most issues in a 20-point peace plan, but critical territorial disputes remain unresolved
  • Russia has captured approximately 4,700-6,000 square kilometers of territory in 2025 alone
  • 70% of Russians surveyed expect the war to end in 2026 with Moscow achieving its objectives
  • U.S. officials express skepticism about reaching a peaceful resolution
  • Ukraine expands maritime operations, targeting Russian oil infrastructure

Key Recent Developments

Florida Peace Negotiations

Marathon talks in Florida between U.S., Ukrainian, and Russian envoys have produced a 20-point draft peace proposal. According to NPR, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy briefed journalists on the plan, stating that "consensus has been found" on most points between Ukraine and the United States, though two critical issues remain unresolved: control of the Donetsk region (Point 14) and management of the Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant (Point 12).

The talks, described by U.S. envoy Steve Witkoff as "productive and constructive," have resulted in approximately 90% consensus on terms according to White House officials. However, as Secretary of State Marco Rubio noted, "the hardest issues are always the last issues."

Russian Public Opinion Shift

A significant development comes from within Russia itself. According to state-owned pollster VTsIOM, 70% of 1,600 Russians surveyed view 2026 as a more "successful" year for Russia, with 55% specifically linking their optimism to the possible completion of what Russia officially calls its "special military operation" and the achievement of stated objectives. This marks a notable shift in Russian public sentiment, with approximately two-thirds now supporting peace talks according to independent pollster Levada—the highest number since the war began in 2022.

Territorial Gains and Losses

The BBC reports that Russia has taken about 4,700 square kilometers (1,800 square miles) of territory in 2025—an area approximately twice the size of Moscow city—though Russian officials claim to have captured 6,000 square kilometers. The advance has been slow but steady, with Russian forces "churning mile by mile through the wide open fields of the Luhansk and Donetsk regions."

Diplomatic Efforts and Peace Talks

The 20-Point Peace Plan

The draft agreement emerging from Florida talks contains several key provisions:

  • Security Guarantees: Ukraine would receive "strong" security guarantees mirroring NATO's Article 5, with a separate bilateral U.S. document detailing conditions for response to renewed Russian aggression
  • Territorial Arrangements: The U.S. has proposed transforming disputed areas into free economic zones, while Ukraine insists any arrangement must be ratified by referendum within 60 days
  • Nuclear Plant Management: The U.S. proposes a three-way consortium (U.S.-Ukraine-Russia, 33% each) for the Zaporizhzhia plant, while Ukraine counter-proposed a U.S.-Ukraine joint venture
  • Military Force Withdrawal: Russian forces would withdraw from Dnipropetrovsk, Mykolaiv, Sumy, and Kharkiv regions
  • International Monitoring: International forces would be stationed along the contact line to monitor implementation
  • Economic Provisions: Acceleration of U.S.-Ukraine free trade, with Ukraine seeking $800 billion in reconstruction funding through equity, grants, and loans
  • EU Accession: A specific date for Ukraine's ascension to the European Union

Remaining Obstacles

Critical Sticking Points:

The two most difficult issues remain:

  1. Donbas Control: Russia demands Ukraine relinquish all remaining territory in the Donbas that it hasn't captured. Russia controls most of Luhansk and about 70% of Donetsk. Zelenskyy has offered to put the 15% of Donbas still under Ukrainian control under a demilitarized zone, but Russia insists on full control.
  2. Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant: Management of Europe's largest nuclear facility, currently under Russian occupation, remains contentious. The U.S. proposes equal three-way ownership, while Ukraine opposes any joint commercial arrangement with Russia.

Official Positions

United States: Secretary of State Marco Rubio acknowledged progress but emphasized that "it's not our war"—a war on another continent that the U.S. cannot force either party to end. Vice President JD Vance offered a more sober assessment, stating he lacks "confidence" there will be a peaceful resolution: "I think there's a good chance we will, I think there's a good chance we won't."

Ukraine: President Zelenskyy has expressed willingness to withdraw troops from Ukraine's eastern industrial heartland as part of a peace plan if Moscow reciprocates by pulling back its forces and allowing the area to become a demilitarized zone monitored by international forces. He has also signaled readiness to drop Ukraine's NATO bid in exchange for robust Western security guarantees.

Russia: President Vladimir Putin maintains maximalist demands, including recognition of all captured territories in four regions plus Crimea, Ukraine's abandonment of NATO aspirations, limits on Ukraine's army size, and official status for the Russian language. The Kremlin has dismissed proposed European and Ukrainian amendments to the U.S. peace plan, stating they "do not improve the possibility of achieving long-term peace."

Military Situation

Current Battlefield Status

Russian forces have made incremental gains across multiple fronts. According to Putin's annual press conference, Russian troops have "fully seized strategic initiative" and are advancing "all across the line of contact." The Guardian reports Russian attempts to break through in the Sumy region, with fighting ongoing in the village of Grabovske and Russian troops having captured the nearby village of Vysoke.

Pokrovsk and the Donetsk "Fortress Belt"

The eastern town of Pokrovsk remains a focal point of Russian offensive operations. Once a key logistics hub for Ukraine's military, the town is now largely in ruins. Russian officials claim to have captured it (known in Russian as Krasnoarmeysk), which could provide a platform to drive north toward Kramatorsk and Slovyansk—the two biggest remaining Ukrainian-controlled cities in the Donetsk region.

However, the BBC notes that Russia has been trying to take Pokrovsk (about 23 square kilometers) for nearly two years, and the Institute for the Study of War suggests it would take Russian forces another two years to seize the remainder of the Donetsk region "at great cost."

Strategic Context

Russia is attempting to create a buffer zone inside Ukraine's northern borders to get within artillery range of Kharkiv, Ukraine's second-biggest city. This follows Ukraine's 2024 counter-offensive in Russia's Kursk region, where Ukrainian forces captured territory before being driven out with help from North Korean troops.

Ukrainian Deep Strikes

Ukraine has expanded its operational reach, striking deep inside Russian territory:

  • Drone attacks on air bases across five Russian regions (Murmansk, Irkutsk, Ivanovo, Ryazan, and Amur)
  • A massive oil depot fire near Sochi on the Black Sea
  • Recent strikes on oil tankers in what the New York Times describes as an effort to target Russia's "shadow fleet" and war financing

According to General Oleksandr Syrskyi, Ukraine's long-range strikes have cost the Russian economy more than $21.5 billion in 2025 alone.

Casualties

Military experts estimate between 165,000 and 235,000 Russian service personnel have been killed since the invasion. Ukraine last updated its casualty figures in December 2024, when President Zelenskyy acknowledged 43,000 Ukrainian deaths among soldiers and officers—a figure Western analysts believe to be an underestimate.

International Response

European Support

European Union leaders have agreed to provide a massive interest-free loan to Ukraine, though differences with Belgium prevented the use of frozen Russian assets to raise the funds. Leaders opted instead to borrow on capital markets. The EU has also pledged to provide security protection to Ukraine.

British Prime Minister Keir Starmer discussed efforts to achieve a "just and lasting end" to the war in a call with Donald Trump, focusing on the work of the "coalition of the willing" countries supporting Kyiv.

U.S. Intelligence Assessment

Reuters reports that U.S. intelligence continues to warn that "Putin has not abandoned his aims of capturing all of Ukraine and reclaiming parts of Europe that belonged to the former Soviet empire." The intelligence, dating from late September, contradicts Putin's denials that he is a threat to Europe. As Representative Mike Quigley noted: "The intelligence has always been that Putin wants more. The Europeans are convinced of it. The Poles are absolutely convinced of it. The Baltics think they're first."

NATO Dynamics

Zelenskyy has acknowledged that "The United States don't see us in NATO, for now," while emphasizing that Ukraine still believes NATO membership remains the best security guarantee. Ukraine has conceded its NATO bid as part of peace negotiations, seeking instead Article 5-like security guarantees from the U.S. and European partners.

Financial Projections

According to an American Enterprise Institute analysis cited by Small Wars Journal, a Russian victory would force the United States to spend an additional $808 billion over five years to reinforce NATO—nearly seven times the current $112 billion in U.S. support for Ukraine. This would create a 2,600-mile militarized frontier on Europe's eastern border.

Future Outlook

War Termination Scenarios

Small Wars Journal has published a comprehensive analysis comparing five historical war-termination models and their applicability to Ukraine:

  1. Burnout Model: Prolonged attrition until both sides are too exhausted to continue, typically restoring status quo rather than producing decisive victory. Carries risks of frozen lines and incomplete sovereignty restoration.
  2. Korean-Style Armistice: Military agreement suspending hostilities without political peace treaty, establishing a demarcation line and buffer zone. Would reduce attrition but at cost of territorial integrity and legitimization of occupation.
  3. Finlandization: Ukraine preserves statehood but concedes territory in exchange for sovereignty guarantee and strategic neutrality. Would require long-term self-restraint in foreign policy—a politically unlikely outcome given Ukrainian public opinion.
  4. India-Pakistan Recurring Crisis Model: Unresolved sovereignty disputes produce cycles of escalation, limited wars, and crisis bargaining rather than definitive settlement. Would trap Ukraine in instability and require constant Western mediation.
  5. Georgian Hybrid Occupation Scenario: Russia maintains partial control while cultivating pro-Russian government in Kyiv. Would mean formal independence paired with deep strategic vulnerability and weakened democratic institutions.

Strategic Implications

The Small Wars Journal analysis concludes that most endgames pose significant risks for Ukraine unless anchored in strong and enduring Western security guarantees. The analysis emphasizes:

  • Ukraine must retain full agency in shaping any settlement
  • Strategic clarity is essential—ambiguity breeds vulnerability
  • No settlement should legitimize territorial losses or transform de facto lines into de jure borders
  • Any settlement must be anchored in strong, institutionalized Western security guarantees
  • Ukraine must strengthen a resilient interim state while preserving the path to full territorial restoration

Time Horizons

Russian Expectations:

With 55% of Russians linking their optimism for 2026 to the war's end with Moscow's objectives achieved, there are signals that the Kremlin may be preparing public sentiment for a potential resolution. However, Putin's insistence on maximalist demands and his statement that Russian forces are advancing across all sectors suggest he believes time is on Russia's side.

Conversely, ISW analysts estimate it would take Russian forces another two years to seize the remainder of the Donetsk region "at great cost," suggesting Ukraine's fortified "fortress belt" defenses could significantly slow Russian advances even without immediate peace.

The Critical Question

As Small Wars Journal concludes: "The decisive question is not when the war ends, but what kind of order follows it." The analysis warns that Ukraine's future security trajectory and place within the Euro-Atlantic architecture will be determined by "the strategic quality of peace, not merely the cessation of hostilities."
