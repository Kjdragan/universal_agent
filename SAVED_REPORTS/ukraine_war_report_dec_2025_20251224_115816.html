Ukraine War Military Assessment: December 2025

Report Period: December 17-24, 2025
Prepared: December 24, 2025

Executive Summary

The war in Ukraine has entered a critical phase with intensified Russian offensive operations across multiple frontlines, particularly focusing on the capture of Pokrovsk and the consolidation of gains in Donetsk Oblast. Russian forces have made incremental territorial advances, gaining approximately 138 square miles in the period from November 25 to December 22, 2025. Meanwhile, Ukraine continues its strategic drone campaign against Russian infrastructure deep behind enemy lines. Diplomatic efforts led by the United States continue, with separate negotiations ongoing in Miami, though the Kremlin has shown little willingness to compromise on its core demands.

1. Recent Russian Military Operations and Strategies

Cognitive Warfare Campaign in Northern Ukraine

According to the Institute for the Study of War (ISW), Russian forces have launched a new "cognitive warfare campaign" through limited cross-border attacks in the previously dormant northern frontline areas of Sumy and Kharkiv oblasts. These attacks, which include the seizure of the small village of Hrabovske in Sumy Oblast, appear designed to create a narrative of Ukrainian frontline collapse to influence Western perceptions and ongoing peace negotiations.

Key characteristics of this campaign:

  • Limited tactical scope: Small-scale infiltrations (approximately 15 infantry in some cases) targeting rural border villages
  • Informational objectives: Attacks conducted with unusually large formations shoulder-to-shoulder, designed to be highly visible rather than tactically optimal
  • Buffer zone narrative: Putin referenced creating a "buffer zone" in Ukraine during his December 19 press conference, using these attacks to reinforce demands for territorial concessions

Assessment: ISW notes that Russian forces have not set conditions for a major new offensive across the international border. There is no evidence of large-scale force redeployment or the intermediate-range strikes against Ukrainian logistics that typically precede major Russian ground offensives.

Siversk Capture After 41 Months

Russian forces have likely seized the town of Siversk (pre-war population under 11,000) after 41 months of fighting. This represents an approximately 12-kilometer advance from Lysychansk, achieved at a very slow pace. Ukrainian resistance significantly delayed Russian efforts:

  • Verkhnokamyanske (4km east of Siversk) seized October 9, 2024
  • Bilohorivka (10km northeast) seized February 23, 2025
  • Serebryanka (4km northeast) seized August 16, 2025
  • Forces entered town limits by November 18, requiring 33 additional days to fully capture

Pokrovsk Offensive and Urban Warfare

The battle for Pokrovsk continues as a focal point of Russian offensive operations. Russian forces have become bogged down in urban warfare within the town, with Ukrainian forces conducting local counterattacks and retaking areas on the western outskirts. According to Ukrainian commander Brigadier General Yevhen Lasiychuk, Russian forces have lost at least 6,500 servicemembers in the Pokrovsk direction since September 1, 2025.

Increased Drone Warfare Operations

According to the Hudson Institute, Russia has significantly escalated its drone warfare campaign in the second half of 2025:

  • Doubled operational tempo: Launching approximately 10,000 drones per month at Ukrainian population centers and critical infrastructure
  • Technological innovations: New variants featuring thermobaric warheads, cluster-munition payloads, and R-60 air-to-air missiles
  • Mass deployment strategy: Reliance on low-cost airframes to impose sustained attrition at favorable cost ratios

2. Recent Ukrainian Military Responses and Actions

Strategic Drone Campaign Against Russian Infrastructure

Ukraine has dramatically expanded the geographic scope of its maritime pressure campaign, striking shadow fleet tankers in the Mediterranean Sea. In a multi-stage operation, Ukrainian drones struck the Oman-flagged crude oil tanker Vecsey, demonstrating an ability to project force far from Ukrainian territory.

Domestic Drone Production Surge

According to Ukrainian Defence Minister Denys Shmyhal, Ukraine's military will receive 3 million first-person-view (FPV) drones by the end of 2025 - nearly 2.5 times more than in 2024. The Defence Procurement Agency supplied 2.4 million FPV drones in 2025, with most produced domestically.

STING Counter-Drone System: Developed by the Wild Hornets volunteer group, this cost-effective Shahed-killer drone has successfully targeted thousands of Shahed drones. Ukraine plans to integrate such systems more closely into layered air defense networks in 2026.

Targeting Russian Energy Infrastructure

Ukrainian long-range strikes have caused significant damage to Russia's energy sector:

  • RFE/RL estimated at least 60 billion rubles ($655 million) in damage as of March 2025
  • Nearly 40% of Russia's oil refining capacity forced offline by October 2025
  • General Syrskyi reports strikes have cost the Russian economy more than $21.5 billion in 2025

Limited Cross-Border Attacks

Geolocated footage indicates Ukrainian forces recently advanced across the international border into a forest area northeast of Mukhin, Belgorod Oblast, during a limited cross-border attack.

3. Key Developments in the Conflict

Diplomatic Negotiations

December 19, 2025: Vladimir Putin held his annual "Direct Line" press conference, signaling no compromise on Ukraine's future. He rejected the EU's plan to use frozen Russian assets to fund Ukraine, calling it "robbery."

December 21, 2025: Separate US delegations continued meetings with Ukrainian and Russian delegations in Miami. Ukrainian Defense Council Secretary Rustem Umerov and Chief of the Ukrainian General Staff Major General Andriy Hnatov conducted a third day of meetings. Russian lead negotiator Kirill Dmitriev met with US Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and former Senior Advisor Jared Kushner.

December 19, 2025: EU leaders announced a 90 billion euro ($105 billion) interest-free loan for Ukraine to support military and economic needs through 2027, funded by borrowing rather than using frozen Russian assets.

US Administration Policy Shifts

The Trump administration has pursued an end to the war through negotiations, with a US-backed peace plan unveiled in November 2025. Under this plan:

  • Ukraine would cede control of all of Luhansk, Donetsk, and Crimea
  • Ukraine would withdraw from parts of Donetsk it still holds, creating a demilitarized zone
  • Russian forces would withdraw from small areas of Ukraine outside those regions

President Zelenskyy has consistently stated Ukraine will not hand over the Donbas in exchange for peace, though he has indicated possible flexibility on NATO membership if given bilateral security guarantees similar to NATO's Article 5.

North Korean Involvement

According to the Hudson Institute, North Korean support has been significant:

Spring 2025: Russian forces backed by the elite North Korean 11th Corps recaptured Kursk region territory that Ukraine had briefly held. This marked a significant escalation, with North Korea moving from arms transfers to direct combat involvement. In 2026, North Korea could further expand involvement and potentially deploy troops in Ukraine.

Forcible Deportations

December 20, 2025: Russian forcibly deported approximately 50 civilians from Hrabovske, Sumy Oblast to Russia. The victims were primarily elderly residents who had previously refused to evacuate. Forced deportation of residents from occupied areas across de jure international borders is illegal under the Fourth Geneva Convention.

5. International Support and Implications

European Union Support Package

90 Billion Euro Loan: EU leaders agreed to provide Ukraine with 90 billion euros ($105 billion) in interest-free loans for 2026-2027. The agreement was reached after difficult negotiations, with the EU choosing to borrow cash on capital markets rather than use frozen Russian assets due to legal concerns from countries like Belgium.

NATO Air Defense Commitments

France's President Emmanuel Macron announced that Ukraine will receive eight SAMP/T air defense systems. France and Italy are prioritizing this delivery. Macron stated work would continue from January in Paris to provide Ukraine with "solid security guarantees."

US Position Shifts

US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth stated that NATO membership for Ukraine is "unrealistic," fueling speculation that Washington and Moscow may negotiate over Ukraine's fate without consulting Kyiv. This has caused concern among European allies and Ukrainian officials.

China-Russia Cooperation Concerns

President Zelenskyy announced that Ukraine has recorded "increased ties between Russia and entities in China" that may be providing space-based intelligence data. He noted correlations between Chinese satellite imaging of Ukrainian territory and subsequent Russian strikes on energy infrastructure.

Strategic Outlook

Russian Advantages

  • Incremental territorial gains: Slow but steady advances in Donetsk Oblast, particularly around Pokrovsk
  • Superior drone numbers: Ability to launch mass drone attacks at doubled tempo compared to 2024
  • North Korean support: Combat troops and equipment contributing to Russian operations
  • Energy exports: Shadow fleet transporting ~3.7 million barrels of oil per day, generating $87-100 billion annually

Ukrainian Advantages

  • Domestic drone production: 3 million FPV drones projected for 2025, effective counter-drone systems
  • Strategic deep strike capability: Ability to hit Russian energy infrastructure and even Mediterranean shipping
  • Western financial support: New EU loan package providing stability through 2027
  • Fortress Belt defenses: ISW describes a 50km "fortress belt" through western Donetsk with significant defensive infrastructure

Key Assessment

ISW assesses that despite Russian advances, "the frontlines in Ukraine are not in danger of rapid collapse and that a Russian victory is not inevitable." Russian gains remain slow and grinding, with the capture of Siversk taking 41 months for a town of only 10 square kilometers. The Kremlin's cognitive warfare campaign appears designed to compensate for slow battlefield progress by creating a perception of Ukrainian collapse to influence Western diplomatic positions.
