Russia-Ukraine War Report

December 21-26, 2025

Comprehensive Analysis of Recent Developments

Executive Summary: As the Russia-Ukraine war approaches its fourth anniversary, US-led peace negotiations have intensified while fighting continues on multiple fronts. This week saw massive Russian airstrikes targeting Ukraine's energy infrastructure, productive but inconclusive peace talks in Miami, and growing Western skepticism about Vladimir Putin's true intentions. Key sticking points include territorial control of the Donbas region and management of the Zaporizhzhia nuclear plant. Meanwhile, US intelligence assessments warn that Putin's expansionist ambitions extend far beyond Ukraine's borders.

Peace Negotiations: Progress Amid Persistent Obstacles

US and Ukrainian negotiators have reached consensus on approximately 90% of a 20-point peace proposal following marathon talks in Florida, according to multiple sources. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy described the progress as "quite solid" while acknowledging that "key differences remain."

The Deal So Far: According to NPR's reporting, the draft proposal includes:
  • Security guarantees for Ukraine mirroring NATO's Article 5
  • A contact line freeze across five Ukrainian regions
  • Russian withdrawal from Dnipropetrovsk, Mykolaiv, Sumy, and Kharkiv regions
  • International forces stationed along the contact line
  • Accelerated US-Ukraine free trade agreement
  • An $800 billion reconstruction package

However, two critical issues remain unresolved: territorial control in the Donbas region and management of the Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant. Russia maintains maximalist demands, insisting Ukraine relinquish all remaining territory in the Donbas—an ultimatum Kyiv has rejected.

Conflicting US Signals

The Trump administration has sent mixed signals about the prospects for peace. Secretary of State Marco Rubio stated Friday that negotiators have made "progress" but emphasized that "we have a ways to go" and that "it's not our war." Meanwhile, Vice President JD Vance struck a more pessimistic note, saying he lacks "confidence" there will be a peaceful resolution: "I think there's a good chance we will, I think there's a good chance we won't."

Peace envoy Steve Witkoff, following meetings with both Ukrainian and Russian negotiators in Miami, described the talks as "constructive and productive" but offered no concrete breakthroughs. The Kremlin has been less positive, with spokesman Dmitry Peskov characterizing a peace deal as a "working progress" rather than a turning point.

Military Developments: Intensified Fighting and Strategic Shifts

While diplomats talked in Florida, the war on the ground intensified significantly. On December 23, Russia launched its largest airstrike in months, firing more than 650 drones and three dozen missiles at 13 regions across Ukraine. The attack killed at least three civilians, including a four-year-old child in the Zhytomyr region, and forced emergency power cuts amid freezing winter temperatures.

Christmas Eve Attack: Ukrainian Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko condemned the "deliberate and cynical" attack on the eve of Christmas, noting that energy infrastructure in western Ukraine was "hit the hardest." The drone barrage was so extensive that Poland, a NATO member, scrambled fighter jets to secure its airspace.

On the ground, Russian forces are pressing advantages in multiple sectors. The Ukrainian army is battling an attempted Russian breakthrough in the Sumy region, with ongoing fighting in the village of Grabovske. Russia has reportedly captured the nearby village of Vysoke and forcibly moved approximately 50 civilians from Grabovske into Russia, according to Ukraine's human rights ombudsman.

Ukrainian forces have withdrawn from the eastern town of Siversk, located in Donetsk region, after what the General Staff described as a "significant numerical advantage" and "constant pressure from small assault groups in difficult weather conditions." The withdrawal was described as a measure to preserve lives and resources.

Maritime Theater Expansion

Ukraine has demonstrated a willingness to expand the conflict into the maritime domain. A Ukrainian drone attack damaged two vessels and two piers at the Volna terminal on Russia's Black Sea coast in Krasnodar region, sparking a fire that spread across 1,500 square meters. This follows earlier attacks on oil tankers—part of Russia's "shadow fleet"—signaling Kyiv's intent to target Moscow's war financing infrastructure.

Putin's Imperial Ambitions: Beyond Ukraine

Perhaps the most alarming development this week comes from US intelligence assessments, reported by Reuters, indicating that Vladimir Putin "has not abandoned his aims of capturing all of Ukraine and reclaiming parts of Europe that belonged to the former Soviet empire." The intelligence, dating from late September, contradicts Putin's public denials that he threatens Europe.

"The intelligence has always been that Putin wants more. The Europeans are convinced of it. The Poles are absolutely convinced of it. The Baltics think they're first."

— Rep. Mike Quigley (D-IL), House Intelligence Committee

The Atlantic Council provides detailed analysis of Putin's expansionist rhetoric. In a December 17 address to Russian Defense Ministry officials, Putin declared that the maximalist goals of his Ukraine invasion would be met "in their entirety" and framed the war as a crusade to reverse Russia's post-Soviet retreat. "If the opposing side and their foreign patrons refuse to engage in substantive discussions, Russia will achieve the liberation of its historical lands by military means," Putin stated.

The analysis notes that Putin has referenced "Novorossiya"—a Czarist-era term encompassing approximately half of Ukraine including major cities like Odesa and Kharkiv. More broadly, Putin has stated that "the Soviet Union is historical Russia" and called its collapse "the disintegration of historical Russia." This vision could potentially extend to Finland, Poland, the Baltic states, Moldova, the Caucasus, and Central Asia.

The Warning: As the Atlantic Council analysis concludes: "Clearly, this would not be enough to placate Putin and cannot serve as the basis for a sustainable settlement... After four years of fighting to reverse the verdict of the Cold War, any peace deal that safeguards Ukrainian independence would be recognized in Moscow as a Russian defeat of historic proportions."

Humanitarian Impact: War's Toll on Civilians

The human cost of the war remains staggering. The December 23 attacks targeted residential buildings across Ukraine, damaging apartment blocks in Kyiv and killing civilians in multiple regions. The four-year-old child killed in Zhytomyr region exemplifies how Russia's drone warfare increasingly threatens non-combatants.

Beyond immediate casualties, the attacks on energy infrastructure threaten to leave Ukrainian families without electricity, heat, and safety during winter months. Emergency power cuts have been implemented across the country as repair crews work to stabilize the energy system.

According to DW's reporting, Ukrainians are marking their fourth Christmas under fire. Despite the onslaught, Christmas markets have opened in Kyiv, Odesa, and Kharkiv—symbols of resilience in the face of sustained aggression.

War Termination Scenarios: What History Teaches

As negotiations continue, analysts are examining historical models for how the war might end. The Small Wars Journal published a comprehensive analysis comparing five war-termination scenarios:

  • Burnout Model (Iran-Iraq War 1980-1988): Both sides become too exhausted to continue, but this produces frozen lines and incomplete sovereignty restoration.
  • Korean-Style Armistice: Military suspension of hostilities without political settlement, creating a demilitarized buffer zone.
  • Finlandization (Winter War 1939-1940): Ukraine preserves statehood but concedes territory and accepts strategic neutrality.
  • India-Pakistan Recurring Crisis: Unresolved sovereignty disputes produce cycles of limited wars and crisis bargaining.
  • Georgian Hybrid Occupation: Russia maintains partial occupation while cultivating a pro-Russian government in Kyiv.

The analysis concludes that most endgames pose significant risks for Ukraine "unless anchored in strong and enduring Western security guarantees." The decisive question, according to the author, is "not when the war ends, but what kind of order follows it."

European and NATO Response

NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte warned that Europe faces growing threats if support for Ukraine is reduced, emphasizing that Kyiv's strength is imperative to prevent Putin from attempting to attack a NATO member. "If we do those two things, we are strong enough to defend ourselves, and Putin will never try (to attack a NATO member)," Rutte said.

Germany's Foreign Minister echoed these concerns, stating that Russia would continue to pose a threat regardless of any ceasefire. He urged allies to "prepare ourselves for the possibility" of Russian aggression against NATO, noting that Germany is boosting military spending and forces in response.

Meanwhile, British Prime Minister Keir Starmer discussed efforts to achieve a "just and lasting end" to the war in a call with Donald Trump, focusing on the work of the "coalition of the willing" countries supporting Kyiv.

Looking Ahead: Prospects for 2026

As the war potentially enters its fifth year in February 2026, analysts offer varying predictions. Some, like former Ukrainian Deputy Chief of Staff Ihor Romanenko, believe the only realistic achievement might be a "pause" in the conflict rather than a full peace. "There won't be peace with Russia until we liberate the lands within Ukraine's [post-Soviet] 1991 borders," he said.

Volodymyr Fesenko of the Kyiv-based Penta think tank suggested a "window of opportunity" for a peace deal might emerge in the second half of 2026 if Russia fails to breach the front line and realizes Ukraine can sustain a war of attrition. However, he warned that even if Putin agrees to terms, it would take months to finalize arrangements.

One analyst, Nikolay Mitrokhin of Germany's Bremen University, offered a more pessimistic assessment, suggesting Ukraine might be forced out of the remaining one-fifth of southeastern Donetsk region and recognize the loss of significant territory in Zaporizhia and Dnipropetrovisk regions.

What remains clear is that the war's outcome will shape European security for decades. As one American Enterprise Institute assessment cited in the Small Wars Journal noted, a Russian victory would force the United States to spend an additional $808 billion over five years to reinforce NATO—nearly seven times current US support for Ukraine—creating a 2,600-mile militarized frontier on Europe's eastern border.
