Reporting Period: December 19-26, 2025

Russia-Ukraine War: Comprehensive Situation Report

Weekly Developments, Military Analysis, and Diplomatic Updates

Executive Summary

The week of December 19-26, 2025, marked a critical period in the Russia-Ukraine war, characterized by intensified Russian airstrikes on Ukrainian civilian infrastructure, continued heavy fighting along multiple frontlines, and significant diplomatic developments as US-led peace negotiations advanced. The conflict entered its fourth winter with no immediate end in sight, as both sides maintained maximalist positions despite ongoing shuttle diplomacy.

Escalating Air Campaign: Russia launched its largest drone and missile barrage of the winter, striking 13 Ukrainian regions with over 650 drones and three dozen missiles, causing massive power outages and civilian casualties during Christmas week.
Diplomatic Momentum: US and Ukrainian negotiators reached agreement on a 20-point peace plan that will be presented to Moscow, though key territorial and security guarantee issues remain unresolved.
Territorial Shifts: Russian forces gained approximately 51 square miles of territory in the past week, while Ukrainian forces achieved tactical successes near Kupyansk, highlighting the fluid nature of the frontline.
Humanitarian Crisis: Ukrainian energy infrastructure suffered catastrophic damage, leaving hundreds of thousands without power, heat, or water during freezing winter temperatures.
Strategic Implications: Intelligence assessments indicate Putin's war aims remain unchanged, with continued Russian demands for Ukrainian withdrawal from all occupied territories and abandonment of NATO aspirations.

Key Military Developments

Russian Aerial Offensive Intensifies

In what Ukrainian officials termed a "deliberate and cynical" attack on the eve of Christmas, Russian forces launched their most extensive aerial campaign of the winter on December 23-24. According to the Ukrainian Air Force, Russia fired more than 650 drones and three dozen missiles across 13 regions of Ukraine in attacks that stretched from nighttime through daylight hours.

Civilian Casualties: At least three civilians were killed, including a 4-year-old child who died when a Russian drone struck a residential building in the Zhytomyr region. Other fatalities were reported in Kyiv and Khmelnytskyi regions.

The attacks targeted critical infrastructure on an unprecedented scale, with Ukrainian Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko condemning the timing as "an attempt to leave Ukrainian families without electricity, heat, and a sense of safety" during the holiday period. The strikes forced emergency power cuts across multiple regions as temperatures dropped below freezing.

Frontline Dynamics and Territorial Changes

Territorial Control Update (Dec 16-22, 2025):
  • Russian gains: 51 square miles (approximately half the size of Nantucket Island)
  • Monthly average for 2025: 176 square miles per month
  • Total Russian-controlled territory: 19.2% of Ukraine (44,810 square miles)
  • Ukrainian foothold in Kursk/Belgorod: 3 square miles (unchanged)

Notable Frontline Developments:

  • Kupyansk Direction: In a significant development, Ukrainian forces achieved notable successes, encircling up to two understrength Russian companies in central and southern Kupyansk. According to Ukrainian military observer Kostyantyn Mashovets, Russian forces are unable to adequately supply their encircled troops, who are reportedly without food and running low on weapons and ammunition.
  • Siversk Withdrawal: Ukrainian forces withdrew from the embattled eastern town of Siversk (pre-war population approximately 10,000). The Ukrainian General Staff stated the withdrawal was necessary to preserve lives and resources amid constant pressure from Russian assault groups exploiting difficult weather conditions. This withdrawal brings Russian forces closer to the last remaining "fortress belt" cities of Slovyansk and Kramatorsk.
  • Pokrovsk Fighting: Intense fighting continued around Pokrovsk, where Russian forces have established positions within the town. Ukrainian officials reported controlling northern Pokrovsk while maintaining the ability to conduct rotations. In a significant combat action, Ukrainian forces destroyed at least eight tanks, 18 infantry fighting vehicles, five armored personnel carriers, and eleven motorcycles during a Russian company-sized mechanized assault near Fedorivka.
  • Hulyaipole Advances: Russian forces made gains in southern Hulyaipole and advanced to the T-0401 highway in Varvarivka (north of Hulyaipole), though these advances came at significant cost and remain tenuous.
  • Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka Area: Both Ukrainian and Russian forces made tactical advances in this sector, with Ukrainian forces advancing in northern Kleban-Byk and Russian forces pushing southeast of Stepanivka.

Ukrainian Strike Campaign Continues

Ukrainian forces continued their long-range strike campaign against Russian military and energy infrastructure:

  • Struck the Tamanneftegaz oil terminal in Krasnodar Krai, damaging a pipeline, two berths, and two vessels, causing a fire covering more than 1,000 square meters
  • Targeted the Yefremov Synthetic Rubber Plant in Tula Oblast, which produces components for explosives and solid rocket fuel
  • Hit a Shahed drone warehouse and launch site near occupied Donetsk City
  • Struck a storage facility for marine drones in occupied Crimea
  • Attacked the Novoshakhtinsk oil refinery in Russia's Rostov region using Storm Shadow missiles
  • Continued successful operations against Russia's shadow fleet, with the SBU striking the QENDIL tanker in the Mediterranean Sea

Asymmetric Warfare Behind Russian Lines

Ukrainian security services continued their campaign of targeted assassinations against high-ranking Russian military officials:

  • Lieutenant General Fanil Sarvarov, chief of the Russian General Staff's Operational Training Department, was killed in a car bombing in Moscow City on December 22
  • This followed the December 17 assassination of Lieutenant General Igor Kirillov, head of Russian Nuclear, Biological, Chemical Defense Forces
  • An explosion in Moscow on December 25 killed three people, including two police officers, with Ukrainian military intelligence (GUR) claiming responsibility

Political and Diplomatic Updates

The 20-Point Peace Plan

In what represents the most significant diplomatic development of the week, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy revealed details of a new 20-point peace plan agreed upon by US and Ukrainian negotiators following marathon talks in Florida. The plan, which will be presented to Russian officials, marks an evolution from the earlier 28-point proposal and includes several key provisions:

Key Elements of the 20-Point Plan:
  • Territory: The frontline would be frozen along current lines upon signing, with no requirement for Ukraine to withdraw from unoccupied Donbas
  • Military: Ukraine's peacetime military would be capped at 800,000 personnel (increased from 600,000 in the earlier proposal)
  • Security Guarantees: Strong security guarantees mirroring NATO's Article 5, with a separate bilateral US-Ukraine document outlining response mechanisms
  • Economic Recovery: $800 billion in reconstruction funding through equity, grants, loans, and private sector contributions
  • NATO Membership: The plan does not explicitly prohibit Ukraine's NATO membership aspirations (a key change from the 28-point version)

Unresolved Issues

Despite progress on many fronts, Zelenskyy acknowledged that critical issues remain unresolved:

  • Donetsk Region: The future of Ukrainian-controlled parts of Donetsk Oblast remains the most difficult point. Russia controls about 75% of Donetsk and 99% of neighboring Luhansk. The US has proposed transforming contested areas into free economic zones or demilitarized zones monitored by international forces.
  • Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant: Control of Europe's largest nuclear facility remains contentious. The US proposed a three-way consortium (Ukraine, US, and Russia) with equal 33% stakes, while Ukraine countered with a US-Ukraine joint venture proposal. Any solution would require demilitarization of the facility's territory.
  • Security Guarantees: Technical details about enforcement mechanisms and monitoring systems remain under discussion.

Russian Position and Demands

The Kremlin has signaled that it will reject many provisions of the new proposal, maintaining its commitment to the maximalist demands Putin outlined in his June 2024 speech:

Russia's Core Demands (Unchanged):
  • Complete Ukrainian withdrawal from all of Luhansk, Donetsk, Zaporizhia, and Kherson oblasts
  • Ukraine's abandonment of NATO membership aspirations and commitment to permanent neutrality
  • Ukrainian "demilitarization" (preventing Ukraine from defending itself)
  • "Denazification" (replacement of Ukraine's government with a pro-Russian puppet regime)
  • International recognition of Russia's annexations of the four oblasts and Crimea
  • Lifting of all Western sanctions against Russia

Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov stated that Russia does not support a temporary ceasefire and called for a permanent settlement that guarantees the results of what Russia terms its "special military operation." Kremlin officials have repeatedly referenced Putin's June 2024 speech and alleged understandings from the August 2025 Alaska Summit, signaling their rejection of proposals that freeze current lines in Zaporizhia and Kherson oblasts.

US Intelligence Assessment

According to a Reuters report citing US intelligence assessments, Putin's war aims remain unchanged. US intelligence continues to warn that the Russian president has not abandoned his objectives of capturing all of Ukraine and reclaiming parts of the former Soviet Empire. The assessment indicates that despite ongoing negotiations, Putin remains committed to achieving his original war goals.

European Support

European allies continue to back Ukraine:

  • The EU agreed to provide Ukraine with a 90 billion euro interest-free loan for 2026-2027, which Ukraine would only need to repay if Russia pays reparations for the war
  • NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte warned that reducing support for Ukraine could encourage Putin to attack NATO members
  • German Foreign Minister stated that Russia would continue threatening European security regardless of any ceasefire and urged allies to maintain pressure on Moscow

Peace Talks Timeline and Assessment

Current Status of Negotiations:
  • US and Ukrainian teams: 90% complete on "first developments" with a concrete but unfinalized 20-point plan
  • Russia: Has rejected the Christmas ceasefire proposal and signaled rejection of many Ukrainian/European counterproposals
  • Key sticking point: Russia insists on complete Ukrainian withdrawal from all occupied territories, which Ukraine rejects
  • US Vice President JD Vance noted unresolved issues about Ukrainian-controlled parts of Donetsk Oblast and control of the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant

Humanitarian Impact

Energy Infrastructure Catastrophe

Ukraine's energy infrastructure suffered catastrophic damage during the week's bombardment:

  • Western Ukraine: Energy facilities in western regions were "hit the hardest" according to Prime Minister Svyrydenko
  • Chernihiv Oblast: Tens of thousands of residents lost power after strikes on critical infrastructure
  • Kharkiv City: A thermal power plant was struck, killing at least one person and injuring 13, while forcing the city to switch to "energy islands" for power provision
  • Odesa Oblast: Approximately 159,000 people left without electricity and 180,000 without heat following strikes on critical infrastructure
  • National Impact: About 180,000 consumers across five Ukrainian regions (Mykolaiv, Zaporizhia, Cherkasy, Dnipropetrovsk, and Sumy) lost power
Systemic Damage: According to ISW assessments, Russia's ongoing strike campaign aims to split Ukraine's power grid east-west, with eastern regions "at the brink" of blackout and Kyiv also at risk. Reports indicate Kyiv residents face up to 16 hours daily without power due to infrastructure damage.

Civilian Casualties and Displacement

The week's attacks resulted in numerous civilian casualties beyond those previously mentioned:

  • A woman was killed when a Russian drone struck her car while crossing a bridge in Odesa; three children were injured in the same incident
  • In Kherson and Sumy oblasts, at least eight people were wounded, including a 17-year-old girl and a 20-year-old man
  • In Zaporizhzhia region, 705 Russian strikes on 25 settlements injured five people in the city and damaged hundreds of buildings and vehicles

Environmental Consequences

The environmental impact of the fighting continues to escalate:

  • A sunflower oil spill caused by Russian aerial bombardments contaminated the shoreline around Odesa, killing wildlife and triggering warnings from conservationists
  • The Pivdenny port in Odesa region was temporarily closed to facilitate cleanup operations
  • Damage to energy infrastructure has created long-term environmental risks that will persist beyond the conflict

Prisoner Exchanges and Detainees

The humanitarian situation extends to those detained by both sides:

  • A Russian-backed court in occupied Donetsk sentenced a Colombian man, Oscar Mauricio Blanco Lopez, to 19 years in prison for fighting with Ukrainian forces
  • A British man, Hayden Davies, was sentenced to 13 years in a maximum security prison camp for fighting for Ukraine
  • Ukraine is demanding the release of all prisoners held since 2014, along with civilian detainees, political prisoners, and children

Analysis and Outlook

Short-Term Prospects (Winter 2025-2026)

The coming months present several critical challenges and potential scenarios:

Military Trajectory:
  • Russia will likely continue incremental advances at high cost, averaging approximately 176 square miles per month if current patterns hold
  • Ukrainian forces face manpower and ammunition shortages that may limit their ability to conduct major counteroffensives
  • Weather conditions will continue to favor defensive operations while limiting mechanized maneuver
  • Both sides are likely to prioritize striking infrastructure over territorial gains during the winter months

The Fortress Belt Challenge

Russian forces continue their push toward Ukraine's heavily fortified "Fortress Belt" cities in Donetsk Oblast:

  • Current Russian Strategy: Multi-pronged offensives toward Slovyansk, Kramatorsk, Druzhkivka, and Kostyantynivka—four cities that form the backbone of Ukrainian defenses in Donbas since 2014
  • Ukrainian Assessment: Ukrainian military analyst Ihor Romanenko notes that the "only realistic achievement" might be a temporary "pause" in the war rather than a complete end to hostilities
  • Resource Constraints: Russian milbloggers acknowledge that Russia lacks sufficient manpower and materiel to imminently defeat the northern part of the Fortress Belt while simultaneously continuing offensive operations elsewhere

Economic Pressures and Sustainability

Ukrainian Economic Situation:
  • Foreign aid shortfall of $45-50 billion projected for 2026
  • Domestic arms production now provides 40% of military needs (up from 15-20% in 2022)
  • Successfully restructured $2.6 billion in growth-linked debt
  • Energy infrastructure operating at approximately 52% of pre-invasion capacity

On the Russian side, economic indicators show increasing strain:

  • US intelligence reports indicate actual Russian inflation may exceed 20% (far above official claims of 5.6-5.8%)
  • Analyst predictions suggest 2026 will be the "first difficult year" since the full-scale invasion began
  • Russia's Central Bank has begun selling gold reserves for the first time to fund the war budget
  • Labor shortages have driven unemployment to an artificially low 2.2%, contributing to wage inflation

Scenarios for 2026

Analysts have outlined several potential scenarios for how the conflict might evolve in 2026:

Possible Endgame Scenarios:

1. "Finnish Scenario" (Worst Case for Ukraine): Similar to the 1939 Finnish-Soviet war, Ukraine would be forced to recognize Moscow-occupied regions as part of Russia and cede approximately one-fifth of southeastern Donetsk region plus territories in Zaporizhia and Dnipropetrovsk.

2. "Georgian Scenario": Ukraine loses control over occupied areas but refuses to recognize them as Russian territory, creating a frozen conflict similar to the situation in Georgia's Abkhazia and South Ossetia regions since 2008.

3. "Interim Scenario": The war is frozen along current lines with ongoing diplomatic talks but no permanent resolution.

4. Negotiated Settlement: A comprehensive peace agreement based on the current 20-point plan or a modified version emerges following successful pressure on Russia or a change in Kremlin calculations.

Factors That Could Shift the Conflict

  • Western Unity: Maintenance of sanctions pressure and military aid flows is critical to Ukraine's ability to resist
  • Russian Domestic Politics: Economic pain or casualties could impact Putin's ability to continue the war at current intensity
  • Ukrainian Mobilization: Ukraine's ability to mobilize additional manpower and resources will determine its defensive capacity
  • US Political Dynamics: The Trump administration's approach and congressional support for Ukraine will heavily influence outcomes
  • European Security Architecture: Long-term arrangements for European security beyond NATO could become part of settlement discussions

Strategic Assessment: Putin's Imperial Ambitions

A sobering analysis from the Atlantic Council's Peter Dickinson warns that Putin's imperial ambitions extend far beyond Ukraine. In a December 17 address to Russian Defense Ministry officials, Putin framed the war as a crusade to "reverse Russia's post-Soviet retreat" and reclaim "historical Russian lands."

Warning Signs:
  • Putin has stated he considers "all of Ukraine" to be historically Russian lands
  • The Kremlin's definition of "historical Russia" includes the entire former Soviet Union and Tsarist Empire
  • Potential future targets could include Finland, Poland, the Baltic states, Belarus, Moldova, Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan, and Central Asian nations
  • Any peace settlement that leaves Ukraine independent would be viewed by Moscow as "a Russian defeat of historic proportions"

The analysis concludes that "handing Putin victory in Ukraine would almost certainly mean more war and lead to decades of European instability." This assessment underscores why Ukraine and its European allies insist on robust security guarantees as a non-negotiable element of any settlement.

Critical Questions for the Coming Months

  1. Will Russia accept any compromise short of its maximalist demands? Current indications suggest no, but economic or military pressures could change Kremlin calculations.
  2. Can Ukraine sustain its defensive operations without additional mobilization? Manpower shortages remain acute, and current casualty rates may not be sustainable long-term.
  3. Will Western support remain consistent through 2026? Political transitions in Washington and European elections could affect aid flows.
  4. How will the energy warfare campaign affect Ukrainian civilian resilience? Winter infrastructure damage will test Ukrainian society's endurance.
  5. What security guarantees would actually deter future Russian aggression? This remains the core question for any viable peace agreement.

Concluding Assessment

The week of December 19-26, 2025, demonstrated both the possibilities and limitations of diplomatic progress amid ongoing intense fighting. While the 20-point peace plan represents movement toward a framework that could potentially end the war, the gap between Russian demands and Ukrainian red lines remains substantial.

Military developments on the ground continue to favor Russia in terms of territorial gains, but Ukrainian forces retain the ability to conduct effective defensive operations and strikes against Russian infrastructure. The humanitarian situation, particularly regarding energy infrastructure, continues to deteriorate with severe implications for Ukrainian civilians.

As the conflict enters its fifth year in February 2026, the most likely scenarios involve either a frozen conflict along current lines with ongoing diplomatic efforts, or continued incremental Russian advances at high cost to both sides. A comprehensive peace settlement remains possible but would require either significant Western pressure on Russia or a fundamental shift in Kremlin calculations about the cost-benefit balance of continuing the war.

What remains clear is that any settlement that does not provide Ukraine with robust, enforceable security guarantees risks setting the stage for renewed conflict in the future—with potentially catastrophic consequences for European security architecture and international stability more broadly.
