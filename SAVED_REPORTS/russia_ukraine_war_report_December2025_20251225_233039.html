Russia-Ukraine War Report

Key Developments, Peace Talks, and Territorial Claims

December 2025 | Day 1,400-1,401 of the Full-Scale Invasion

Executive Summary

The war in Ukraine enters its fourth Christmas with intensified diplomatic activity but no breakthrough in sight. US-led peace talks have produced a 20-point framework agreed between Washington and Kyiv, but Russian officials have signaled that European and Ukrainian amendments to the original US proposal "do not improve the prospects for peace." Meanwhile, Russian forces continue grinding advances on the battlefield, claiming over 5,100 square kilometers of territory captured in 2025 alone, while Ukrainian forces strike deep into Russian territory with drones and missiles.

Peace Negotiations: The 20-Point Framework

Zelenskyy's Territorial Compromise

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy has revealed details of a proposed compromise for Russian-occupied areas of eastern Ukraine. Under the US-Ukrainian framework, Ukraine would withdraw troops from the portion of Donetsk it still controls, converting it into a "demilitarized or free economic zone". Zelenskyy stated that this option would be determined by a referendum, with only Ukrainians living in Ukrainian territory permitted to vote—effectively excluding those under Russian occupation.

Key conditions for the Donetsk proposal:

  • A ceasefire first, followed by 60 days to prepare the referendum
  • Ukrainian police would remain in the designated zone
  • Only Ukrainians in Ukrainian territory could vote (those under occupation excluded)
  • International forces must be present to guarantee security

Zelenskyy presented the choice starkly: either Ukrainians agree to the entire peace plan, including converting land fought for over 11 years into this special zone, or reject it and continue the war without security guarantees from the US or Europe.

Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant Dispute

A major sticking point remains the fate of the Zaporizhzhia nuclear power plant, Europe's largest, which Russia has occupied since the early days of the full-scale invasion. The US plan reportedly proposed joint management of the plant with power split between the US, Ukraine, and Russia. Zelenskyy firmly rejected this, stating:

"Clearly, for Ukraine, this sounds very inappropriate and not entirely realistic. How can there be joint commercial activity with the Russians after everything that has happened? We are enemies."

Ukraine's counterproposal demands Russia's complete withdrawal from the plant, with Ukraine and the US splitting ownership 50/50.

Russian Rejection of Amendments

Yury Ushakov, Putin's top foreign policy aide, criticized changes made by European countries and Ukraine to the US-drafted proposals:

"I am sure that the proposals that the Europeans and Ukrainians have made or are trying to make definitely do not improve the document and do not improve the possibility of achieving long-term peace."

Russia has also firmly rejected the presence of foreign troops on the front line, which Ukraine and European negotiators have insisted upon as part of any security guarantee framework. The Kremlin denies that trilateral talks involving Ukraine, Russia, and the US are under discussion, despite Zelenskyy's statements about Washington proposing three-way discussions.

US Leadership and Pessimism

Trump administration officials have offered mixed assessments of the peace prospects. Special envoy Steve Witkoff and Trump's son-in-law Jared Kushner have led talks in Miami and Florida, with Witkoff calling meetings "constructive and productive." However, Vice President JD Vance struck a far more sober tone:

"I think there's a good chance we will [reach peace], I think there's a good chance we won't."

Vance's assessment represents one of the most pessimistic public statements from a high-ranking US official to date, highlighting that the "thorniest issues have never been resolved."

Territorial Situation and Battlefield Developments

Conflicting Claims on Territory

Russian President Vladimir Putin claimed during his year-end news conference that Moscow's forces had seized significant territory in the eastern Donetsk region and Vovchansk in the northern Kharkiv region. He stated that Russia held at least half of Lyman and Kostiantynivka in Donetsk, and Hulyaipole in southern Zaporizhia.

Russian claims vs. independent assessments:

  • Russian Defence Minister Andrei Belousov: 6,000 sq km captured in 2025
  • Russian commander-in-chief Valery Gerasimov: 6,300 sq km captured
  • Institute for the Study of War (ISW): No more than 4,984 sq km (1,900 sq miles) containing 196 settlements

The ISW stated that satellite and open-source visual evidence contradict Putin's claims, observing no evidence to confirm "extensive advances" in the towns he mentioned. Even Russian milbloggers' claimed advances do not support many of Putin's assertions, with maximum estimates of around 7-11 percent control in disputed areas.

Measurable Russian Advances

Despite the exaggeration, Russia is making measurable gains. According to Russia Matters' analysis of ISW data:

Territorial control (Nov 25–Dec 22, 2025):

  • Russian forces gained 138 square miles of Ukrainian territory in this period
  • In the past six days (Dec 16–22): 51 square miles gained
  • Since January 1, 2025: Average of 176 square miles gained per month
  • Russia presently controls approximately 45,625 square miles (about 20% of Ukraine)

Russian forces have taken control of Sviato-Pokrovske in eastern Donetsk and Siversk, where Ukrainian troops withdrew after heavy fighting. The Ukrainian military reported Russia's "significant advantage" in the Siversk area.

Ukrainian Strikes on Russian Infrastructure

Ukraine continues its campaign targeting Russian energy and military infrastructure deep behind enemy lines:

December 20: Ukrainian State Security Service (SBU) struck two Russian Su-27 fighter jets at Belbek airbase near occupied Sevastopol in Crimea. Two days earlier, Ukraine had weakened air defense systems at the base and hit a MiG-31 interceptor aircraft.

December 25 (Day 1,401): Long-range Ukrainian drones targeted oil storage facilities at Temryuk port in southern Russia's Krasnodar region, triggering a huge fire across roughly 2,000 square meters. Ukraine also struck a gas processing plant in Russia's Orenburg region and the Novoshakhtinsk oil refinery in Rostov region using Storm Shadow missiles.

December 18-24: Ukraine struck a Russian oil rig in the Caspian Sea belonging to Lukoil, damaging one of its drilling platforms. Earlier, on December 15, Ukraine claimed to have sunk a Russian submarine off Novorossiysk with an underwater drone (a claim Russia denied).

December 23 (Day 1,400): Ukraine's drone strikes hit the Yefremov synthetic rubber plant in Russia's Tula region, south of Moscow, and a storage facility for marine drones in Russian-occupied Crimea.

Escalating Drone Warfare

Both sides are rapidly expanding their drone capabilities. Ukraine is deploying inexpensive interceptor drones to counter Russia's aerial attacks on urban centers and energy infrastructure. These homegrown systems can intercept high-altitude suicide drones at a fraction of conventional missile costs.

According to Zelenskyy, over one week Russia launched approximately 1,300 attack drones, nearly 1,200 guided aerial bombs, and nine missiles of various types against Ukraine. In a single night (December 23), Russia launched 673 aerial targets against Ukraine, of which Ukraine's air defense forces destroyed or suppressed 621.

Casualties and Human Impact

The human cost continues to mount:

Notable incidents:

  • A Russian attack on Odesa killed at least eight people as peace talks continued
  • At least three people, including a four-year-old child, were killed and dozens wounded in a wave of Russian attacks on Ukraine on December 23
  • An explosion in Moscow killed three people, including two police officers; Ukraine's military intelligence (GUR) said it was a Ukrainian operation targeting officers involved in the war
  • Russia forcibly removed about 50 Ukrainian civilians from the village of Hrabovske in the Sumy border region to Russian territory

International Involvement

European Union Financial Support

European Union leaders agreed to provide 90 billion euros ($106 billion) to Ukraine for military and economic needs over the next two years. This decision came after Italy, Bulgaria, Malta, and Belgium vetoed a plan to use frozen Russian assets as collateral, requiring the EU to borrow from capital markets instead.

Zelenskyy told European leaders: "No one will ever be able to explain to European voters why Europe should give 200bn [$235bn] back to Putin – after everything he's destroyed."

The US-Ukrainian 20-point plan pledges to mobilize $800 billion for Ukraine's reconstruction. Separately, the EU has indicated Ukraine will become a full member in the near future, which would entitle it to mutual defense from most EU members (who are also NATO members).

Security Guarantees

A significant development is the US agreement to provide NATO-level security guarantees for Ukraine – a mutual defense clause that could bring NATO into a war on Ukraine's side if Russia attacks again. The plan also allows Ukraine to keep its military at full strength (up to 800,000 soldiers during peacetime) and does not call on it to recognize occupied territory as de facto Russian.

Notably, under the new framework Ukraine would no longer have to give up its NATO bid – a point on which Moscow had insisted and the US had included in its original proposal.

Putin's Year-End Press Conference

Vladimir Putin held his annual marathon news conference on December 19, lasting over four hours. Key statements included:

  • Russia remains "ready and willing" to end the war peacefully, but on principles laid out in June 2024 – demanding Ukraine withdraw from Donetsk and Luhansk and abandon NATO ambitions
  • He claimed to have "practically agreed" to Trump's proposals during their August summit in Alaska, but Russia has not budged on territorial demands
  • The EU's plan to use frozen Russian assets would amount to "robbery" and undermine trust in the Eurozone
  • Russia would not launch another "special military operation" if treated with "respect" by the West and NATO does not expand further
  • Russia is willing to consider a brief halt to strikes on Ukraine to allow elections

Critically, Putin dismissed Zelenskyy's visit to Kupiansk (which Russia claimed to have captured in November) by calling him "an artist, a talented artist" and taunting: "Why stand on the threshold? Come inside." The Institute for the Study of War reported "mounting evidence" that Ukrainian forces had liberated a significant portion of Kupiansk.

International Reactions

French President Emmanuel Macron said he would continue work with the "coalition of the willing" to provide Ukraine with solid security guarantees, stating in January Paris would continue these efforts as "a prerequisite for a robust and lasting peace."

Poland scrambled fighter jets to intercept a Russian reconnaissance aircraft near its airspace over the Baltic Sea and reported that dozens of objects entered Polish airspace from Belarus overnight, warning the incidents during the holiday season may signal a provocation.

Energy Infrastructure Damage

Ukraine's energy devastation:

  • Approximately 64% of electricity generating capacity destroyed or occupied as of 2024
  • Most thermal power generation capacity destroyed by May 2025
  • Most hydropower installations damaged or destroyed by May 2025
  • Energy infrastructure operating at only a fraction of pre-invasion capacity as of Fall 2025
  • Russian strikes destroyed most of Ukraine's gas production ahead of winter 2025-2026
  • Eastern regions "at the brink" of blackout, with Kyiv residents facing up to 16 hours daily without power

A sunflower oil spill caused by Russian bombardments contaminated the shoreline around Odesa, killing wildlife and forcing the temporary closure of the Pivdenny port for cleanup.

Energy Infrastructure Warfare

Both sides continue targeting energy infrastructure. Ukrainian drone strikes have forced nearly 40% of Russia's oil refining capacity offline, with at least 70% of shutdowns directly linked to these strikes according to Russian energy market sources. November Ukrainian drone strikes on the Caspian Pipeline Consortium terminal near Russia's Black Sea port of Novorossiysk left just one of three jetties operational.

Outlook and Projections

A Russian state poll (VTsIOM) found that 70% of 1,600 respondents saw 2026 as a more "successful" year for Russia than 2025, with 55% linking this hope to a possible end to the war. This suggests the Kremlin may be testing public reaction to a potential peace settlement.

However, fundamental disagreements remain unresolved. Russia demands Ukraine cede Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhia, Kherson, and Crimea in full. Ukraine refuses, while European leaders suggest leaving territorial discussions until after a full ceasefire.

The fundamental deadlock: Ukraine views Russian control of Donbas as a "major security problem," but as Vice President Vance noted, the Ukrainian military would likely "lose" the territory regardless if fighting persists. The "terrible territorial concession," as Vance called it, remains a significant hold-up in negotiations.

As Ukraine marks its fourth consecutive Christmas under full-scale invasion, Zelenskyy told his nation: "Ukrainians are together tonight... It hardly matters what dishes are on the table – what matters is who is at the table."
