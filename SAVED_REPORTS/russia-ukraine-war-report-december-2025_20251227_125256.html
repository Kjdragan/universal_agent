Russia-Ukraine War: Comprehensive Assessment Report

Strategic Analysis & Situation Update - December 27, 2025 (Day 1,402)

Executive Summary

The Russia-Ukraine war enters its fifth year in February 2026 with intensifying combat, accelerating diplomatic negotiations, and catastrophic humanitarian consequences. As of late December 2025, Russia has gained approximately 138 square miles of Ukrainian territory in the past month alone, averaging 176 square miles per month in 2025. Russian forces now occupy roughly 19-20% of Ukraine's territory, equivalent to the size of Pennsylvania or Ohio.

Simultaneously, historic peace negotiations are underway. A US-brokered 20-point peace plan has been presented to Russia, with Ukrainian President Zelenskyy scheduled to meet US President Trump on December 28, 2025, to finalize the framework. However, fundamental disagreements remain, particularly regarding territorial control of Donetsk Oblast and security guarantees for Ukraine.

Key Development: Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov stated on December 26 that the Kremlin "will not sign any peace agreements to end the war in Ukraine 'right now'", rejecting Ukraine's proposal and indicating Moscow's intent to continue fighting until its demands are met.

1. Current Situation Overview (December 2025)

Battlefield Dynamics

As of December 27, 2025 (day 1,402 of the full-scale invasion), the war is characterized by:

  • Slow Russian Advances: Russia is gaining territory gradually but at high cost—approximately 176 square miles per month on average in 2025. In the past six days alone (December 16-22), Russian forces gained 51 square miles (about half the size of Nantucket Island)
  • Multi-Front Fighting: Active combat continues across multiple axes: Kharkiv, Kupyansk, Lyman, Siversk, Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka, Pokrovsk, Novopavlivka, Huliaipole, Zaporizhia, and Kherson directions
  • Ukrainian Resilience: Despite being outmanned and outgunned, Ukrainian forces have demonstrated capability for effective counterattacks, notably in Kupyansk where they liberated territory and encircled approximately 200 Russian soldiers in mid-December

Escalating Strikes Campaign

Major Attack on Kyiv (December 27): Russia launched a massive drone and missile strike on Ukraine's capital, killing at least one person and leaving one-third of Kyiv without heat amid freezing temperatures. The attack involved approximately 500 drones and 40 missiles targeting energy facilities and civilian infrastructure, cutting power to more than 1 million homes in and around Kyiv.

Previous large-scale attacks include:

  • December 22-23: Russia launched 650 drones and 30 missiles across 13 Ukrainian regions, killing three people, including a four-year-old girl in Zhytomyr region
  • December 23-24: 116 drones targeted critical infrastructure in Chernihiv Oblast, causing tens of thousands of residents to lose power

Strategic Context

The war is at a critical juncture where military realities are colliding with diplomatic initiatives. While Russian forces continue grinding advances on the battlefield, parallel diplomatic tracks are attempting to negotiate an end to hostilities. The disconnect between Russia's battlefield momentum and its uncompromising diplomatic position creates a volatile situation with multiple possible trajectories.

4. Diplomatic Efforts & Peace Talks

The 20-Point Peace Plan

A US-brokered peace framework has emerged from talks between US, Ukrainian, and European representatives. Key elements include:

Core Provisions (Leaked Details):

  • Territory: Freeze frontlines in Luhansk, Donetsk, Zaporizhia, and Kherson oblasts as of signing date
  • Demilitarized Zone: Creation of a "free economic zone" in Ukrainian-held parts of Donetsk Oblast, subject to referendum
  • Military Limits: Ukraine's peacetime military capped at 800,000 personnel (increased from 600,000 in earlier versions)
  • Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant: US-Ukraine joint management proposed (50-50 split), Russia strongly objects
  • Security Guarantees: Detailed US response mechanisms if Russia attacks Ukraine again
  • Reconstruction: "Roadmap for Ukraine's Prosperity" outlining post-war economic development

Russia's Position

Strong Kremlin Rejection: Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov stated on December 26 that Russia "will not sign any peace agreements to end the war in Ukraine 'right now'". Key Russian demands include:

  • Ukraine's complete withdrawal from all of Luhansk, Donetsk, Zaporizhia, and Kherson oblasts
  • Ukraine's abandonment of NATO membership aspirations and commitment to neutrality
  • Ukraine's "demilitarization" (limits preventing self-defense) and "denazification" (regime change)
  • International recognition of Russia's annexations
  • Lifting of all Western sanctions

Russian officials have called the 20-point plan "radically different" from what Russia has discussed with the US and indicated no willingness to compromise on maximalist territorial demands. Kremlin spokesperson Peskov rejected a proposed Christmas ceasefire, and Moscow insists on addressing the "root causes" of the war as defined by Russia (NATO expansion, alleged discrimination against Russian speakers).

Upcoming Meetings

Trump-Zelenskyy Meeting (December 28, 2025):

  • Location: Mar-a-Lago, Florida
  • Agenda: Finalize 20-point plan, security guarantees, territorial issues, ZNPP control
  • Zelenskyy stated: "A lot can be decided before the New Year"
  • European leaders will join virtually

Referendum Proposal

Zelenskyy has proposed putting the peace plan to a Ukrainian referendum under strict conditions:

  • 60-day ceasefire to prepare for voting
  • International observers to monitor the process
  • Only Ukrainians in Ukrainian territory can vote (those in occupied areas excluded)
  • International forces must guarantee no one enters the demilitarized zone

However, Kremlin officials have explicitly rejected both the ceasefire and referendum proposals, calling them "deception" and "delay tactics."

Analysis: The fundamental incompatibility between positions remains stark. While Ukraine has shown flexibility (offering to withdraw from parts of Donetsk it still controls), Russia maintains maximalist demands for complete Ukrainian capitulation on territory, neutrality, and security architecture. The gap between the two positions appears difficult, if not impossible, to bridge.

5. Humanitarian Impact

Civilian Casualties

Recent Russian attacks have caused significant civilian harm:

  • December 27 (Kyiv attack): One 47-year-old woman killed, 22 wounded including two children
  • December 23 (Zhytomyr): Four-year-old girl killed; five others injured in same attack
  • December 22-23: Three people killed across multiple regions including a woman in Kyiv region and one person in Khmelnytskyi
  • December 25 (Moscow): Two police officers and one civilian killed in car explosion—claimed by Ukrainian intelligence

Energy Infrastructure Devastation

Critical Situation: Russia's systematic targeting of Ukraine's energy infrastructure has caused catastrophic damage:

  • 48% of Ukraine's pre-war power generation capacity has been destroyed or occupied
  • 64% of electricity generating capacity destroyed or occupied as of 2024
  • Nearly all thermal power generation in Ukraine has been destroyed
  • Hydropower: Some installations damaged and destroyed as of May 2025
  • Gas production: Russia destroyed 30% of Ukraine's gas production capacity ahead of winter 2025-2026
  • Consumption: Ukraine's overall electricity consumption has fallen by approximately two-thirds since the invasion

Energy Crisis in Cities

Kharkiv (December 23-24): Russian drone attack on thermal power plant killed at least one person, injured 13, and disrupted power generation, heat supplies, and transport. Mayor stated serious damage forced switch to "energy islands."

Kyiv (December 27): One-third of capital left without heat after Russian strikes; temperatures near freezing. More than 1 million homes lost power.

Chernihiv Oblast: Russian drone strikes (48 drones hit 19 locations) caused tens of thousands to lose power; strikes hit apartment buildings.

Displacement

Ukraine: 10.6 million displaced Ukrainians (24% of pre-war population of 44 million), including both internally displaced persons and refugees abroad.

Russia: Estimates indicate 900,000 to 1.5 million Russians have left the country for economic or political reasons (0.6% of population).

Environmental Damage

  • Sunflower Oil Spill (Odesa): Russian aerial bombardment damaged sunflower oil tanks, contaminating shoreline, killing wildlife, triggering conservation warnings. Pivdenny port temporarily closed for cleanup.
  • Forest Fires: NASA FIRMS detected multiple fires at energy infrastructure facilities (oil depots, refineries, power plants) across Ukraine and Russia from continued strikes.

6. International Response

European Union

Major Development (December 12, 2025): The EU agreed to indefinitely freeze 210 billion euros (~$247 billion) in Russian assets to provide Ukraine with a reparations loan of up to 165 billion euros (~$194 billion). This:

  • Changes voting requirements from unanimous to qualified majority (15 of 27 member states)
  • Provides long-term financial support mechanism
  • Requires Ukraine to repay loan only if Russia pays reparations

United States

Trump Administration's Role:

  • Leading diplomatic negotiations through Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner
  • Proposed 15-year security guarantee deal (renewable); Ukraine seeking longer-term agreement
  • Key Statement: President Trump stated of Zelenskyy's proposal: "He doesn't have anything until I approve it"

Canada

Prime Minister Mark Carney announced an additional $2.5 billion USD ($1.82 billion CAD) in economic aid during meeting with Zelenskyy in Halifax. Emphasized that peace depends on a "willing Russia."

China

Emerging Multi-Polar World: Analysts note that 2026 will see changes in the "collective West" as the US steps back from "global policeman" role, with China boosting global clout in Asia but still unable to fully challenge Washington's domination.

North Korea

Leader Kim Jong Un declared that his nation and Russia shared "blood, life and death" as he sent New Year's greetings to President Putin. Pyongyang has deployed thousands of troops to Russia to support war efforts.

Belarus

Escalating Involvement: Belarus is increasingly being used as a launchpad for Russian attacks:

  • Zelenskyy accused Russia of using Belarusian territory to intensify drone strikes on western Ukrainian railway infrastructure
  • Belarusian President Lukashenko agreed to deploy up to 10 Russian Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missiles
  • NATO intercepted Russian reconnaissance aircraft near Baltic airspace; dozens of aerial objects believed to be balloons entered Polish airspace from Belarus
  • Moscow stationing new nuclear-capable hypersonic ballistic missiles at former airbase in eastern Belarus

Other Developments

  • Poland: Scrambled fighter jets to intercept Russian reconnaissance aircraft; warned of possible provocations

Possible Scenarios

Scenario 1: "Finnish Model" (Worst Case for Ukraine)

Similar to 1939 Finnish-Soviet War: Ukraine forced to recognize Moscow-occupied regions as part of Russia, losing territory but maintaining nominal independence.

Scenario 2: "Georgian Model"

Similar to 2008 Russia-Georgia War: No control over occupied areas, but Kyiv refuses to recognize them as Russia's. Frozen conflict with ongoing tensions.

Scenario 3: "Interim Scenario"

War is frozen along current lines; talks continue intermittently without final resolution. Could involve temporary ceasefires that break down periodically.

Scenario 4: Negotiated Settlement (Best Case)

Successful implementation of 20-point peace plan with modifications acceptable to both sides. Requires Russia to compromise on maximalist demands and Ukraine to accept territorial concessions with strong security guarantees.

Key Factors Shaping 2026

  • Trump Administration Policy: Will US pressure on Ukraine to make territorial concessions increase or decrease?
  • European Unity: Can EU maintain consensus on sanctions and support as war fatigue sets in?
  • Russian Economy: Despite sanctions, Russia's economy shows signs of strain but resilience; how long can it sustain war economy?
  • Ukrainian Mobilization: Can Ukraine overcome manpower shortages and "mobilization fatigue"?
  • Western Military Aid: Will continued support provide Ukraine with decisive advantage?
  • North Korean Support: Impact of thousands of additional North Korean troops on battlefield dynamics?
Warning: ISW assesses that at Russia's current tempo, it will likely take at least 2-3 more years for Russian forces to seize the remainder of Ukrainian-controlled Donetsk Oblast alone—assuming current rates of advance continue without significant changes to either side's capabilities.

Immediate Challenges

  • Winter Campaign: Russia attempting to split Ukraine's energy grid east-west, with eastern regions "at the brink" of blackout
  • Fighting Season: Poor weather (fog, snow) limiting some operations but not stopping assaults; both sides adapting
  • Domestic Politics: Ukrainian public growing weary; Russian public expecting war to end in 2026 (70% in one poll)
  • Information Warfare: Widespread false reporting to Russian military leadership creating distorted picture of battlefield reality

Bottom Line: The most likely trajectory through 2026 is continued grinding warfare with periodic diplomatic initiatives, unless there's a significant shift in one of the key factors above. The gap between Russian demands and Ukrainian red lines remains substantial, making a comprehensive peace settlement unlikely in the near term without major concessions from one or both sides.
