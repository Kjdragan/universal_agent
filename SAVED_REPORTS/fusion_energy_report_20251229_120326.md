**SPARC Magnet Validation**

- SPARC, CFS's marquee fusion project, will use powerful magnetic fields to confine plasma and demonstrate net energy gain
- The company will showcase SPARC at CES in early January 2026, signaling fusion's emergence as a mainstream technology

**Funding Success**
- Closed an $863 million Series B2 funding round
- Total capital raised now exceeds $2 billion
- Remains the world's largest private fusion company (MIT spinoff)

**Scientific Significance:** High-temperature superconducting magnets are enabling compact, cost-effective fusion reactors that could be commercially viable much faster than traditional approaches.

### 2. ITER Project Progress

**First Plasma Timeline**
- ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) is scheduled for first plasma in December 2025
- This represents the culmination of 35 nations collaborating on the world's largest fusion experiment
- Located in France, ITER aims to prove fusion can produce net energy on a power-plant scale

**Global Collaboration:** The project demonstrates unprecedented international scientific cooperation, with the EU, US, China, India, Japan, Korea, and Russia all contributing resources and expertise.

## Challenges and Remaining Hurdles

### Technical Challenges

**Plasma Confinement**
- Maintaining stable plasma at temperatures exceeding 100 million degrees Celsius
- Preventing plasma disruptions that can damage reactor components
- Achieving sustained burn for power generation

**Materials Science**
- Developing materials that can withstand extreme neutron radiation
- Creating breeding blankets for tritium fuel production
- Managing heat flux on reactor walls

**Energy Balance**
- Achieving Q > 1 (more energy out than in)
- Scaling from experimental to commercial levels
- Efficient energy conversion systems

### Economic Challenges

**Capital Requirements**
- Fusion plants require billions in upfront investment
- Long development timelines (10-20 years)
- Competition from falling renewable energy costs

**Regulatory Framework**
- No existing regulatory structure for fusion power plants
- Nuclear licensing processes may not fit fusion technology
- International safety standards still being developed

**Market Timing**
- Need to deploy before 2050 to meet climate goals
- Competition from battery storage and advanced nuclear fission
- Grid integration challenges
